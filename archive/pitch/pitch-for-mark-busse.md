**Talk Title (working):** "Punk Rock AI: Xerox Machines, Stolen Work, and the Most Creative Year of My Life"

Zines were Xerox machines turned against the culture that made them. Punk took the cheapest, most accessible reproduction technology on the planet and used it to critique the very systems that manufactured it.

AI is our generation's Xerox machine. Built by the biggest corporations on earth. Trained on the stolen work of every artist who ever posted anything online — including about 1,800 of my own photos, scraped from Flickr without my permission or consent. Nobody asked. They just took it.

And here's the part I can't look away from: I'm also more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been in my entire life.

Both of those statements are true. At the same time. Not contradiction. Complexity.

That's what I want to talk about at Creative Mornings. Not the clean narrative. The messy one. The one where you hold the critique in one hand and the capability in the other and you walk forward anyway — because if every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person opts out, who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used? Who builds the guardrails?

The people who don't see the problems. That's who.

I've spent two years training hundreds of creatives to work with AI — photographers, filmmakers, designers, Indigenous researchers, a musician who sat on cassette tapes for 27 years before AI gave him the last 15% he needed to finally release his album. The pattern is always the same: the ones who engage critically make the best work. The ones who pretend they can sit it out lose their seat at the table.

Here's what I want to give the Creative Mornings crowd:

**A punk rock framework for engaging with AI without selling your soul.**

1. **Name what you're seeing.** Stop saying "bias." Say misogyny. Say racism. Say theft. AI doesn't know what's fair — it knows what's frequent. The mirror reflects but doesn't correct. That's your job.

2. **Externalize your taste.** Your weird specific eye, your refusal to use certain words, your instinct for when something's off — that's not magic. It's pattern recognition you haven't articulated yet. I'll walk the room through a dead-simple exercise: three documents you can write in ten minutes that completely change your relationship with AI. Your personal policy. Your style guide. Your worldview. Do that, and the machine stops giving you average and starts amplifying what makes you *you*.

3. **Ship something by May 29th.** Release Day is the perfect punk rock deadline. Not perfect. Not polished. Punk never was. Just real, and yours, and out the door. I'll challenge every person in that room to use the month of May to create something with AI — not because AI is the point, but because building is how you discover what you actually think.

The theme is CREATE. I want to make the room uncomfortable, inspired, and dangerous. In that order.

## Notes for Kris

### Key Stories to Include

- **Matt Lock / Matlock** — 27 years of cassette tapes, AI gave him the last 15% to finally release R3ALMS (November 2025). "AI more for the enhancement of creativity versus the replacement of creativity."
- **Luke Minaker** — 30 years bringing monsters to life, 80% drudgery / 20% creative. AI flipped the ratio. "I don't care about the money. I am so excited that I get to do art again." That's not replacement. That's liberation.
- **Belgium (actress)** — Bedridden 18 months. "AI film is my hope to be able to continue to exist and to tell something." When debates get abstract, remember Belgium. Remember what access actually means.
- **Kevin Friel** — 25 years Hollywood VFX. The conductor metaphor. "Get it 85% there with AI, then use craft to finish." The tools don't replace the conductor — they give the conductor a bigger orchestra.
- **Vicki (visual artist)** — "I'm a visual person, not a writer." Uploaded 20 photos, asked AI what patterns it sees. Realized her intuition wasn't magic — it was pattern recognition she'd developed over years. Unexamined expertise can't be taught. Articulated taste can scale.
- **The 1,800 photos** — Personal testimony. Flickr, Creative Commons, scraped without consent. "I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual."

### Frameworks to Deploy

1. **Punk Rock AI** — Xerox machines / zines parallel. Using the master's tools to tell stories of resistance. Not waiting for permission. Not asking gatekeepers for access.
2. **Both Hands Full** — Critique in one hand, capability in the other. Walking forward. "I'd rather be uncomfortable and effective than comfortable and irrelevant."
3. **The Selector's Era** — DJs were originally called "selectors." Generation is cheap. Taste is not. Your job isn't to compete with AI on generation — it's to remain indispensable on judgment.
4. **Three Documents Hack** — Personal AI policy (3 min), writing style guide (4 min), worldview document (3 min). "All of a sudden, the thing isn't massaging you on the surface. It becomes a feminist, an anarchist, whatever it needs to become — specifically in your way and style."
5. **Fair vs. Frequent** — AI knows what's frequent, not what's fair. The mirror reflects but doesn't correct. Three intervention questions: Who is missing? What assumptions did you use? What would change this response?
6. **The Iceberg** — Tasks (automate freely) → Decisions (AI assists, humans decide) → Judgment (humans only, non-negotiable). "The higher I go, the slower I get."

### Release Day Activation Idea

Challenge the room: use the month of May to externalize your creative taste using AI, then release something built with that taste by May 29th. Could be a zine (full circle), a short film, a photo series, a design system, a song, a website — doesn't matter. The act of articulating your creative DNA and then shipping something with it IS the creative act of the AI era.

Could we do a punk-style zine or broadsheet collecting everyone's Release Day projects? Digital composting meets Xerox culture.
