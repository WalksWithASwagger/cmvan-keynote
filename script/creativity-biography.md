# Kris Krüg: Creativity Biography
## Reference doc for the CMVan talk — scenes, lines, and moments to draw from

**Purpose:** This is not a script. It's a curated archive of real scenes, specific details, and usable lines from Kris's 30-year creative practice. Pull from here when writing the prologue, truth bombs, or any biographical beats. Sources are the book draft (`source-material/life-love-internet/`), the Bossier interview, and the Notion KB.

**The through-line:** Every chapter is the same move — pick up a tool built by someone else, use it wrong, share it freely, build community around it. The tech changes. The move doesn't.

---

## SCENE 1: The first computer (Harrisburg, South Dakota, ~1982)

Hand-me-down Commodore 64. Stayed up late in the blue glow of the screen typing BASIC commands like incantations. `CREATE. RUN. DEBUG.` "The poetry of potential."

Also played trumpet for ten years. "Jazz, classical, didn't matter. It was all algorithms to me — patterns to be understood, then broken."

**Usable line:** "I was programming before I knew what programming was. Ten years of trumpet — same thing. Find the pattern. Break it. Start again."

## SCENE 2: The website (Trinity Western University, September 1995)

First week at university. Wanders into an open dorm room and finds Jun and Mikey, Korean exchange students, hunched over a computer. "Ever built a website?" Over the next weeks, fueled by instant noodles and insatiable curiosity, dives headfirst into web design. Also meets Sarah — philosophy major, part-time hacker — in the computer lab at 2 AM. She introduces him to Marshall McLuhan: the medium shapes the message.

**The decision moment:** Standing in the empty computer lab, snowboard in the corner. "The snow will always be there. But this — this digital revolution — is once in a lifetime." Calls his parents: "I think I've found my calling."

**Usable line:** "I didn't study web design. I learned it at 2am from Korean exchange students in a dorm room. That's how it's always gone — somebody shows you a thing, you stay up all night until you understand it, and then you teach everybody else."

## SCENE 3.5: "Worldly" — the permission gap (~1990s–2001)

Growing up, Kris didn't know that a person could be a creative professional. Not in the abstract — literally didn't know any artists, photographers, filmmakers, designers, fashion people. People who worked in those worlds were called **"worldly"** — a loaded word from a religious conservative background that meant: not for you, not your people, not a real path.

So he dabbled. Music. Drawing. Writing. Things he could never quite get right. Not because he lacked something — because he had no map. No models. No one who showed him that making things was a life you could actually build.

At 20, he got to know a photographer for the first time. That was the insight: *"Yo, there are people out there that work in these industries. These are real jobs. These are real lives."* A permission structure cracked open.

But it still didn't click — not the camera, not yet. More years of trying things. More dabbling. The medium he'd eventually live in for thirty years was still out there somewhere.

It found him at 31, in a hospital, in a crisis. (→ Scene 4)

**Why this matters for the talk:** The gatekeeping wasn't institutional — it was cultural, religious, built into how the world was named to him as a kid. "Worldly" is one word for it. The talk is about who gets permission to be a creative, and Kris didn't grow up with that permission. He found it sideways, late, in the last place anyone would look for it.

**Use with care:** Kris's note — "we don't make too big a deal of it." A line or two in the open, not a set piece. It deepens the camera origin story without becoming its own story.

**Usable line:** "I didn't grow up knowing creative professionals existed. Where I came from, those people were called worldly — which was not a compliment. I found photography at 31, in a hospital, and it was the first creative thing that ever actually worked for me. I didn't know until then that I'd been looking for it."

## SCENE 4: The camera (Silicon Valley / Stanford NICU, 2001) ← THE HUMAN ORIGIN STORY

This is the scene that's missing from the current script. 

2001. iVMG, an Accel Partners-backed startup in Silicon Valley. 9/11 hits. Then: Judah is born with serious health complications. Rushed to the NICU at Stanford. Kris is there every day, watching his newborn son hooked up to more wires than a server rack.

*"I pick up a camera. Not for some artsy bullshit reason, but because the company needs photos and we're too broke to hire a pro."*

He starts taking it everywhere — to the hospital, to the streets of San Francisco, trying to make sense of a world that feels fragile. Posting raw, unfiltered photos on Flickr. One of Judah's NICU photos ends up on Flickr's homepage.

*"Each click of the shutter is like a middle finger to the universe — yeah, life's a bitch, but I can still create something beautiful."*

*"It's more than that. It's therapy. It's a way to process this shitstorm."*

**The corporate world starts to lose its luster.** Kris starts thinking about technology differently — not as an end in itself, but as a tool. A means to connect, to share, to make a difference.

**Usable line:** "I didn't pick up a camera because I wanted to be a photographer. I picked it up in a hospital because my son was fighting for his life and I needed something to hold onto. And people started to respond. Not to the technique — to the truth in the image."

**Usable line:** "That's when I understood what technology is actually for. Not for IPOs. For moments like that one."

## SCENE 5: Bryght / Dead.net / Open Source (Vancouver, 2004)

Back in Vancouver. Co-founds Bryght with Boris Mann — the world's first Drupal services company. East Van office, second-hand desks, whiteboards covered in code. "We weren't just building websites. We were building a fucking movement. Open-source wasn't just about free software; it was about freedom, period."

Landing Dead.net for the Grateful Dead. Warner Brothers Records. Building digital homes for musicians. "If that's not a long, strange trip, I don't know what is."

Client conversation: "So, let me get this straight — you want me to pay you for software that's free?" Kris: "You know how to drive a car, right? But if your engine explodes, you're not gonna rebuild it yourself. You're gonna pay a mechanic. We're the mechanics of the digital world. The software's free, but our expertise? That's fucking priceless."

**The values:** "Open-source wasn't just about code. It was about refusing to build walls. The more you give away, the more valuable you become."

Eventually acquired by Raincity Studios. The anarchists learning to march in formation. Grew the impact without losing the ethos.

## SCENE 6: Flickr and 100,000 Creative Commons photos

Released 100,000 photos under Creative Commons. People thought he was crazy. *"Kris, you're devaluing your work!"*

"In the digital world, scarcity is a myth. The more you give away, the more valuable you become. Those Creative Commons photos spread across the internet like a benevolent virus, each one carrying my name with it."

Result: calls from National Geographic, Rolling Stone, New York Times. They didn't want the free stuff — they wanted the premium Kris Krüg experience. By giving away, he made himself invaluable.

**Then the flip:** When he later checked the AI training data, he found roughly 1,800 of those same Flickr images inside a major corpus. He'd shared them freely — that was the deal. Nobody asked him. Nobody paid him. They just took it.

**Usable line:** "I published 100,000 photos under Creative Commons because I believed sharing builds more than hoarding. That's a creative value I still hold. And it's also how I learned that my photos were scraped into an AI model without anyone asking me. Both things are true. I haven't resolved that contradiction. I'm living in it."

## SCENE 7: The feedback loop — Flickr, mastery, and what AI actually does

**The core idea:** Every era of technology has compressed the feedback loop between making a thing and learning whether it's good. The compression is what actually accelerates mastery. AI is the latest — and most dramatic — step in that compression. This is a through-line from Kris's Flickr practice to his current AI work.

**The three eras:**

*Traditional era (pre-social media):* Shoot a roll of film. Develop it — days of waiting. If you're good enough, a magazine publishes it. A reader writes a letter to the editor. The letter goes in the mail. Weeks later, if you're lucky, it's published. You read it. Loop time: months to a year. Traditional mastery path: ~15 years.

*Flickr era (2004–2012):* Post every day. Get feedback every day. Comments, favorites, real signals. Incorporate, go out the next day, run the whole loop again. Loop time: 24 hours. Estimated mastery compression: ~15 years → ~5 years. The key: not the audience, the loop speed.

*AI era (now):* Make hundreds of variations, critique them, filter them, run taste loops in an afternoon that used to take a season. But the loop is only as good as the judgment you bring to it. Speed without taste is noise at scale.

**The critical point:** The machine doesn't teach you what good looks like. Twenty years of choosing does. AI amplifies existing taste — it doesn't create taste from nothing. Someone who has run the Flickr loop for a decade gets more from AI than someone who hasn't. The investment in the iterative practice compounds.

**Swarm looping (Matt Lambert / cardeo):** What AI enables now is running many variations in parallel — a "swarm" of iterations — rather than one at a time. This is a further compression of the feedback loop. But Lambert's key insight (from his SWARM protocol) is that the human must remain active at every *transition* between phases, not just at the end. "The AI assists within each phase; it must not determine progression between phases." The loop is only as valuable as the judgment applied at each handoff. Intuition develops through repeated exposure to patterns — the swarm accelerates the pattern exposure, but the human decides what carries forward.

**Connection to Three Documents:** Externalizing your taste (policy, style guide, worldview) is how you make the AI loop actually yours — otherwise you're running someone else's taste through the machine.

**Usable lines:**

"Think about what the feedback loop used to look like. Shoot a roll of film. Develop it. Magazine publishes it. Reader writes a letter. Letter goes in the mail. That loop took a year. That's why mastery took fifteen."

"Then Flickr happened. Post every single day. Get feedback every day. Run the loop again tomorrow. I think that compressed fifteen years to five — for people who were genuinely using it."

"What AI does now is compress that further. Dramatically further. The question is never whether the tools are fast. The question is whether you've developed the judgment to run the loop well. Whether you know which ninety-seven to throw away."

"Speed without judgment is just noise at scale. The machine didn't teach me what good looks like. Twenty years of choosing did."

"Taste isn't what you keep. It's what you throw away. The cutting room floor is where taste actually lives."

## SCENE 8: True North Media House / Vancouver Olympics (2010)

Set up True North Media House as a guerrilla newsroom at the Vancouver Olympics. No official press passes — they made their own. Turned every blogger and smartphone into a potential reporter. "While the mainstream media was bogged down with bureaucracy, we were nimble."

Ran operation out of a converted warehouse. Bloggers rubbing shoulders with veteran photojournalists, coders collaborating with activists. "The air's thick with the smell of coffee and revolution."

Also covered the BP oil spill, Copenhagen Climate Summit, Bolivia with Evo Morales.

**The disintermediation principle:** "We weren't just reporting on the Olympics; we were creating a parallel narrative, one that gave voice to the stories on the margins."

## SCENE 9: Teaching at Emily Carr / VFS

"Your camera is the least important tool in your arsenal." A classroom with no rows of desks — screens flickering with livestreams from protests, 3D printers humming, students coding new apps for crowdsourced journalism.

"I wasn't there to create photographers. I was there to mold visual revolutionaries."

Debates with veteran photographers like Bev Davies and Alex Waterhouse-Hayward — "New School vs. Old School" throwdowns. "It's not about old or new. It's about impact. It's about using whatever tools you have to tell stories that matter."

## SCENE 10: Galiano Island / off-grid / IMERSS

Philimore Point — off the grid, solar power, collecting rainwater, growing food. The analog oasis as counterweight to the digital dojo. Living in partnership with the Lelum Saratanaough Coast Salish people and the Galiano Conservancy. Co-founded IMERSS (Institute of Multidisciplinary Ecological Research in the Salish Sea).

"True sustainability isn't just about technology — it's about listening, about learning from those who have lived in harmony with the land for generations."

**Why this matters for the talk:** The punk who tears down walls also knows when to be quiet and listen. This is the empathetic, mature side Mark is talking about.

## SCENE 11: Kris's brain dump framework (from the Mark Busse thread)

Kris's own words, April 2026 — this is his distilled creative philosophy:

- **Daily looping iteration** — the practice
- **Flickr as shortcut to mastery** — iteration compounds
- **AI swarms turn that to 11** — amplifier, not replacement
- **Disintermediation through Technology** — every chapter is the same move
- **Finding voice, getting to know self, connecting with audience, building a life** — this is the through-line
- **Kevin Kelly's thousand true fans** — diversified revenue, different prices for different people
- **Open everything** — software, building in public, open mind, open culture
- **Any dead fish can float downstream** — it takes a live fish to swim against the current

## THE MANTRA (for the close)

"Any dead fish can float downstream. But it takes a live fish to swim against the current."

Origin: Dr. Ronald Shaw, youth pastor, Sacramento, ~1992. Said it to Kris's church youth group as a motivational sermon. Kris has carried it as a life mantra since age 15 — originally about not following the crowd, later about choosing to engage with AI critically rather than floating along with the hype.

Kris used it in the same chat thread where he was talking to Mark. It belongs at the close of the May 1 talk.
