# Talk Framework v4: Punk Rock AI
## Discussion-First Format — Creative Mornings Vancouver, May 1, 2026

**Format:** Kris prompts → crowd discusses (~3–5 min) → Kris responds with truth bombs  
**Timing:** 20–25 min total  
**Primary script:** This document  
**Full prose fallback:** `script/full-talk-script.md` (v3 monologue — all truth bombs written out in full)  
**Biography reference:** `script/creativity-biography.md`  
**Mark's feedback:** `docs/MARK-FEEDBACK.md`

---

## GUIDING PRINCIPLE

This is not a presentation. It's a conversation Kris is prepared to lead. The truth bombs are not predetermined talking points — they're the things Kris knows are true, ready to deploy based on what actually comes up in the room. The crowd discussion isn't a gimmick; it's the whole point. Mark said it: "Let's let the crowd have their time to chat first, then you engage them and blow some minds."

Read the room. Trust the material. The framework holds whether the room goes dark or light, scared or excited.

---

## OPEN (2 min)

**[No slides yet. Center stage. Morning light. Coffee cups. No podium.]**

Walk out. Take a breath. Don't rush.

> I'm going to tell you something true, and then I want to hear what you think.

**[Beat. Let the room settle.]**

> I've been building things on the internet since 1995. Websites, companies, communities, photos — thirty years of making stuff with whatever tool was in front of me. Open-source software when nobody was doing that. A hundred thousand photos on Flickr under Creative Commons because I believed sharing builds more than hoarding. A guerrilla newsroom at the Olympics with no press pass. A research institute on a tiny island. Hundreds of creative professionals trained to work with AI.

> The tool changes. The move is always the same: pick it up, use it wrong, share what you learn, build community around it.

**[Pause.]**

> Here's where I am right now. I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual. I released those hundred thousand Flickr photos freely — that was the deal I made. When I checked a training data search tool, I found roughly 1,800 of my images scraped into a major AI corpus. Nobody asked me. Nobody paid me. They just took it.

> And I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been in my entire life.

> Both of those statements are true. At the exact same time.

**[Pause. Slower.]**

> That's what I want to talk about this morning. But before I say another word —

**[Look around the room. Make contact.]**

> What's actually on your mind? About AI, about your work, about where this is all going. No wrong answers. Tell me what you're sitting with.

## CROWD DISCUSSION (3–5 min)

**[Step back. Genuinely listen. Don't perform listening — actually listen.]**

Common themes that will likely come up:
- Fear of replacement / job loss
- "I don't know how to use these tools"
- "The work feels fake now" / loss of craft meaning
- Excitement mixed with guilt
- Stolen work, consent, attribution
- "Who benefits from this?"
- "Is this just hype?"

**[Take mental notes. Pick 2–3 themes to respond to. Don't try to cover everything.]**

---

## TRUTH BOMBS (15 min)

These are modular. Deploy based on what the room raised. The clusters below are organized by theme — pick the ones that connect to the actual conversation. The order isn't fixed. Trust your instincts.

---

### CLUSTER A: Theft, Consent, Naming What You See

*Deploy if the room raises: stolen work, consent, fairness, "who benefits," AI trained on our stuff*

**Truth bomb 1: Non-consensual**
> I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual. I said that already. I want to sit with it. I released those photos because community mattered more to me than locking every file in a vault. That's a creative value I still hold. And it's also how I got scraped. The thing that made me who I am — the openness — is the thing that was exploited. I haven't resolved that. I'm living in it.

**Truth bomb 2: Stop saying bias**
> We've said the word "bias" so many times it's lost all meaning. Stop saying bias. Name what you're seeing. When you type "CEO" into an image generator and get twenty white men in power poses — that's not bias. That's racism and misogyny encoded into mathematics. When it mimics an artist's exact style — name the theft. AI doesn't know what's fair. It only knows what's *frequent* on the internet. The mirror reflects. It doesn't correct. Correcting the mirror is your job.

**Three questions to carry into every AI interaction:**
> Who is missing from this output? What assumptions did the model make? What would change if the training data weren't dominated by English-language internet?

---

### CLUSTER B: Liberation — What's Also True

*Deploy if the room raises: replacement fears, loss of meaning, or if the room is too doom-heavy*

**Truth bomb 3: Luke Minaker**
> Luke is an animator. Twelve years old, he sat in a theater watching Jurassic Park and said: I want to bring monsters to life. Thirty years later he's not bringing monsters to life. He's grinding out corporate animation. 80% drudgery, 20% creative. That's what the industry gave him. AI tools arrive. The ratio flips. 80% creative, 20% grind. Luke told me: "I don't care about the money. I am so excited that I get to do art again." The twelve-year-old who wanted to bring monsters to life? He's back. After thirty years. That's not replacement. That's liberation.

**Truth bomb 4: Belgium**
> An actress who goes by Belgium was bedridden for eighteen months. Couldn't work. Couldn't perform. Couldn't do the thing that made her who she is. She started making AI avatars. Creating characters. Building stories from her bed. She said: "AI film is my hope to be able to continue to exist and to tell something." When the debates get abstract and ideological — remember Belgium. Remember what access actually means.

**Truth bomb 5: Matlock**
> My friend Matt Lock — Matlock — is a musician. He makes incredible beats. For twenty-seven years, Matt had a box full of cassette tapes sitting in his house. Decades of unreleased music. Why didn't he release it? Because making the music is only 85% of the job. Finishing it — the mastering, the album art, the distribution — that last 15% is brutal friction. Last November, Matt finally released *R3ALMS*. The tools helped him with the last fifteen percent. Not the soul of the thing. That's the kind of creative problem I actually care about: how we finish. How we stop apologizing for being alive in public.

**The both/and:**
> My personal testimony: AI is trained on stolen work without consent. And I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I have ever been in my entire life. Both statements are true. At the exact same time. That is not a contradiction. That is complexity. That is the punk condition.

---

### CLUSTER C: Practice — Taste, the Selector, Three Documents

*Deploy if the room raises: "how do I use these tools," craft, the skill gap, what to actually do*

**Truth bomb 6: The Selector**
> In the dancehalls of Kingston, Jamaica, the hero of the dance wasn't the musician. It was the Selector. The person who chose the records. Because making the sound wasn't the hard part anymore. Knowing *which* sound the room needed — that was the skill. That was taste. That idea took a boat to the Bronx and became Hip Hop. Generation is cheap. Taste is not. That's always been true. AI just turned the volume up on it.

**Truth bomb 7: What did you throw away?**
> Here's a question nobody asks: what did you throw away this week? What's on your cutting room floor? Because that's your taste. Not what you chose to keep — what you refused to keep. I spent ten years posting photos to Flickr every single day. Not because every photo was good. Because I was training my eye to know the difference. The practice of throwing away is the practice of taste. AI can generate ten thousand options. Your job is to know which one is yours.

**Truth bomb 8: Three Documents**
> This is the practical punk move. I challenge every person in this room to write three documents before May 29th.

> One: A Personal AI Policy. What will you use AI for? What will you refuse? Three minutes. Write it down.

> Two: A Style Guide. What are the ten words you absolutely refuse to say? What does your work sound like when it's really you? Ban the word "delve." *(laughter)* Four minutes.

> Three: A Worldview Document. Are you a feminist? An anarchist? A punk? A Buddhist? What do you believe about people, about craft, about justice? Five sentences. Three minutes.

> Feed those three documents to whatever AI you use. Do that, and the machine stops giving you the average of the internet. It starts amplifying what makes you *you*. As someone way smarter than me once said: "If your values aren't in text, to AI they basically don't exist. And that is dangerously close to won't exist."

> Writing for the bot isn't narcissism. For a designer in Vancouver, for an Indigenous artist, for anyone whose perspective isn't already over-represented in training data — it's cultural activism.

---

### CLUSTER D: The Punk Condition — Why You Can't Opt Out

*Deploy if the room raises: "I don't want to use it," ethics of participation, feeling complicit*

**Truth bomb 9: Who writes the rules?**
> If every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person in Vancouver opts out of using AI because it is messy — who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used? Who builds the guardrails? The tech bros. The people who don't see the problems. That's who.

> Vancouver is the home of Adbusters and Greenpeace. We've been turning corporate infrastructure into counterculture since before most tech cities had a scene. That's the tradition you're in. Not opting out — using it wrong. Using it to tell stories that matter.

**Truth bomb 10: AI is not a shortcut**
> Everyone thinks AI is a shortcut. Ha. Bullshit. The people doing the most interesting work with these tools are working harder than they ever have — just on different things. The drudgery goes away. The taste work, the judgment work, the "is this actually true to who I am" work — that gets more intense, not less. You work hard. The tools don't replace that. They redirect it.

## CLOSE (2 min)

**[Quieter. Slower. Move to center.]**

**[SLIDE 9 — RELEASE DAY. Optional: hold slides until now entirely if the crowd discussion ran hot.]**

> So here's where I want to land.

> The theme this month is CREATE. The global activation — Release Day — is May twenty-ninth. Four weeks from today.

> I don't just want you to think about this. I want you to make something.

> Write those three documents. Feed them to the machine. Build something that only you could build. A zine. A short film. A photo series. A manifesto. A webapp. The album you've been sitting on for twenty-seven years. I don't care what it is. Just release it by May 29th.

**[Pause.]**

> It doesn't need to be perfect.

> Punk never was.

**[Pause. Slower.]**

> There's a line I've carried since I was fifteen. My youth pastor used to say it in Sacramento. It stayed with me through Silicon Valley, through a NICU in Stanford, through Flickr, through Bryght, through building AI community in this city:

**[SLIDE 10 — Kevin Friel quote.]**

> *Any dead fish can float downstream. But it takes a live fish to swim against the current.*

**[Hold. Three seconds.]**

> My friend Kevin Friel puts it differently: "Let's use these tools to f*** it up, y'all."

**[Direct eye contact across the room.]**

> You coming?

**[Silence. Then:]**

> Thank you Vancouver.

## SPEAKER NOTES

### Physical
- Open: CENTER. No slides. Stand still.
- Crowd discussion: STEP BACK — literally give them space
- Truth bombs: move WITH the energy — stage left for the hard stuff, stage right for the liberation stories
- Close: CENTER, quieter, slower than anything that came before

### Slides (revised for discussion-first format)
- **Hold all slides until after the crowd discussion**, or at minimum until you begin the truth bombs
- The open and the crowd prompt happen with NO SLIDES — just you and the room
- Advance slides only when you're in a truth bomb that has a corresponding visual
- Slide 9 (Release Day) and Slide 10 (Kevin Friel) are the close

### What to do if the room goes quiet after the prompt
Don't panic. Wait a genuine five seconds — longer than comfortable. Then:
> "Okay — hands up: how many of you have used an AI tool in the last week?" *(hands)*
> "Keep your hand up if you felt weird about it." *(usually most hands stay)*
> "That's what I want to talk about."

### What to do if the room goes dark (all doom, no excitement)
Lead with Belgium. Lead with Luke. Get a liberation story in early. The framework holds — just start with the right hand instead of the left.

### What to do if the room goes light (all hype, no critique)
Start with the consent story. The 1,800 photos. "I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual." That lands in any room.
