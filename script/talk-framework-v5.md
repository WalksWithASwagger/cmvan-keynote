# Talk Framework v5: Sequential Narrative
## Creative Mornings Vancouver — May 1, 2026
## 20 minutes · Five acts · Slides cued throughout

**Format:** Straight monologue. Biography as argument. Story first, ideas second.  
**Timing:** 20 min hard.  
**Primary prose:** `dress-rehearsal/elevenlabs-full-script.md` — read that for the full version of every section. This document is the stage map.

---

## GUIDING PRINCIPLE

The talk has one through-line: every chapter of a 30-year creative life is the same move — pick up the tool, use it wrong, share everything, build community. AI is the latest chapter. The argument is made by telling the story, not by explaining the argument.

Start human. Stay human. The ideas land because the story lands first.

---

## ACT I — WHO I AM (~4 min · Slides 1–5)

*The biographical foundation. By the end of this act the audience knows who Kris is and what he believes about sharing and openness — which makes the betrayal in Act III land harder.*

**[SLIDE 1 — Title]**

Walk out. No rush. Let the room settle.

> I grew up not knowing that a person could be a creative professional.

**[SLIDE 2 — The Permission Gap]**

> I mean that literally. Where I came from — religious conservative, small-town South Dakota — the people who made art, who took photographs, who worked in film — those people were called *worldly*. That was not a compliment. I tried things. Ten years of trumpet. Drawing. Writing. I wasn't bad at any of it. I just had no map.

**[SLIDE 3 — The Camera Origin]**

> I found photography at thirty-one, in a hospital. My son Judah was born with serious health complications — rushed to the NICU at Stanford. I picked up a camera not for any artsy reason. The company needed photos and we were too broke to hire a pro.
>
> But something else happened. The camera got me out of the house. It gave me somewhere to put my attention when my attention was otherwise drowning. I could hold my grief up to the light instead of being swallowed by it. The camera was a survival device before it was a creative tool. It interrupted the despair.
>
> I posted the photos to Flickr. One of Judah's NICU photos ended up on the homepage.
>
> It's not about the technique. People are responding to the truth in the image. That's when I understood what any of this is actually for.

**[SLIDE 4 — 145,000 Frames]**

> I released a hundred and forty-five thousand photographs under Creative Commons. People told me I was devaluing my work. I told them they were thinking about it wrong: piracy isn't your biggest fear. Obscurity is. Those photos spread like a benevolent virus — each one carrying my name. The calls came from National Geographic, Rolling Stone, the New York Times. They didn't want the free stuff. They wanted the premium experience. *Giving away made me invaluable.*
>
> For twenty years that logic was sound. AI broke it. I'll come back to that.

**[SLIDE 5 — Open Source as Values]**

> Same principle when I moved back to Vancouver and co-founded Bryght with Boris Mann — the world's first Drupal services company. We built Dead.net for the Grateful Dead. A client asked: "You want me to pay for software that's free?" I said: you know how to drive a car. But if the engine explodes, you're not rebuilding it yourself. The software was free. The expertise wasn't. Open-source wasn't a business model. It was a refusal to build walls.
>
> Open source taught me: value doesn't live in the locked file. Value lives in the relationship, the expertise, the community, the trust. I couldn't see it yet, but that's the principle AI would later turn against me.

## ACT II — THE PATTERN (~5 min · Slides 6–11)

*The lineage. This is where the talk earns the right to say "this story is not new." The biographical is now expanding into history — showing that every generation does the same thing with the infrastructure they inherit.*

**[SLIDE 6 — Dada → Punks → DJs → AI]**

> I'd been making this move before I knew it had a name. In 1998, I launched a community called Spark Online — "exploring electronic consciousness." Pre-blog, pre-social media, a small circle of people building public lives on the web. And the practice that came with it: document everything. Publish before, during, and after. Not to build an audience — to build infrastructure against forgetting.
>
> Before I get to what happened next — I need to give you a framework. Because this story is not new.
>
> The Dadaists picked up the newspaper — a corporate mass-production technology — and tore it into collage. The Xerox punks picked up the copy machine and made zines. The DJs in Jamaica picked up the sound system and invented a whole new art form. That idea took a boat to the Bronx and became Hip Hop.
>
> Every generation: the corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it's actually for.

**[SLIDE 7 — Burroughs / Situationists]**

> The lineage goes deeper than Dada. Burroughs cut up text and rearranged it on the floor. His point: *arrangement and selection is the medium.* The Situationists coined *détournement* — taking the tools of corporate spectacle and turning them against the spectacle. Not rejecting the infrastructure. Redirecting it.
>
> Every time we talk about AI as unprecedented — we're forgetting centuries of artists who did exactly this.

**[SLIDE 8 — The Selector]**

> In the dancehalls of Kingston, the hero wasn't the musician. It was the Selector — the person who chose the records. Because making the sound wasn't the hard part anymore. Knowing *which* sound the room needed at that exact moment — that was the skill. That was taste.
>
> And here's what I want you to notice: the Selector was listening first. Before the first record dropped. Taste isn't just aesthetic preference. It's social intelligence. It emerges from being present in the room before you open your mouth.
>
> Generation is completely free now. AI can write a screenplay in ten seconds. What's scarce — genuinely scarce — is taste. Your specific weird eye. Your instinct for when something is technically fine but somehow wrong.
>
> The Selector is the job. It always was.

**[SLIDE 9 — The Feedback Loop]**

> Think about what mastery used to look like. Shoot a roll of film. Develop it. Hope a magazine publishes it. A reader writes a letter. That loop took a year. That's why mastery took fifteen.
>
> Then Flickr happened. I posted every single day. Feedback every day. I ran the loop again the next morning. I think that compressed fifteen years of mastery to maybe five — for people who were genuinely using it, genuinely willing to throw work away.
>
> AI compresses it further still. You can run taste loops in an afternoon that used to take a season. People call it swarm looping. But here's what nobody tells you: *speed is not the point.* The point is what you do at each transition. The AI assists within each phase. It must not determine what comes next. That's the human's job. Every time.

**[SLIDE 10 — The Cutting Room Floor]**

> The loop is only as good as the taste you bring to it. The machine didn't teach me what good looks like. Twenty years of choosing did.
>
> Here's a question nobody asks: *what did you throw away this week?* What's on your cutting room floor? Because that's your taste. Not what you chose to keep — what you refused to keep.

**[SLIDE 11 — Vicki]**

> A visual artist I know uploaded twenty of her own photographs and asked AI to abstract the patterns in her work. What came back stopped her cold. The AI had named a structural pattern she'd been doing for fifteen years without ever articulating it.
>
> She said: it was like seeing a map of a city you've lived in for decades but never seen from above.
>
> What you think of as artistic instinct? That's twenty years of choosing, encoded. AI made it legible. That's not replacement. That's revelation.

## ACT III — THE COMPLICATION (~4 min · Slides 12–16)

*The temperature drops. This is where the story earns the right to say "both things are true" — because the critique is specific and honest, not vague or performative.*

**[SLIDE 12 — The AI Chapter]**

> And then AI arrived. And here's where it gets complicated in a way I haven't fully resolved.
>
> I need to tell you about the deal I made. For twenty years I operated on a covenant with the internet: I share freely, I get attribution, I get community, I get the relationship back. Open source taught me value lives in relationship, not locked files. So I shared. Radically. A hundred and forty-five thousand photographs under Creative Commons.
>
> That deal held for twenty years. Then AI came and broke it.

> I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

> When I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly eighteen hundred of my images scraped into a major AI training corpus. Nobody asked me. Nobody paid me. They took the file. They did not keep the relationship.
>
> The thing that made me who I am — the openness — is the thing that was exploited. I haven't resolved that. I'm living inside it.

**[SLIDE 13 — The Three Fears]**

> I want to name the fears directly. Not "AI is scary" — that's useless. The specific things.
>
> Consent and theft. Not just my photos — the entire infrastructure of human creative work, scraped without permission, without compensation, to build commercial products. I am not anti-remix. Dada was remix. Hip Hop was remix. The open internet I helped build was remix. *Remix has culture. Extraction has appetite.* What AI did was not remix — it was extraction.
>
> The junior pipeline. How does a young photographer learn if there's no market for the work that teaches you? What happens when the bottom rungs disappear?
>
> The race to the bottom. AI-generated content competing with human creative work at the low end, driving down rates, normalizing mediocrity at scale.
>
> These are real. I don't want to paper over them.

**[SLIDE 14 — The Junior Pipeline]**

> On the pipeline — I want to be honest in a way that's uncomfortable. I don't have a clean answer. The people who say they do are not being honest with you. What I do know: the answer isn't "don't use the tools." And the junior pipeline is a generation of creative people trying to figure out if there's a rung on the ladder for them. We owe them honesty, not reassurance.

**[SLIDE 15 — Name What You See]**

> The first job is not prompting. The first job is noticing. Slow down enough to see what's actually in the output — who's present, who's absent, what assumptions the model made.
>
> We've said the word bias so many times it's lost all meaning. Stop saying bias. Name what you're seeing. When you type CEO into an image generator and get twenty white men in power poses — that's not bias. That's racism and misogyny encoded into mathematics. When a model mimics an artist's exact style without credit — name the theft.
>
> AI doesn't know what's fair. It only knows what's *frequent* on the internet. The mirror reflects. It doesn't correct. Correcting the mirror is your job.

**[SLIDE 16 — Frequent ≠ Fair]**

> Three questions to carry into every AI interaction: Who is missing from this output? What assumptions did the model make? What would change if the training data weren't dominated by English-language internet?

## ACT IV — THE PUNK CONDITION (~5 min · Slides 17–23)

*The turn. Both things are true. The talk earns the right to say "use it anyway" because it has been honest about why not to. This act also grounds the argument in Kris's actual life — Galiano, Vancouver, the community.*

**[SLIDE 17 — The Punk Condition]**

> And here's what I need you to sit with at the same time: I am more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I have ever been in my entire life.

**[SLIDE 18 — What's Also True]**

> Both of those statements are true. At the exact same time.
>
> That's not a contradiction. That's complexity. The Dadaists were in the same position with the newspaper. The Xerox punks with the copy machine. The Jamaican selectors with the sound system. I hold the critique in one hand. I hold the capability in the other. Both hands full. That's the only honest place to stand.
>
> And if you're paralyzed by the weight of the critique — action is the antidote to despair. Not solutions. Not answers. Action. Make something small. Make it today.

**[SLIDE 19 — The Analog Oasis]**

> I want to give you a true picture. I live part of the year on Galiano Island, off the grid — solar power, rainwater, growing food. I co-founded a research institute in partnership with the Coast Salish people to study the Salish Sea. I needed the analog oasis as a counterweight to the digital noise.
>
> I've done ten-day digital fasts. Twenty-one-day digital fasts. No camera, no computer, no cell phone. Every time, the same thing: the stuff that doesn't matter fades away, and what comes back is the signal. I don't want my nervous system colonized by technology.
>
> The punk who tears down walls also knows when to be quiet. The Selector also knows when not to play a record.

**[SLIDE 20 — Who Writes the Rules?]**

> If every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person opts out of using AI because it is messy — who writes the rules? The tech bros. The people who don't see the problems.
>
> Vancouver is the home of Adbusters and Greenpeace. We have been turning corporate infrastructure into counterculture since before most tech cities had a scene. That's the tradition you're in. Not opting out — using it wrong.
>
> And don't do it alone. Don't build community — that word has lost its teeth. Build a posse. A small circle who share your taste, your ethics, your trust. The Selector didn't work alone. The weirdos always traveled in packs.

**[SLIDE 21 — True North]**

> At the 2010 Vancouver Olympics — no press credentials. We made our own. We didn't have permission. We had cameras, feeds, tags, links, and each other. Bloggers and veteran photojournalists in the same warehouse. While mainstream media was bogged down in bureaucracy, we were nimble. That's the move. Not waiting for permission from the people who built the system.

**[SLIDE 22 — Anthony Joseph]**

> Every Vancouver AI Community gathering starts with an indigenous welcome. Anthony Joseph — Isiléyu — from the Coast Salish territory said something I think about constantly. He said: my ancestors didn't have chainsaws. But they would've used them if they were there.
>
> That's it. The creative impulse is continuous. The tools change. The people who pick them up are in the same lineage. Adaptability is not capitulation.
>
> The tool is never neutral. But neither are we.

**[SLIDE 23 — The Best Tool]**

> I wrote a book called *Killer Photos with Your iPhone*. The whole argument: the best camera is the one you have with you. The perfect tool you don't use has produced exactly zero work.
>
> Same principle. Don't wait for the ethical perfect AI. Use what's available, on your terms, toward something that matters.

## ACT V — THE MOVE (~2 min · Slides 24–28)

*The turn from argument to action. Concrete, fast, and closing. The call to action should feel earned — the audience has been through the full story.*

**[SLIDE 24 — The Three Documents]**

> The most practical punk move I know: write three documents.
>
> One — a personal AI policy. What will you use these tools for? What will you refuse? Three minutes.
>
> Two — a style guide. What are the ten words you refuse to say? What does your work sound like when it's really you? Ban the word *delve*. Four minutes.
>
> Three — a worldview document. Are you a feminist? An anarchist? A punk? Five sentences. Three minutes.
>
> Feed those to whatever AI you use. The machine stops giving you the average of the internet. It starts amplifying what makes you *you*. If your values aren't in text, to AI they basically don't exist.

**[SLIDE 25 — Write for the Bot]**

> For anyone whose perspective isn't already over-represented in the training data — writing those documents isn't narcissism. It's not personal branding. It's anti-erasure infrastructure. If your voice doesn't exist in text, it doesn't exist in the model.
>
> And every time you document your process — the making, the choosing, the rejecting — you leave a trace that proves a human was there. In the AI age, process is provenance.

**[SLIDE 26 — The Stubborn Human Soul]**

> There is something in creative work that doesn't compress. That twenty years of Flickr practice built in me that I cannot fully articulate, cannot hand to a machine, cannot explain even to myself. It lives in the things I refuse without being able to say exactly why. It lives in the cutting room floor.
>
> The AI era doesn't make that less valuable. It makes it more valuable. Because now everything else is cheap.

**[SLIDE 27 — Release Day]**

> The global Release Day is May twenty-ninth. Four weeks.
>
> Write those three documents. Feed them to the machine. Build something that only you could build. A zine. A short film. A photo series. The album you've been sitting on for years. I don't care what it is.
>
> It doesn't need to be perfect.
>
> Punk never was.

**[SLIDE 28 — Close]**

> I've carried a line since I was fifteen. My youth pastor used to say it: any dead fish can float downstream. But it takes a live fish to swim against the current.
>
> You have more creative power available to you right now than any generation before you.
>
> Pick it up. Use it wrong. Share what you learn. Build a posse.

*(Quieter.)*

> You coming?
