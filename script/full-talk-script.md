# Full Talk Script: Punk Rock AI
**Duration:** ~25 minutes  
**Speaker:** Kris Krüg  
**Field talk:** Creative Mornings Vancouver — **May 1, 2026**  
**Release Day CTA (global):** **May 29, 2026**

---

*(Stage is dark. The projector flickers on. The sounds of a cassette tape clicking into a deck, followed by heavy tape hiss.)*

**(Slide: Matlock - 27 Years)**

**Kris:**
My friend Matt Lock—Matlock—is a musician. He makes incredible beats. For twenty-seven years, Matt had a box full of cassette tapes sitting in his house. Decades of unreleased genius. 

Why didn’t he release it? Because making the music is only eighty-five percent of the job. Finishing it—the mastering, the album art, the distribution—that last fifteen percent is brutal friction. It requires a whole different set of skills. So he sat on those tapes. For twenty-seven years.

Last November, Matt finally released *R3ALMS*. AI didn't write the music. AI didn't replace his creativity. AI gave him the last fifteen percent he needed to push it across the finish line. 

That is not replacement. That is liberation.

**(Slide: The Avatar / Title Card)**

Every cultural movement that has ever mattered started the exact same way. Someone picks up a cheap tool that a massive corporation built, and they use it wrong.

In 1916, Dada poets took the daily newspaper—the corporate information system of the era—cut it up with scissors, and rearranged it on the floor. That was sampling decades before vinyl even existed. William S. Burroughs did it and called it literature.

In 1976, a kid in London named Mark Perry saw the Ramones at the Roundhouse, walked out, and realized he didn't need a record label. He went to work, hijacked a defense contractor’s Xerox machine, and printed sixty copies of a zine called *Sniffin' Glue*. The punk rock press was born on stolen corporate ink.

**(Slide: The Selector)**

Around the exact same time, in the dancehalls of Kingston, Jamaica, a revolution was happening. The hero of the dance wasn't the guy playing the guitar. It was the Selector. 

The Selector was the person choosing the records. Because suddenly, making the sound wasn't the hard part—knowing *which* sound the room needed was the hard part. The skill was taste. 

That idea took a boat to the Bronx and became Hip Hop.

The pattern never changes. The corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it’s actually for.

**(Slide: The Xerox Machine)**

AI is just our generation's Xerox machine.

It is built by the biggest, most well-funded corporations on the face of the earth. And it is trained on the stolen work of every human being who ever had the audacity to post something online. 

I’m a photographer. I’ve found roughly one thousand, eight hundred of my own photos inside these AI models. They scraped Flickr. They scraped Creative Commons. Nobody asked me. Nobody paid me. I find my relationship with AI to be completely non-consensual.

**(Slide: Both Hands Full)**

But here is the part that I cannot look away from. 

Using these exact same stolen tools, I am more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I have ever been in my entire life.

Both of those statements are true. At the exact same time. 
That is not a contradiction. That is complexity. That is the punk condition.

You see it everywhere. I work with Luke Minaker, a guy who has spent 30 years building physical monsters for film. His whole career was 80% drudgery, 20% creative joy. AI flipped the ratio. He told me, "I don't care about the money. I am so excited that I get to do art again."

I think about Belgium, an actress who was bedridden for 18 months. She told me, "AI film is my hope to be able to continue to exist and to tell something." 

I think about my friend Kevin Friel, a 25-year Hollywood VFX veteran, who calls these tools an extension of his own brain. Kevin said, "There's something very punk rock about taking a tool that’s staring you in the face and using it to tell the stories that matter."

**(Slide: The Pivot)**

If every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person in Vancouver opts out of using AI because it is messy... who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used?

The tech bros. The people who don't see the problems. That's who.

I want to give you a punk rock framework for engaging with AI without selling your soul. 

**(Slide: Name What You See)**

**Rule Number 1: Name what you are seeing.** 
Stop using the word "bias." Bias sounds like a rounding error. When you type in "CEO" and it gives you twenty white men, that's not a bias. That is racism and misogyny encoded into mathematics. When it spits out an image mimicking an artist's exact brushstrokes, name the theft.

AI doesn't know what's fair. It only knows what's frequent on the internet. The mirror reflects, but it doesn't correct. Correcting the mirror is your job.

**(Slide: Externalize Your Taste)**

**Rule Number 2: Externalize your taste.**
In 2005, if you started a blog, you were a tastemaker, because generating content was hard and curation was easy. Today, generation is completely free. AI can generate a symphony or a screenplay in ten seconds. 

The new scarcity is taste. 

Your weird specific eye. Your refusal to use corporate jargon. Your instinct for when an image is just a little too glossy. You think that's magic, but it's not. It is pattern recognition that you haven't articulated yet. Unexamined expertise cannot be taught to a machine.

I challenge every single person in this room to go home today and write three documents.
1. A Personal AI policy. What will you use it for? What will you refuse to use it for?
2. A Writing Style Guide. What are the ten words you absolutely refuse to say? (Ban the word "delve").
3. A Worldview Document. Are you a Marxist? An anarchist? A punk? A Buddhist?

Feed those three documents to ChatGPT or Claude. Do that, and the machine stops giving you the average of the internet. It starts amplifying what makes you, *you*. You stop being a passive user, and you become the Selector.

**(Slide: Release Day)**

Which brings us to right now. The theme of this month is CREATE. The global activation is **Release Day on May twenty-ninth** — four weeks from tonight.

I don't just want you to think about this. I want you to make something.

I challenge every person in this room to use the rest of the month of May to externalize your creative taste. Feed it to the machine. And build something that only you could build.

A zine. A short film. A photo series. A manifesto. The album you’ve been sitting on for 27 years. I don’t care what it is. I just want you to release it by May 29th.

It doesn’t need to be polished. It doesn’t need to be perfect. 

Punk never was. 

As Kevin Friel said at our last meetup: "Let's use these tools to f*** it up, y'all."

You coming?

*(Fade to black. Outro music swell: The Clash or heavy dub bassline).*
