**Release Day CTA (global):** **May 29, 2026**

- **Prologue (0–2):** Warm, grounded, earning trust — the punk lineage
- **Act 1 (2–8):** Heavy, validating — naming what's real

## PROLOGUE: THE PATTERN (0:00 – 2:00)

**[NO SLIDES. Screen dark or OFF. Walk to center. No podium. Take a breath. Look at the room. Coffee cups. Morning light. The Creative Mornings energy — warm, curious, not yet caffeinated.]**

Good morning.

Every cultural movement that has ever mattered started the exact same way. Someone picks up a cheap tool that a massive corporation built — and they use it wrong.

In 1916, Dada poets took the daily newspaper — the corporate information system of the era — cut it up with scissors, and rearranged it on the floor. That was sampling decades before vinyl even existed.

In 1976, a kid named Mark Perry saw the Ramones at the Roundhouse in London, walked out, and realized he didn't need a record label. He went to work the next day, hijacked a defense contractor's Xerox machine, and printed sixty copies of a zine called *Sniffin' Glue*. The punk rock press was born on stolen corporate ink.

**[Beat.]**

Around the exact same time, in the dancehalls of Kingston, Jamaica, the hero of the dance wasn't the musician. It was the Selector. The person who chose the records. Because making the sound wasn't the hard part anymore. Knowing *which* sound the room needed — that was the skill. That was taste.

That idea took a boat to the Bronx and became Hip Hop.

**[Pause.]**

The pattern is always the same. The corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it's actually for.

## ACT 1: LEFT HAND — WHAT'S REAL (2:00 – 8:00)

**[Walk stage left. Energy shifts. This is where we name what hurts.]**

### The Non-Consensual Opening (2:00 – 4:00)

**[SLIDE 1: "I FIND MY RELATIONSHIP WITH AI COMPLETELY NON-CONSENSUAL." — Black background. White stencil text. NON-CONSENSUAL in RED.]**

**[Pause. Direct eye contact. Serious tone.]**

I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

**[Let that land. Silence.]**

I'm a photographer. I released thousands of photos on Flickr over two decades under Creative Commons. Shared them freely — that was the deal.

When I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly 1,800 of my images scraped into a major training corpus. Nobody asked me. Nobody paid me. They just took it.

**[Turn to audience.]**

If you've ever posted a photograph, uploaded a design, published a blog post, shared anything online — it may already be inside these models. You weren't asked. That's not hypothetical. That's what happened.

### Three Fears That Are Real (4:00 – 5:30)

**[SLIDE 2: Three Fears — numbered by hand, red check marks, typewriter text on black]**

Not hypothetical fears. Real things happening right now.

**One: Stolen work without consent.** Training data equals humanity's creative output scraped without permission. Your photos. Your designs. Your writing. Call it what it is.

**Two: Race to the bottom.** "Good enough" AI outputs are devaluing human craft. Clients are already saying it: "Can't you just use AI for this?" Translation: "Why should I pay you?"

**Three: Junior pipeline collapse.** Entry-level jobs are vanishing. The roles that trained the next generation are disappearing. How do people become art directors if nobody hires junior designers anymore?

**[Direct.]**

I teach this stuff for a living and I'll be honest: I don't have the junior pipeline problem solved. Nobody does. Anyone who tells you they do is selling something.

### Name What You're Seeing (5:30 – 7:00)

**[SLIDE 3: "STOP SAYING 'BIAS.' NAME WHAT YOU'RE SEEING." — RED stencil on black. Feels spray-painted.]**

And there's something else we need to talk about.

We've said the word "bias" so many times it's lost all meaning. Algorithmic bias. Training bias. Gender bias. Generic. Safe. Abstract.

Stop saying bias. Name what you're seeing.

**[Beat.]**

When you type "CEO" into an image generator and get twenty white men in power poses, that's not a bias. That's racism and misogyny encoded into mathematics.

When it spits out a design mimicking an artist's exact style, name the theft.

AI doesn't know what's fair. It only knows what's *frequent* on the internet. The mirror reflects. It doesn't correct. Correcting the mirror is your job.

### Holding the Left Hand (7:00 – 8:00)

**[SLIDE 4: RED hand print — LEFT hand. Spray paint edges. Primal.]**

**[Hold up left hand. Physical gesture. Slow.]**

This is what I carry. Stolen work. Devalued craft. Collapsing pipelines. Discrimination laundered through math. A relationship I never consented to.

Don't drop these. They keep you honest.

**[Pause. Then the turn:]**

Now let me show you what's in my right hand.

**[Energy shifts. Walk stage right.]**

## ACT 2: RIGHT HAND — WHAT'S ALSO TRUE (8:00 – 14:00)

**[SLIDE 5: "WHAT'S ALSO TRUE" — Background INVERTS from black to WHITE. First white-dominant slide. The visual shift should be jarring.]**

What's also true. Not hype. Not LinkedIn bullshit. Not some tech bro telling you AI will solve everything. Just actual transformations I've watched happen with my own eyes.

### Matt Lock: The 27-Year Cassette (8:00 – 10:00)

My friend Matt Lock — Matlock — is a musician. He makes incredible beats. For twenty-seven years, Matt had a box full of cassette tapes sitting in his house. Decades of unreleased music.

Why didn't he release it? Because making the music is only 85% of the job. Finishing it — the mastering, the album art, the distribution — that last 15% is brutal friction. It requires a completely different set of skills. So he sat on those tapes. For twenty-seven years.

**[Beat.]**

Last November, Matt finally released *R3ALMS*. AI didn't write his music. AI didn't replace his creativity. AI gave him the last fifteen percent he needed to push it across the finish line.

That is not replacement. That is liberation.

### Luke Minaker: The Monster Builder (10:00 – 11:30)

**[SLIDE 6: "80% GRIND / 20% CREATIVE" — crossed out BY HAND in thick red marker. Below: "80% CREATIVE / 20% GRIND" — circled in red.]**

Luke is an animator. Twelve years old, he sat in a theater watching Jurassic Park and said: "I want to bring monsters to life."

Thirty years later, he's not bringing monsters to life. He's grinding out corporate animation. 80% drudgery, 20% creative. That's what the industry gave him.

AI tools arrive. The ratio flips. 80% creative. 20% grind.

**[Pause.]**

Luke told me: "I don't care about the money. I am so excited that I get to do art again."

**[Let that land.]**

The 12-year-old who wanted to bring monsters to life? He's back. After 30 years. That's not replacement. That's liberation.

### Belgium: A Hope to Continue to Exist (11:30 – 12:30)

**[SLIDE 7: BLACK. White typewriter text. Clean. No collage. No texture. Just the words floating in darkness. Single small red dot in the corner. A heartbeat.]**

**[Voice drops. Slower pace.]**

An actress who goes by Belgium was bedridden for 18 months. Couldn't work. Couldn't perform. Couldn't do the thing that made her who she is.

She started making AI avatars. Creating characters. Building stories from her bed.

She said: "AI film is my hope to be able to continue to exist and to tell something."

**[Let the room sit with that.]**

When the debates get abstract and ideological, remember Belgium. Remember what access actually means.

### The Both/And Statement (12:30 – 14:00)

**[SLIDE 8: "AI is trained on stolen work without consent. AND I'm more creative than I've ever been. BOTH STATEMENTS ARE TRUE." — Heavy Xerox grain. Photocopied so many times the edges bleed. RED "AND" is the hinge.]**

**[Center stage. Both hands up.]**

My personal testimony:

AI is trained on stolen work without consent. And I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I have ever been in my entire life.

Both of those statements are true. At the exact same time.

**[Pause.]**

That is not a contradiction. That is complexity. That is the punk condition.

**[Hold up right hand.]**

Don't drop this either. The transformation is real. Luke got his art back. Kevin found a new way to conduct. Belgium found a way to exist.

## ACT 3: THE PUNK ROCK FRAMEWORK (14:00 – 20:00)

**[Move center. Both hands visible. Then lower them.]**

If every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person in Vancouver opts out of using AI because it is messy... who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used?

The tech bros. The people who don't see the problems. That's who.

**[Beat.]**

Vancouver is the home of Adbusters and Greenpeace. We've been turning corporate infrastructure into counterculture since before most tech cities had a scene. So here's a punk rock framework for engaging with AI without selling your soul.

### Step 1: Name What You See (14:30 – 15:30)

**[SLIDE 9: Punk zine spread — "STOP SAYING BIAS" crossed out, replaced with "SAY THEFT. SAY RACISM. SAY MISOGYNY."]**

We covered this. Stop laundering discrimination through soft language. When you see it, name it.

AI knows what's frequent. Not what's fair. Three questions to carry into every AI interaction: Who is missing from this output? What assumptions did the model use? What would change this response if the training data weren't dominated by English-language internet?

### Step 2: Become a Selector (15:30 – 17:00)

**[SLIDE 10: "GENERATION IS CHEAP. TASTE IS NOT." — stencil text, red underline on "TASTE"]**

Remember the Selector from Kingston? The person who chose the records at the sound system dance because taste was the skill?

In 2005, any kid with a blog was a tastemaker — because generating content was hard and curation was easy. Today, generation is completely free. AI can generate a screenplay or a brand identity in ten seconds.

**[Pause.]**

The new scarcity is taste.

Your weird specific eye. Your refusal to use corporate jargon. Your instinct for when a design is just a little too glossy. You think that's magic, but it's not. It's pattern recognition you haven't articulated yet.

Unexamined expertise can't be taught to a machine. But articulated taste can scale.

### Step 3: Externalize Your Taste — The Three Documents (17:00 – 19:30)

**[SLIDE 11: Three documents — glowing, radioactive, typewriter text on black. "PERSONAL POLICY / STYLE GUIDE / WORLDVIEW"]**

This is the punk rock move. This is practical.

I challenge every single person in this room to go home today and write three documents.

**One: A Personal AI Policy.** What will you use AI for? What will you refuse? Three minutes. Write it down.

**Two: A Writing Style Guide.** *(Or a design style guide, or a photography style guide — whatever your discipline.)* What are the ten words you absolutely refuse to say? What does your work sound like when it's really you? Ban the word "delve." *(Laughter.)* Four minutes.

**Three: A Worldview Document.** Are you a feminist? An anarchist? A punk? A Buddhist? What do you believe about people, about craft, about justice? Five sentences. Three minutes.

**[Direct.]**

Feed those three documents to ChatGPT or Claude. Do that, and the machine stops giving you the average of the internet. It starts amplifying what makes you *you*.

**[Pause.]**

As someone way smarter than me once said: *"If your values aren't in text, to AI they basically don't exist. And that is dangerously close to won't exist."*

**[Let that land.]**

Writing for the bot isn't narcissism. For a designer in Vancouver, for an Indigenous artist, for anyone whose perspective isn't already over-represented in training data — it's cultural activism.

## ACT 4: THE SOUL + CLOSE (20:00 – 25:00)

**[Quieter now. Slower. More intimate.]**

### Creative DNA (20:00 – 22:30)

**[SLIDE 12: BLACK. White typewriter. "CLOSE YOUR EYES."]**

So. The title of this talk. "Punk Rock AI." What does that actually mean?

Punk was never about the music, really. It was about agency. About picking up the tool and making something without waiting for permission. Without asking a gatekeeper for access.

**[Pause.]**

The list of what makes us uniquely human is getting shorter. Laughing until you can't breathe. Crying at a film that sees you. Kissing someone for the first time. Getting lost in the woods. Dancing until your atoms rearrange. The smell of this city after rain.

When the machines handle the calendaring, the accounting, the editing, the layout, the first draft — what actually remains?

Embodied experience. Desires and hopes. Empathy and intuition. The things you refuse to automate.

Those are the stubbornly human things. That's where your soul lives.

**[Pause.]**

I want to try something with you. If you're comfortable.

Close your eyes for a moment.

**[Wait for the room to settle. Give it a beat. Morning light. Coffee cups. Trust.]**

Think about one thing you do in your creative work — as a designer, as a photographer, as a writer, as a maker — that you would never want to give up to AI.

Not because you couldn't automate it. Maybe you could. But because it's the part that makes the work *yours*.

The last thing you'd hold onto.

**[Silence. Give them 15 seconds. Don't rush this. This is the emotional peak of the entire talk.]**

Open your eyes.

**[Pause.]**

That thing you were thinking of? That's your creative DNA. That's what the machines can't generate. That's what this whole talk has been about, really.

Everything else is negotiable. You can let AI help with everything else. That part? That part isn't negotiable.

### Release Day (22:30 – 24:30)

**[SLIDE 13: "MAY 29 — RELEASE DAY" — giant graffiti text, spray-painted across a digital canvas. Below: "SHIP SOMETHING IMPERFECT."]**

Which brings us to right now. The theme this month is CREATE. The global activation is **Release Day on May twenty-ninth** — four weeks from today.

I don't just want you to think about this. I want you to make something.

I challenge every person in this room to use the rest of the month of May to externalize your creative taste. Feed it to the machine. And build something that only you could build.

A zine. A short film. A photo series. A manifesto. A webapp. The album you've been sitting on for 27 years. I don't care what it is. I just want you to release it by May 29th.

**[Pause.]**

It doesn't need to be polished. It doesn't need to be perfect.

Punk never was.

### The Close (24:30 – 25:00)

**[SLIDE 14: Two RED hand prints — left and right — on black. Between them: "BOTH HANDS FULL. YOU COMING?"]**

**[Center stage. Both hands up.]**

Three options.

**[Left hand up.]** One: Embrace AI uncritically. Become a booster. Ignore the harms.

**[Right hand up.]** Two: Resist completely. Become a doomer. Maintain purity.

**[Both hands up.]** Three: Walk forward with both hands full.

**[Pause.]**

I'm here. Both hands full. Walking forward.

As my friend Kevin Friel says: "Let's use these tools to f*** it up, y'all."

**[Beat. Direct eye contact across the room.]**

You coming?

**[Hold silence. Three seconds. Then:]**

Thank you Vancouver.

**[Fade to black.]**
