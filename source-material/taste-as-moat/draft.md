# Taste As Moat: Why Judgment Matters More Than Generation

Week 2, coaching session.

Vicki. Visual artist. Portfolio gap. Uncertain about career.

Her question:

> "I'm a visual person, not a writer. I know we're supposed to document our thinking, but... what if I don't have words for what I do? What if my strength can't be explained?"

The fear underneath: "If AI requires me to articulate my process, and my process is visual/intuitive, then I can't use AI effectively."

My response:

> "Okay. Upload 20 of your favorite photos. Ask AI: 'What patterns do you see in my work? What choices am I making consistently?'"

She did.

The AI's analysis:

> "Your photos seem impromptu but there's deep intentionality. The connection between photographer and subject reveals itself in the eyes—that's where meaning concentrates. Your color palette trends toward warm earth tones with singular cool accent. Your composition follows rule of thirds but breaks it for emotional emphasis..."

Vicki's reaction.

Pause. Then:

> "It... it sees things I didn't know I was doing. I DO care about eyes. I DO use that color approach. I thought it was just intuition, but it's actually a pattern."

The revelation: She had taste. Deep, sophisticated taste. She just hadn't articulated it before.

AI became the mirror showing her own patterns.

The insight: You don't need to "be a writer" to have a process worth documenting. You just need to let AI show you the patterns you're already making.

That's not replacing your expertise. That's revealing it to you.

This article is about why taste—articulated taste—is your competitive moat in an AI world.

## Generation Is Cheap, Selection Is Not

2004. Documentary called RiP: A Remix Manifesto. About mashup culture, remix rights, creative theft.

The line that stuck:

> "Culture always builds on the past. The past tries to control the future."

DJs navigated this tension. They were called selectors.

Not creators. Selectors.

Why that word matters:

2005: Generation was hard, selection was easy.
- Hard to make music → Easy to choose what to play

2025: Reversed.
- Easy to generate anything → Hard to choose what's actually good

The shift: DJ was curator. Now everyone is.

The new scarcity:

Not: "Can you generate?" Everyone can. Midjourney, ChatGPT, Sora—commodity generation.

But: "Can you select?" Can you look at 10 AI outputs and explain why THIS one, not THAT one?

Most people can't.

They know what they like. They can't explain why.

That's the problem.

The taste gap:

Person A: "I like this design."
Person B: "Why?"
Person A: "I just... do. It feels right."

That's intuition. Valuable. But not transferable.

If you can't articulate why you chose it, you can't:
- Teach a team to make same choice
- Give AI guidance to replicate it
- Defend your decision to stakeholders
- Scale your judgment

Intuition that stays internal equals bottleneck.

You become the person everyone has to go through. Sounds powerful until you realize it means you can't scale, can't delegate, can't build systems.

The excavation:

AI forces you to externalize your taste.

Not because AI needs it (though it does). Because scaling needs it.

Vicki's realization: "I thought my intuition was magic. Actually it's pattern recognition I've developed over 10 years. AI helped me see the patterns."

Once you see them, you can teach them.

That's the moat.

## The Four Documents That Externalize Your Taste

The Creative DNA excavation process. What I teach students to build.

**Document 1: Writing Style Guide**

The exercise: Analyze 5-10 pieces you're proud of. Ask AI: "What patterns do you see in how I write?"

Example output:

> "You write in short sentences. Active voice dominant. Conversational tone but not sloppy. You use 'yo' and 'folks' frequently. You avoid corporate jargon, buzzwords, and emojis. You lead with story, end with takeaway. Average sentence: 12-15 words. Paragraph length: 2-4 sentences."

The realization: "Oh. THAT'S my voice. I didn't know I had such consistent patterns."

Now you can teach AI (or humans) to match it.

I did this exercise myself. Found patterns I didn't know were there. Documented them. Now when AI writes for me, it sounds like me. Because I gave it my actual patterns, not vague instructions.

**Document 2: Worldview Document**

The exercise: Answer these:
- What do I believe about my craft?
- What matters most to me in my work?
- What am I trying to achieve beyond deliverables?
- What do I refuse to compromise?

Example (designer):

> "I believe design should be accessible, not just beautiful. Every choice should serve the user's goals, not my portfolio. I will not sacrifice usability for aesthetics. I care about people using the thing successfully more than winning awards."

The realization: Your values shape your decisions. If AI doesn't know your values, it can't make decisions aligned with them.

This sounds obvious until you try it. Most people have never written down what they actually believe about their work.

**Document 3: Glossary + Anti-Glossary**

Glossary: Words you use constantly.

Example:
- "Folks" (not "guys" or "people")
- "Build" (not "develop" or "create")
- "Ship" (not "launch" or "release")
- "Systems" (not "hacks" or "tips")

Anti-Glossary: Words you never use.

Example:
- Never: "Leverage" (as verb)
- Never: "Synergy"
- Never: "Circle back"
- Never: "Low-hanging fruit"

The effect: AI now knows your language identity. Outputs sound like you, not corporate memo.

I have a whole anti-glossary. Words that make me cringe. If AI uses them, I know I didn't give it enough guidance.

**Document 4: Decision Logs**

The practice: When you make a choice (design A over B, word X over Y, approach 1 over 2), document why.

Example:

> "Chose serif font over sans-serif because this project is about tradition and trust. Sans-serif felt too modern/tech for the emotional tone we needed."

After 10 decisions logged, you have a pattern of how you think.

Feed that to AI. It learns your decision-making framework.

Vicki's example after documenting 15 photo selections:

> "I always choose the one where subject's eyes show vulnerability, even if composition is slightly worse. Eyes > perfection. That's my actual taste."

Now AI can help her select photos using that criterion.

The moat: Anyone can generate 1,000 photos. Vicki can select the one that matters. And explain why.

That's irreplaceable.

## The Taste Audit (How To Discover Your Own Patterns)

The exercise I give students:

**Step 1: Gather Your Best Work (10-20 examples)**

What are you proud of? Not what performed well. What represents your taste at its best.

**Step 2: Upload to AI**

"Analyze these. What patterns do you see in my choices, style, approach?"

**Step 3: Read The Analysis**

Look for surprises. Things AI sees that you didn't know you were doing.

Vicki: "Connection in eyes"
Designer student: "You break symmetry intentionally for tension"
Writer student: "You end every piece with a question"

**Step 4: Validate or Refine**

AI's analysis will be about 70% accurate.

Your job:
- What's true? (Keep that)
- What's wrong? (Correct it)
- What's missing? (Add it)

**Step 5: Create Your One-Page Taste Profile**

Format:

"I care about [what matters most].
I prioritize [hierarchy of values].
I always [consistent patterns].
I never [anti-patterns].
When choosing between X and Y, I pick X when [criteria]."

Example (photographer):

> "I care about human connection more than technical perfection. I prioritize emotional truth over aesthetic beauty. I always include eyes (even if it means worse composition). I never over-process skin tones. When choosing between sharp-but-cold and soft-but-warm, I pick warm when the subject is vulnerable, sharp when they're confident."

Now AI knows your taste framework.

**Step 6: Test It**

Give AI a task using your taste profile. See if output matches your judgment.

Refine until it does.

The result: You've externalized your taste.

Now it's:
- Teachable
- Scalable
- Valuable beyond your personal execution

That's the moat.

I did this myself. Took my best articles. Had AI analyze them. Found patterns I didn't know were consistent. Documented them. Now when I ask AI to write something, it sounds way more like me.

Not perfect. But 80% there instead of 20%.

That difference matters.

## The Shift From Execution to Judgment

What's actually changing in the AI moment:

Before: Your value came from execution. Your ability to make the thing.

Now: Your value comes from judgment. Your ability to choose the right thing.

Execution is getting commodified. Judgment isn't.

Anyone can generate 10 logo options. Can you pick the right one? Can you explain why it's right for this client, this moment, this context?

That's judgment. That's taste.

The students who get this stop worrying about AI replacing them. They start focusing on developing the judgment that AI can't replicate.

Because AI can generate. It can't judge. Not really.

It can approximate judgment if you feed it your criteria. But it can't develop new criteria. It can't understand context it hasn't seen. It can't make the leap from "this worked before" to "this is what this moment needs."

You can. If you develop that muscle.

The taste muscle gets stronger through:
- Documenting your decisions
- Explaining your reasoning
- Testing your frameworks
- Refining based on results

Most people skip this work. They execute based on intuition. Over and over. Never examining the intuition.

AI forces the examination.

And that examination makes you more valuable.
