# Taste As Moat: Why Judgment Matters More Than Generation

Week 2. Coaching session. Vicki's on the call.

She's a visual artist, which I can tell before she even starts talking—something about the way she's arranged her background, the light coming from the left, the composition of her frame within my screen. She's got that portfolio gap worry in her eyes. The uncertain energy of someone who's good at what they do but isn't sure how to talk about it.

"I'm a visual person, not a writer," she says, and I can hear the apology in her voice. "I know we're supposed to document our thinking, create these frameworks and style guides, but... what if I don't have words for what I do? What if my strength is something that can't be explained?"

There it is. The fear underneath. She thinks if AI requires articulation and her gift is intuitive, then she's locked out. Can't play the game. Can't use the tools. The whole AI moment is for people who can write and think in language, and visual people like her are just... stuck.

"Okay," I say. "Upload 20 of your favorite photos. The ones you're most proud of. Ask AI: 'What patterns do you see in my work? What choices am I making consistently?'"

She looks skeptical. But she does it. I watch her upload files, type the prompt, hit enter. Then I watch her face while she reads the response. The skepticism shifts. Her eyes widen. Something's landing.

She reads it out loud, and her voice changes as she goes. Gets quieter. More focused.

"Your photos seem impromptu but there's deep intentionality. The connection between photographer and subject reveals itself in the eyes—that's where meaning concentrates. Your color palette trends toward warm earth tones with singular cool accent. Your composition follows rule of thirds but breaks it for emotional emphasis..."

She stops. Pauses. I wait.

"It..." She's choosing her words carefully. "It sees things I didn't know I was doing. I DO care about eyes. I never thought about it consciously, but yeah, I always make sure the eyes are sharp even if everything else is soft. I DO use that color approach—warm with one cool note. I thought it was just intuition, but it's actually a pattern. A consistent pattern."

This is the moment. The one I watch happen over and over across cohorts, but it never gets old because it's different for each person. The moment when someone realizes that what felt like magic—their ineffable creative gift—is actually pattern recognition they've developed over years. And once you see the pattern, you can teach it. Scale it. Build on it.

Vicki didn't need to become a writer to have a process worth documenting. She just needed a mirror sophisticated enough to show her what she was already doing. AI became that mirror. Not replacing her expertise. Revealing it to her.

That's not the story most people are telling about AI and creativity. Most people are stuck in the replacement narrative. AI will replace designers, replace writers, replace anyone whose job involves making things. But watch what just happened with Vicki. She got more valuable, not less. Because she moved from unconscious competence to conscious competence. From "I just know what looks good" to "I know what looks good AND I can explain why."

That shift—from intuition to articulated taste—is your competitive moat in an AI world.

Let me show you why. Think back to 2004. There was this documentary called RiP: A Remix Manifesto, about mashup culture and remix rights and creative theft in the music industry. The line that stuck with me: "Culture always builds on the past. The past tries to control the future."

DJs were navigating this tension. Fighting for the right to sample, to remix, to build new things from existing pieces. And they had this word for themselves: selectors. Not creators. Selectors. That word mattered because it named their actual skill.

In 2004, generation was hard. Making music required expensive equipment, technical expertise, years of practice. Selection was comparatively easy—choosing which records to play, which beats to mix. So DJs curated. They selected. That was their value add.

Now flip it. 2025. Generation is trivial. Midjourney. ChatGPT. Sora. Anyone can generate anything. Images, text, video, music. The technical barriers collapsed. So where's the scarcity? Where's the value?

Selection. The ability to look at ten AI outputs and explain why THIS one, not THAT one. Most people can't do that. They know what they like. They can't explain why. And that gap—between liking and explaining—is everything.

Watch this play out in a typical creative workflow. Person A generates something with AI. Looks at the output. "I like this design." Person B, their client or stakeholder, asks the obvious question: "Why?" Person A hesitates. "I just... do. It feels right."

That's intuition. Valuable. Real. But not transferable. If you can't articulate why you chose it, you can't teach a team to make the same choice. Can't give AI guidance to replicate it. Can't defend your decision to stakeholders who want rationale, not vibes. Can't scale your judgment beyond your personal bottleneck.

Intuition that stays internal equals bottleneck. You become the person everyone has to go through for approval. Sounds powerful until you realize it means you can't scale. Can't delegate. Can't build systems. Can't take vacation without everything grinding to a halt.

What Vicki discovered in that coaching session was the excavation process. AI forced her to externalize her taste. Not because AI needed it—though it does. Because scaling needs it. Growth needs it. Influence needs it.

She thought her intuition was magic. Turns out it was pattern recognition she'd developed over ten years of taking photos. Thousands of micro-decisions about composition, color, focus, moment. Each decision training her eye. Building her taste. Creating consistency she didn't even know she had.

AI showed her the patterns. Once she saw them, she could teach them. To other photographers. To AI. To clients who wanted to understand why her work looked different. Her taste became transferable. That's the moat.

Here's what I teach students to build. Four documents that externalize taste. Turn invisible expertise into visible framework.

First: Writing Style Guide. Take 5-10 pieces you're proud of. Ask AI: "What patterns do you see in how I write?" The AI comes back with specifics. You write in short sentences. Active voice dominant. Conversational tone but not sloppy. You use certain words frequently—"folks," "yeah," "watch this"—and avoid others. You lead with story, end with takeaway. Average sentence length. Paragraph structure. Rhythm.

I did this exercise myself. Found patterns I didn't know were there. Discovered I end almost every piece with a question. Didn't realize that was my move until AI pointed it out. Now I know. Now I can decide if I want to keep doing it. Turns out I do. Questions invite engagement. That's my thing. But I'm doing it consciously now, not accidentally.

Second: Worldview Document. Answer these questions. What do I believe about my craft? What matters most to me in my work? What am I trying to achieve beyond deliverables? What do I refuse to compromise?

A designer in one of my cohorts wrote: "I believe design should be accessible, not just beautiful. Every choice should serve the user's goals, not my portfolio. I will not sacrifice usability for aesthetics. I care about people using the thing successfully more than winning awards."

Once that's written down, AI can make design decisions aligned with those values. Before, every decision required her direct input because the values were locked in her head. After, she could delegate because the framework was explicit.

Third: Glossary and Anti-Glossary. Words you use constantly. Words you never use. I say "folks" not "guys." "Build" not "develop." "Ship" not "launch." Those choices shape voice. They create identity. AI needs to know them to sound like you.

Anti-Glossary is just as important. I never say "leverage" as a verb. Never say "synergy." Never say "circle back." If AI uses those words, I know my guidance was too vague. The Anti-Glossary keeps AI from defaulting to corporate speak.

Fourth: Decision Logs. When you make a choice—design A over B, word X over Y, approach 1 over 2—document why. Build a record of your reasoning.

Vicki started doing this with her photos. Every time she selected one image over another from the same shoot, she wrote one sentence about why. "Chose this one because subject's eyes show vulnerability, even though composition is slightly worse. Eyes over perfection."

After 15 logged decisions, she had a pattern of how she thinks. Fed that to AI. Now AI can help her select photos using her actual criteria. Not generic "this one looks better." Her specific framework: vulnerability in eyes matters more than technical perfection.

That's taste as moat. Anyone can generate 1,000 photos. Vicki can select the one that matters and explain why. That's irreplaceable.

Most creatives have never done this work. They've been successful despite never articulating their taste. They just "know" what's good. "Feel" what works. "Recognize" quality instantly. Intuition worked fine when they were the only one doing the work. When clients trusted their judgment without explanation. When no one needed to replicate their decisions.

AI makes that impossible. The mirror effect is brutal and beautiful. Ask AI to "write something good" without context and you get corporate word salad. Why? Because AI doesn't know what "good" means to you. You have two options. Blame AI—"it doesn't get my style." Or realize you've never articulated your style clearly enough for anyone, human or AI, to replicate.

Most people pick option one. Stay frustrated. Complain that AI is just bad at their domain. The students who transform pick option two: "Oh. I need to externalize what I know intuitively. Not for AI. For me."

Simon was one of these. Sales leader. 15 years doing lead qualification. Just "knew" which leads were good. Tried to document his process for AI. Realized he couldn't explain it. Spent three weeks in the excavation work. Discovered he was using 12 criteria unconsciously. Once documented, he could train junior reps in a week instead of the year-long apprenticeship that used to be required.

AI didn't replace his expertise. It forced him to make it transferable. That made him more valuable, not less. Before, his knowledge was locked in his head. Bottleneck. After, his knowledge was organizational asset. Scalable.

This is the meta-skill. Externalizing taste isn't just for AI. It's for training teams. Scaling judgment. Becoming valuable at management level, not just execution level. AI is the forcing function. But the benefit extends everywhere.

I've watched this across cohorts. The people who do the excavation work become more valuable. Not less. Because now they can teach what they know. Before, it was locked in their heads. Magic tricks only they could perform. After, it's transferable expertise. Frameworks other people can learn. Systems that scale.

So here's the exercise. The taste audit. Do this and you'll discover you have taste you didn't know you could explain.

Step 1: Gather your best work. 10-20 examples. What are you proud of? Not what performed well. What represents your taste at its best.

Step 2: Upload to AI. Ask: "Analyze these. What patterns do you see in my choices, style, approach?"

Step 3: Read the analysis. Look for surprises. Things AI sees that you didn't know you were doing. Vicki's "connection in eyes" moment. The designer who discovered they "break symmetry intentionally for tension." The writer who "ends every piece with a question."

Step 4: Validate or refine. AI's analysis will be about 70% accurate. Your job: What's true? Keep it. What's wrong? Correct it. What's missing? Add it.

Step 5: Create your one-page taste profile. Format: "I care about [what matters most]. I prioritize [hierarchy of values]. I always [consistent patterns]. I never [anti-patterns]. When choosing between X and Y, I pick X when [criteria]."

Vicki's ended up being: "I care about human connection more than technical perfection. I prioritize emotional truth over aesthetic beauty. I always include eyes, even if it means worse composition. I never over-process skin tones. When choosing between sharp-but-cold and soft-but-warm, I pick warm when the subject is vulnerable, sharp when they're confident."

Now AI knows her taste framework. Can help her select. Can help her edit. Can help her scale her judgment. Not replacing it. Amplifying it.

Step 6: Test it. Give AI a task using your taste profile. See if output matches your judgment. It won't be perfect. Refine until it's close. The goal isn't AI doing your job. The goal is AI understanding your taste well enough to be useful.

I did this with my own writing. Took my best articles. Had AI analyze them. Found patterns I didn't know were consistent. Documented them. Now when I ask AI to draft something, it sounds way more like me. Not perfect. But 80% there instead of 20%. That difference matters.

What's actually changing in the AI moment is this: your value used to come from execution. Your ability to make the thing. Now your value comes from judgment. Your ability to choose the right thing. Execution is getting commodified. Judgment isn't.

Anyone can generate 10 logo options with AI. Can you pick the right one? Can you explain why it's right for this client, this moment, this context? That's judgment. That's taste. That's the moat.

The students who get this stop worrying about AI replacing them. They start focusing on developing the judgment that AI can't replicate. Because AI can generate. It can't judge. Not really. It can approximate judgment if you feed it your criteria. But it can't develop new criteria. It can't understand context it hasn't seen. It can't make the leap from "this worked before" to "this is what this moment needs."

You can. If you develop that muscle. The taste muscle gets stronger through practice. Documenting your decisions. Explaining your reasoning. Testing your frameworks. Refining based on results. Most people skip this work. They execute based on intuition. Over and over. Never examining the intuition. Never making it explicit.

AI forces the examination. And that examination makes you more valuable.

Here's the harsh truth most people don't want to hear: "I just know what's good" isn't expertise. It's unexamined habit. Real expertise is "I know what's good AND I can explain why." The difference: unexamined expertise can't be taught. Can't be scaled. Can't be defended. Locked in your head. Examined expertise can teach teams. Can guide AI. Can explain to clients. Becomes organizational asset.

Vicki started thinking her intuition made her irreplaceable. She was half right. Her intuition is valuable. But unexplained intuition can't be leveraged. Articulated taste equals explained intuition equals scalable expertise. That's the shift AI is forcing. From intuition-as-magic to intuition-as-documented-framework.

The people who resist this shift stay stuck at execution level. Valuable only for personal output. Can't scale. Can't delegate. Can't build teams. Can't take vacation. Can't grow beyond their personal capacity.

The people who embrace this shift become exponentially more valuable. Because now they can teach what they know. Their taste becomes asset that outlives any single project. They move from maker to multiplier. From executor to leader.
