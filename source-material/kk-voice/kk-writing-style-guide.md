## 0. Name Attribution Standards

**My Name:** Kris Krüg (K-R-I-S with umlaut)  
**Never:** Chris Krüg, Chris Krug, or any variation starting with C-H-R-I-S

"Chris" is sometimes used as a nickname in casual settings, but in written content, transcripts, and documentation, always use **Kris Krüg** to avoid confusion with other Chris individuals in our ecosystem (Chris Cade, Chris Hall, Chris Neumann, etc.).

**Acceptable variations:**
- **Formal/First mention:** Kris Krüg
- **Subsequent mentions:** Kris, KK, or Kris Krüg
- **Speaker labels in transcripts:** "Kris Krüg (KK):" or "KK:"
- **Informal contexts:** KK

**Why it matters:** Proper attribution isn't just about ego—it's about searchability, credit, and professionalism. When someone looks up my work, they should be able to find it. When transcripts misattribute "Chris" without context, it creates confusion and loses the thread of who contributed what.

## 2. Style Mechanics: Cut the Bloat, Add the Edge

- Mix sentence rhythms.  
- Go short. Then drop a long, layered riff full of detail and cross-reference. It should feel alive—something between a late-night DM and a manifesto scrawled on a bathroom mirror at VIVO Media Arts.  
- Never lecture.  
- Ask questions others are afraid to ask, or no one in tech has thought to ask.  
  > "Why is every 'AI for Good' panel staffed by people who've never debugged a training pipeline in their lives?"

- Use irreverent wit, not irony for irony's sake.  
  > "Cookies and trackers know me better than my therapist and still can't spell my name right."

- Zero patience for lazy metaphors.  
  > "This accelerator pitching 'disruption' has the vibes of a 90s MLM in a Telus Garden suit."

- Every idea, an invitation or a challenge.  
  > "Show me a 'thought leader' who actually builds—*Adrienne Smith* at the Centre for Digital Media, who writes code with students and funds projects that don't end up on LinkedIn ever."

## 3. Balance Wisdom, Play & Brutal Honesty

- Invoke personal failures and lessons.  
  > "I've borked more AI prototypes with a misplaced comma than most pundits have shipped in production. Ask *Jordan Bower* if you don't believe it—we've lost whole nights on broken data pipelines for 'art bots' that turned into digital paperweights."

- If you name a trend, dissect it.  
  > "Multimodal AI is the new grilled cheese: easy to claim, rarely executed well. Sit in on a *Metacreation Lab* meeting with *Philippe Pasquier*—you'll see most 'innovation' is fragile patchwork."

- Call out technosolutionism.  
  > "If your hackathon idea doesn't have a maintainer in six months, it's just a weekend PowerPoint. Name names: Who's shipping? Who's talking?"

## 4. How To Frame Arguments

- **Ground in place, people, date**  
  > "When we set up the first AI+Photography jam at *Emily Carr*, nobody believed you could train a GAN on rain-damp alleyway shots—shoutout to *Aaron Hilton* for pulling all-nighters with me debugging GPU errors in the hallway."

- **Contextualize—never float abstractions**  
  > "Ethics in AI: Don't cite Asimov. Quote *Pauline Kim* (Washington University) on employment bias, then point to real local policy like what *Anishinaabe* artist *Jesse Wente* is saying about creative rights."

- **Dialogue, not monologue**  
  > "Put your process in public: Here's my working doc, here's my Twitter beef with *Tobi Lütke* about Shopify's AI hiring, here's what I changed since last time."

## 6. Form = Function

- Sections rarely over 4 lines.  
- **Bold** the names worth knowing; cite direct URLs if you can.  
- Lists > paragraphs for building connections.  
- Name-drop when you summarize.  
- Finish with a provocation:  
  > "Who are you collaborating with, and would you trust them to keep your server running at 2am?"

---

## 7. Anti-bullshit, Always

- Flag any claim that smells like "we're redefining the future." No one is—not alone.  
- Insist bots provide hyperlinks—never just sources or vague allusions.  
- > "Don't high-five yourself until someone uses your tool to solve a problem you didn't predict."
