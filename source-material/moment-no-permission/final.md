# The Moment Didn't Ask Permission: Agency Within Inevitability

One day you're building a career through craft.

You're learning the tools. Getting good at them. Not overnight—over years. The slow accumulation of skill that comes from showing up every day and doing the work. You're mastering Photoshop or After Effects or your writing voice or your design eye. Building something that feels solid. Something that's yours. Expertise that took time and sweat and failure and persistence.

You're earning your expertise the way everyone always has. Through repetition. Through getting it wrong until you get it right. Through the thousand hours and the ten thousand hours and all the hours after that where you're not counting anymore, you're just doing the work because this is what you do. This is who you are.

Next day, someone's selling your style for $20 a month.

Not metaphorically. Literally. Subscribe to "draw like you" as a service. Your voice. Your aesthetic. Your hard-won way of seeing. Packaged and productized and available to anyone with a credit card and an internet connection.

You didn't choose this. You weren't asked. Nobody sent you an email saying "Hey, we're training AI on everything you've ever made—you good with that?" Nobody held a meeting to discuss whether this fundamental alteration of your industry was something you wanted to participate in. It just happened. While you were paying your bills and raising your kids and doing your work, while you were being a professional and showing up and building your career one careful brick at a time, the world changed underneath you.

That's what I mean when I say the moment didn't ask permission. This wasn't a choice you made. This wasn't a disruption you saw coming and prepared for. This was non-consensual. That word makes people uncomfortable. Good. It should. Because that's what this is.

Ten years mastering Photoshop, and Midjourney replicates your style in 30 seconds. Twenty years developing your writing voice, and ChatGPT approximates it on demand. A career built on the scarcity of skill, and suddenly skill is commodity. Subscribe for $20 a month. Cancel anytime.

You're pissed. Of course you're pissed. Anyone who isn't pissed isn't paying attention. The theft is real. The violation is real. The disruption is real. Your work is in those training sets. Your photos, your words, your code, your designs. They took it without asking. They used it without compensating. They deployed it without consent.

And the moment is still here.

The AI exists. The training happened. The markets shifted. You can be as pissed as you want—and you should be, the anger is righteous—but the anger doesn't change the timeline. The moment is here and it's not asking permission to stay.

So the question becomes: How do you navigate a moment you didn't choose and can't undo?

Most people want you to pick a side. They want this to be simple. Accept it or resist it. Adopt or refuse. Complicity or purity. Choose your team. All in or all out. The Boosters say embrace it fully, stop whining, get on board or get left behind. The Doomers say resist completely, don't be complicit, burn it all down.

Both camps are offering you the same thing underneath the rhetoric: a clean narrative. A simple position. A way to feel certain in an uncertain moment. And I'm saying there's a third option that nobody wants to talk about because it's uncomfortable and messy and doesn't fit neatly into anyone's ideological box.

Engage with agency while acknowledging inevitability.

Let me show you what I mean by inevitability. These are the things you can't change. The things that are true whether you accept them or not.

AI exists. You can protest. You can resist. You can refuse to use it. Won't change the fact that AI still exists. Companies are still using it. Clients are still expecting it. Your competitors are still adopting it. The technology is out there in the world and it's not going back in the box no matter how much you wish it would.

Training data includes your work. You can file lawsuits—some people are. You can opt out going forward—some platforms allow it now. Won't change what already happened. Data already scraped. Models already trained. Outputs already in market. The theft already occurred. The violation is in the past. You can seek remedies, you can demand compensation, you can fight for better systems going forward. But you can't unscramble this egg.

Industries are shifting. You can try to preserve old models. You can advocate for regulations that protect traditional workflows. And you should—we need those protections. But while you're advocating, junior roles are disappearing. Client expectations are evolving. Markets are rewarding AI-augmented workers over traditional approaches. The shift is happening whether you participate or not.

The timeline is measured in months, not years. This isn't slow-burn disruption you have decades to adapt to. In 18 months, productivity tools became standard. AI-fluent workers started outperforming. Companies began restructuring around AI capability. You can't personally slow this down. You can't opt out of living in this timeline.

The anger about all of this is valid. I'm pissed too. The non-consent is real. The theft is real. The disruption is real. Being pissed doesn't change the inevitability. You can be furious about the moment and still have to navigate it.

So what does agency look like inside inevitability? What can you actually control when the big picture is out of your hands?

You can choose how you engage. Emily teaches elementary school. She can't stop kids from using AI. That's the inevitability. They're using it at home. They're using it on their phones. They're going to encounter it whether she likes it or not. But she can choose to teach them about it compassionately. That's agency.

So she built Bee School. Environmental education plus AI literacy. Kids learn how to use AI tools and why consent matters and what data sovereignty means. She's not saying "AI is fine." She's saying "AI is here, and I'm going to make sure kids understand what they're using." Not acceptance. Active shaping. That's what agency inside inevitability looks like.

You can build alternative models. I can't stop AI training on stolen work. That already happened. That's inevitability. But I can build alternatives. That's agency. So I worked with Philippe Pasquier's Metacreation Lab at SFU. We trained models on consented data. My own work. 2,000 cross-processed portraits. Twenty years of street photography. All my photos. Nobody else's data stolen.

The result isn't as sophisticated as models trained on billions of images. I'm not pretending it is. But it proves a point. Other approaches exist. We presented it at Ars Electronica. Got honorable mention. Published peer-reviewed research. Showed the world: you can engage with AI without participating in extraction. Not boycott. Demonstration of alternative. That's agency.

You can set boundaries around what you automate. I give students this exercise: "List five things you'll never automate, even if AI could do them." Thank you notes. Apologies. Creative decisions. Hiring decisions. Ethical calls. The things that require genuine emotion or authentic remorse or taste or human judgment or values. You're not resisting AI broadly. You're creating protocol around where it belongs and where it doesn't.

That's agency. You're drawing lines. Saying "AI can help here, but not here. This stays human." Most people either automate everything they can or refuse to automate anything. Both positions avoid the harder work of discernment. Of deciding what should stay human and what can be delegated. That decision-making is agency.

You can teach others to engage critically. Every cohort I run, Week 1, I show the Five Fears. I don't hide the problems. I name them. Stolen work. Collapsing pipelines. Cognitive dependency. Commodification. All of it. Then I show transformations alongside critique. Both hands full. The result: students engage with eyes open. Not naive adoption. Not blind resistance. Critical engagement.

They see the problems and the possibilities. They make informed choices. That's what I want. People who understand what they're doing and why. Not people who adopt because everyone else is. Not people who resist because it feels pure. People who think it through and choose with intention.

You can advocate for change while using tools. The both/and. Use AI because clients expect it. Use it because it creates capability you need to stay competitive. And advocate for regulation. For artist compensation. For consent frameworks. For better systems. These aren't contradictory. You can benefit from current system and push for better system simultaneously.

Caroline runs Endinomics. She uses AI for Indigenous economic analysis right now, with current tools. She doesn't have the luxury of waiting for perfect ethical systems to exist. Her community needs the analysis now. But simultaneously, she's building data sovereignty frameworks so future Indigenous projects own their data. Not waiting for perfect system. Engaging with current system while building better one.

That's agency inside inevitability. Not acceptance. Not resistance. Strategic engagement.

Here's the governance question that keeps me awake. When new technology emerges, there's a pattern. True Believers adopt uncritically. "This will save the world!" Thoughtful Critics see the problems. Often opt out. "I won't be complicit." Result: governance gets made by True Believers. No critical voices at the table.

Real example from our community. Early AI ethics boards: mostly AI optimists. Critics refused to participate. "I'd be legitimizing something I oppose." Noble stance. Clear conscience. Zero influence. Result: weak ethics frameworks with no teeth. Why? Because the people who understood the risks best weren't in the room.

The lesson: opting out doesn't protect you. It removes your voice. My argument: if you see problems others don't, your responsibility is to stay in the room. Not to validate bad systems. To push for better ones. There's a difference between complicity and critical engagement. Complicity is uncritical participation. Critical engagement is participating while maintaining critique.

Emily's colleagues wanted to ban AI from schools. "Don't participate." Clean position. Clear stance. Zero impact on what kids learn. Emily stayed in. "I'm going to teach kids how to use it responsibly. Because if I don't, who will?" She's not uncritical. She's critically engaged. The result: kids in her program learn AI capability and consent frameworks and data sovereignty and ethical considerations. Because she stayed in the room.

The alternative: if Emily opted out, kids learn AI from YouTube with zero ethical framework. Which outcome is worse? I know my answer.

The people most uncomfortable with AI are often the people who should be shaping its use. If they opt out, we lose the ethical depth we need most. I'm not opting out. I'm staying in the room with both hands full. Using AI while advocating for artist compensation. Teaching capability while maintaining critique. Building with current tools while pushing for better systems. All of it simultaneously.

What does both/and look like day to day? Monday morning: use ChatGPT to compress four hours into 30 minutes. That's engaging capability, recognizing inevitability, choosing to be effective. Monday afternoon: teach students about consent violations in training data. That's maintaining ethical clarity, naming the problems, refusing to pretend everything's fine. Monday evening: build open-source alternative using consented data. That's creating better models, demonstrating alternatives, showing what's possible.

All the same day. Not contradictory. All necessary. The principle: critique doesn't require withdrawal. Engagement doesn't require uncritical acceptance. You can use tools and advocate for artist compensation. Benefit from efficiency and push for consent frameworks. Build with AI and support regulatory limitations. Adopt capability and maintain ethical boundaries. Both. Simultaneously.

The students who get this aren't paralyzed by ethical concerns. They engage with critique intact. They build AI tools for clients—paying their bills. They donate to organizations fighting for artist rights—honoring their values. They teach critical AI use—shaping next generation. All three. Not one or the other. That's the messy middle. Uncomfortable. True. The only honest position I've found.

Most people want clean narratives. They want AI to be all good or all bad. They want simple answers. "Should I use this or not?" And I'm saying both. Neither. Depends on how we use it and what systems we build around it. Can you hold that complexity? Or do you need certainty?

The moment didn't ask permission. AI exists. Training happened. Markets shifted. Industries transformed. You didn't choose this timeline. Nobody asked if you wanted to live through this disruption. It just happened. You're here. Now what?

You can opt out. Maintain purity. Refuse to engage. Clean conscience. Clear position. And governance gets set by uncritical adopters while you watch from the sidelines. You have no voice in how it unfolds. That's one choice.

Or you can opt in. Engage critically. Build alternative models while using the tools. Stay in the room. Keep your hands full—critique in one, curiosity in the other. Uncomfortable. Messy. Effective. That's another choice.

The uncomfortable truth: if all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out, the only voices in the room are True Believers and opportunists. That's worse than engaging with critique intact. I'd rather be in the room with both hands full than outside the room maintaining purity while decisions get made without me.

The moment is here. You didn't choose it. Can't undo it. But you still get to choose how you move through it. Choose agency. Choose critical engagement. Both hands full. The alternative is watching from the sidelines while others decide your future.

I'm not doing that. You coming?
