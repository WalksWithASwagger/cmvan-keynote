# The Moment Didn't Ask Permission: Agency Within Inevitability

One day you're building a career through craft. Mastering your tools. Earning your expertise. Playing the long game.

The next day, someone's selling your style for $20/month.

Your writing. Your art. Your code. Your design sensibility. Packaged. Productized. Available to anyone.

You didn't choose this.

Nobody asked:
- "Can we train AI on your life's work?"
- "Can we commodify your expertise?"
- "Can we fundamentally alter your industry?"

It just... happened.

While you were paying your bills, raising your kids, building your career, the world changed underneath you.

This is what I mean by "the moment didn't ask permission."

For creatives, this is visceral.

10 years mastering Photoshop → Midjourney replicates your style in 30 seconds.

20 years developing your writing voice → ChatGPT approximates it on demand.

Career built on scarcity of skill → Skill becomes commodity.

You're pissed. Rightfully.

And.

The moment is still here.

The question: How do you navigate a moment you didn't choose and can't undo?

The trap: People want you to pick. Accept it (complicity) or resist it (irrelevance).

I'm saying there's a third option: Engage with agency while acknowledging inevitability.

This article is about that navigation.

---

## What Inevitability Means

The things you can't change:

**AI Exists**

You can protest. You can resist. You can refuse.

Won't change: AI still exists. Companies still use it. Clients still expect it.

**Training Data Includes Your Work**

You can file lawsuits (some are). You can opt out going forward (some have).

Won't change: Data already scraped. Models already trained. Outputs already in market.

**Industries Are Shifting**

You can try to preserve old models.

Won't change: Junior roles disappearing. Client expectations evolving. Markets rewarding AI-augmented workers over traditional.

**The Timeline Is Measured in Months, Not Years**

This isn't slow-burn disruption.

In 18 months:
- Productivity tools became standard
- AI-fluent workers started outperforming
- Companies began restructuring around AI capability

You can't slow this down personally.

The anger is valid. I'm pissed too.

The non-consent is real. The theft is real. The disruption is real.

And.

Being pissed doesn't change the timeline.

The choice:

Option 1: Opt out. Maintain purity. Refuse to engage.

Result: Governance and norms get set by uncritical adopters. You have no voice in how it unfolds.

Option 2: Opt in. Engage critically. Build alternative models while using the tools.

Result: You have agency in shaping what happens next.

The uncomfortable truth: If all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out—the only voices in the room are true believers and opportunists.

That's worse than engaging with critique intact.

I'd rather be in the room with both hands full than outside the room maintaining purity while decisions get made without me.

## What Agency Looks Like Within Inevitability

The actions you can take even when the big picture is inevitable:

**1. Choose How You Engage**

Emily's choice:

Can't stop kids from using AI (inevitability).

Can choose to teach them about AI compassionately (agency).

Result: Bee School. Environmental education plus AI literacy. Kids learn how to use AI and why consent matters and what data sovereignty means.

Not acceptance. Active shaping.

She's not saying "AI is fine." She's saying "AI is here, and I'm going to make sure kids understand what they're using."

**2. Build Alternative Models**

My choice:

Can't stop AI training on stolen work (already happened).

Can build alternatives. Metacreation Lab—train models on consented data.

Result: Proof that other models exist. Presented at Ars Electronica. Showed it's possible to engage with AI without participating in extraction.

Not boycott. Demonstration of alternative.

2,000 cross-processed portraits. All my own photos. Trained a model on my own work. Nobody else's data stolen.

The result isn't as good as models trained on billions of images. But it's mine. And it proves a point: You can do this differently.

**3. Set Boundaries Around What You Automate**

The exercise I give students:

"List 5 things you'll never automate, even if AI could do them."

Examples:
- Thank you notes (relationship building requires genuine emotion)
- Apologies (requires authentic remorse)
- Creative decisions (taste is your job)
- Hiring decisions (humans deserve human judgment)
- Ethical calls (can't delegate values)

The effect: You're not resisting AI broadly. You're creating protocol around where it belongs and doesn't.

That's agency.

You're drawing lines. Saying: "AI can help here. But not here. This stays human."

**4. Teach Others To Engage Critically**

My choice:

Every cohort, Week 1: I show the Five Fears. I don't hide the problems. I name them.

Stolen work. Collapsing pipelines. Cognitive dependency. Commodification. All of it.

Then I show transformations alongside critique.

Result: Students engage with eyes open. Not naive adoption. Not blind resistance. Critical engagement.

They see the problems and the possibilities. They make informed choices.

That's what I want. People who understand what they're doing and why.

**5. Advocate for Change While Using Tools**

The both/and:

Use AI tools (because clients expect it, because it creates capability).

Advocate for regulation (because consent matters, because artists deserve compensation).

These aren't contradictory.

You can benefit from current system and push for better system.

Caroline's example (Endinomics):

Uses AI for Indigenous economic analysis (right now, with current tools).

Simultaneously: Builds data sovereignty frameworks so future Indigenous projects own their data.

Not waiting for perfect system. Engaging with current system while building better one.

The meta-pattern: Agency within inevitability equals strategic engagement.

Not acceptance. Not resistance. Active participation in shaping what comes next.

## The Governance Question

The pattern when new technology emerges:

True Believers: Adopt uncritically. "This will save the world!"

Thoughtful Critics: See problems. Often opt out. "I won't be complicit."

Result: Governance gets made by true believers. No critical voices at the table.

Real example from community:

Early AI ethics boards: Mostly AI optimists.

Critics refused to participate. "I'd be legitimizing something I oppose."

Result: Weak ethics frameworks. No teeth.

Why: The people who understood risks best weren't in the room.

The lesson: Opting out doesn't protect you. It removes your voice.

My argument: If you see problems others don't, your responsibility is to stay in the room.

Not to validate bad systems. To push for better ones.

Difference:
- Complicity = Uncritical participation
- Critical engagement = Participating while maintaining critique

Emily's example again:

Her colleagues: "We should ban AI. Don't participate."

Emily: "I'm going to teach kids how to use it responsibly. Because if I don't, who will?"

She's not uncritical. She's engaged critically.

The result: Kids in her program learn AI capability and consent frameworks and data sovereignty and ethical considerations.

Because she stayed in the room.

The alternative: If Emily opted out, kids learn AI from YouTube with zero ethical framework.

Which is worse?

The governance implication: The people most uncomfortable with AI are often the people who should be shaping its use.

If they opt out, we lose the ethical depth we need.

I'm staying in the room. Because someone needs to be saying: "This is problematic and here's how we do it better."

If I leave, who's left saying that?

## The Both/And Practice

What both/and looks like daily:

Monday morning: Use ChatGPT to compress 4 hours into 30 minutes (agency—engaging capability).

Monday afternoon: Teach students about consent violations in training data (critique—maintaining ethical clarity).

Monday evening: Build open-source alternative using consented data (agency—creating better models).

All the same day. Not contradictory. All necessary.

The principle: Critique doesn't require withdrawal.

Engagement doesn't require uncritical acceptance.

You can:
- Use the tools AND advocate for artist compensation
- Benefit from efficiency AND push for consent frameworks
- Build with AI AND support regulatory limitations
- Adopt capability AND maintain ethical boundaries

Both. Simultaneously.

The students who get this aren't paralyzed by ethical concerns. They engage with critique intact.

Example:

Build AI tools for clients (paying bills). Donate to organizations fighting for artist rights (values). Teach critical AI use (shaping next generation).

All three. Not one or the other.

The students who don't get this either adopt uncritically (lose ethical grounding) or resist completely (lose agency in shaping outcomes).

Both positions are cleaner. Both are incomplete.

The messy middle: This is hard. Uncomfortable. Requires holding tension.

But it's the only honest position.

I can't pretend AI is all good. I can't pretend opting out helps anyone.

So I'm here. Both hands full. Using the tools while pushing for better systems.

That's the practice.

## The Three Levels of Response

I've watched people respond to this moment at three levels:

**Level 1: Reactive**

"AI is bad, I'm out." Or "AI is good, I'm all in."

Clean. Simple. Wrong.

Misses the complexity. Either becomes complicit or becomes irrelevant.

**Level 2: Adaptive**

"I'll use AI because I have to, but I don't like it."

Better. At least engaging. But no agency in shaping outcomes.

Just reacting to forces beyond your control.

**Level 3: Strategic**

"I'll use AI critically while building alternatives and advocating for change."

Both hands full. Messy. True.

Engaging with eyes open. Using capability while maintaining values. Shaping outcomes instead of just reacting.

The students who transform operate at Level 3.

They're not naive. They're not pure. They're strategic.

And they're the ones who will have influence 10 years from now. Because they stayed in the conversation.

## The Moment Is Here

The inevitability: AI exists. Training happened. Markets shifted.

You didn't choose it.

The agency: You can choose what you do next.

Engage critically (both hands full). Adopt uncritically (right hand only). Resist completely (left hand only).

The governance question: If all the critics opt out, who writes the rules?

The people who don't see the problems.

The invitation: Stay in the room. Engage with critique intact. Push for better while using what exists. Build alternatives while acknowledging current reality.

That's not compromise. That's strategy.

The moment didn't ask permission.

But you still get to choose how you move through it.

Choose agency. Choose critical engagement. Both hands full.

The alternative is watching from the sidelines while others decide your future.

I'm not doing that.

You coming?
