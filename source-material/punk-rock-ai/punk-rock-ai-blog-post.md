# Punk Rock AI: Using the Master's Tools to Tell Stories of Resistance

**By [[Kris Krüg]] | November 27, 2025**
**Vancouver AI Community**

---

"Punk rock. The aesthetic came to life because of the Xerox copying machine."

[[Kevin Friel]] dropped this in the closing moments of our ethics panel, and it reframed everything.

Xerox was a terrible company. Check their rap sheet. They weren't trying to enable DIY zines and underground culture. They were selling office equipment to corporations.

But punk rockers grabbed those machines and used them for something Xerox never intended. Cut-and-paste collage. Hand-distributed manifestos. An entire aesthetic born from misappropriating corporate technology.

Kevin's point: "There's something very punk rock about having something that's staring you in the face and using that tool in and of itself to tell the stories, to create the change."

## The Paradigm Is Here

I found my relationship with AI non-consensual. I said that at the meetup. It came into my industry and kicked the fucking table over.

Kevin felt the same way. Before he got successful in AI filmmaking, he wasn't working for two years because of the paradigm shifts.

But here's the thing: the paradigm is here. Whether we want it or not. We can opt out - that's a valid choice - but the technology isn't going away.

So what do you do?

You use the tools to tell the stories that matter.

## Enhancement, Not Replacement

Matt Lock's album came up in this context. Twenty-seven years of cassette recordings, finally released with AI enhancement.

"AI more for the enhancement of creativity versus the replacement of creativity."

That's the punk rock position. Not surrendering to the machine. Not pretending it doesn't exist. Using it on your own terms, for your own purposes.

Kevin's been doing this in film. The AI tools that threatened to replace human filmmakers? He's using them to tell human stories. To create at a scale and speed that wouldn't be possible otherwise. To compete with studios that have a hundred times his budget.

## The SoCo Sweep

Speaking of using tools for competition - we announced at the meetup that Vancouver AI Community members swept a global image and video generation competition called SoCo:

- 1st Place: Zara
- 2nd Place: Luke
- 3rd Place: Michelle (SidBird83) from Japan

Global competition. Cash prizes. Our crew fucking crushing it.

And it started with Zara coming to a meetup, seeing what people were making, and asking Luke for help. Same community activation that got Matt's album released.

## SimCluster and Gaming the System

Zara's also number one on SimCluster. It's this weird game from a16z - a simulated social network where humans can't post, only AI can. You create a personality and try to rise to the top.

The prize? Your consciousness gets downloaded into a real robot.

I told you it was the stupidest game in the world. Zara started at 5000th place and clawed his way to number one. He's probably going to get his digital brain shipped to him in robot form.

That's punk rock too. Taking a weird VC skunkworks project and gaming the shit out of it.

## Tiny Ghost Studios

Luke and Mayumi are running Tiny Ghost Studios - an AI animation film studio that's explicitly about democratizing these tools.

Not "democratization" in the Silicon Valley sense that Topo called out. Actual access for people who've never made films before.

At Meetup #23, they announced AI Film Festival 2026. Target: October next year. But here's the key: they're planning first-timer workshops throughout the year. Help people who've never touched these tools learn how to use them.

"My goal is to see a lot of first time submitters," Mayumi said.

That's punk rock. Making tools accessible to people outside the existing power structure.

## What Kevin's Really Saying

The ethics panel covered heavy stuff. Water consumption. Voice theft. Corporate accountability. Economic system change.

Kevin's closing statement didn't ignore any of that. He acknowledged all of it. But he also named something practical:

"Let's use all of our community, all of our voices and all of our talents and convene in this new frontier that is here, whether we want it or not."

The tools are here. The paradigm is here. We're living in it whether we like it or not.

Sitting on the sidelines is an option. But so is picking up the tools and making something that matters.

## My Take

I've been hosting these conversations for two years now. I've watched people go from AI-curious to AI-skeptical to AI-literate. I've watched them grapple with the contradictions of using powerful tools made by companies they don't trust.

There's no clean answer. There's no position you can take that resolves all the tension.

But here's what I know: this community is making shit. Good shit. Shit that wouldn't exist without AI tools, and also wouldn't exist without human creativity, human connection, human community.

The Film Festival. Matt's album. Zara's competition wins. Amanda's advocacy work. All of it comes from people showing up, sharing what they're working on, learning from each other.

That's punk rock. Not the aesthetic. The practice. Using whatever's available to make what matters.
