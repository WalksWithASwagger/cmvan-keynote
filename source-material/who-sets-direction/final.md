# Who Sets the Direction Now? How a Grassroots Community Became Essential Stakeholders in 20 Months

**By Kris Krüg**
**Published:** January 2026
**Reading Time:** 9 minutes

---

October 2025. Office Hours. Loki unmutes.

He's just back from All-In Montreal—Scale AI's big conference, 6,000 people.

"Minister Solomon announced a 30-day AI strategy sprint," he says. "There's a task force, 25-29 members, who have 30 days to revise the pan-Canadian AI strategy. Good luck with that."

I'm listening. Thinking: Wait. Who's on this task force? Who's shaping Canada's AI future?

Loki continues: "Three BC representatives: First Nations Tech Council, Gail Murphy from UBC, Diane Gutiv from CGI."

I notice: All women. Three voices from BC at the table where Canada's AI direction gets set.

Then Loki drops the line that hits me:

"BC was the biggest delegation. It made an impression. Minister Solomon toured all over the place, but he made a point of coming to the Team BC booth."

Here's what matters: BC + AI didn't need to lobby to be part of this conversation. We were already essential stakeholders they had to consult.

Not because we're the richest. Not because we're the biggest.

But because over 20 months, we built something undeniable.

## The Question Everyone's Asking

Who gets to shape AI's future?

Three forces are fighting for control:

**Force #1: Markets.** Silicon Valley. Tech companies with billions in capital and compute infrastructure. They shape AI by building it. ChatGPT drops, world changes overnight. No consultation. No democratic process. Just: Here's the technology. Figure it out.

**Force #2: Policy.** Governments trying to regulate something moving faster than they can understand. Slow. Bureaucratic. The EU AI Act took years to develop and was obsolete by the time it passed. Canada's 30-day sprint? Ambitious. But 30 days to revise national AI strategy? As Loki said: "Good luck with that."

**Force #3: Culture.** Communities. Grassroots organizing. Values-first development. Building alternatives that neither markets nor policy imagined.

The question: Which force wins?

The answer: Whichever one shows up most effectively.

And I can show you exactly how that works.

## From Meetups to Political Power (The 20-Month Journey)

December 2023. I host Meetup #1 at the Space Center. Maybe 80 people show up.

Those first 20 monthly meetups weren't just programming. They were community development infrastructure. Each event built trust, established values, tested formats, refined messaging, and created the relationships that made nonprofit organizing possible.

By August 2024, we'd done 20 consecutive months. That's when we launched the BC + AI Ecosystem Association.

34 founding members. The nonprofit as political strategy.

One month later: 130+ paying members.

No VC funding. No investor pitch decks. Just: "Join us. Build something better together."

And 130+ people across BC said yes.

October 2025: Minister Solomon announces the 30-day AI strategy sprint. BC + AI has a seat at that table.

The progression: Cultural organizing (20 meetups) → Economic power (130+ members) → Political influence (government consultation).

Twenty months. Zero to essential stakeholder.

## Why Association Structure Matters

Think about legitimacy.

"We're a startup" → Government says: "That's nice. Good luck."

"We represent 130+ members across BC" → Government says: "We should probably talk to you."

Associations have political legitimacy corporations don't. We're not lobbyists pushing an agenda. We're stakeholders representing constituents.

When government develops AI policy for BC, they need to consult the actual humans doing AI work in BC.

That's us.

The power: We didn't ask to be included. We became essential by building something government couldn't ignore.

## The Creative Mornings Lesson (Relational Power Building)

October 3, 2025. Same Office Hours call. I'm telling the community about Creative Mornings.

Mark Busussy runs it. 14 years. Monthly creative meetups. 200+ people every month.

Standard sponsorship: $1,500.

Mark's pitch: "You can pay as little as you want, as low as 100 bucks."

I'm thinking: "Bro, I don't think the first thing our association should spend money on is a big sponsorship."

But Mark sees something I don't fully see yet.

We gave him $200 to buy breakfast. Just between us on the call, you know, he counted our $200 as a $1,500 sponsorship and made us a full partner.

Result: Booth space. Speaking slots—me plus Kevin Friel. Printed postcards with our names on them. 200 creative professionals reached.

The math doesn't make sense in transactional capitalism.

But in relational power building, it's perfect.

My exact words from that call: "You got to give more than you take in order for these scenarios to work."

Not transactional. Relational.

That $200 bought us something VC millions can't buy: Credibility with 200 creatives who now see BC + AI as aligned with their values. Some will join. Some will collaborate. Some will advocate for us when policy questions come up.

That's political capital.

## The Anti-Keynote That Became a Movement

August 2024. Meetup #20. Space Center. 250 people.

That night, I was supposed to give a keynote. I canceled it.

Instead of one person talking at 250 people, I created space for Jos Duncan's transformative love-and-justice framework, Kevin Friel's SIGGRAPH connections, the hackathon winners' breakthrough creativity, and genuine community announcements.

I call it "sampling little bits" instead of delivering a traditional keynote.

Why cancel my own keynote?

Because the point wasn't me talking AT you. The point was us building together.

By canceling the keynote, I was modeling the collaborative leadership approach that makes our community sustainable. No single person—including me—should dominate the conversation or define the community's direction.

That night, we launched the BC + AI Ecosystem Association.

The keynote I never gave became the structure we built instead.

## The Container Philosophy

At the launch, I said something that might be the most important thing I said all night, even though it was buried in community announcements:

"We are building a container where you can have challenging different ideas and everyone feels good at the end."

This describes what makes our approach to AI governance unique and replicable.

Most AI discussions force binary choices. You're either pro-AI (booster) or anti-AI (doomer). Pro-market or pro-regulation. Pro-innovation or pro-safety.

We refuse that binary.

We hold critique in one hand and curiosity in the other. We walk forward with both.

Quote from our values statement at launch (talking with Jos Duncan):

"We're values and heart aligned... we're stoked about all these opportunities and possibilities and we have concerns and questions over here and we're walking forward with both those things in our hands at the same time."

That's the sophistication markets and policy don't have. Both can hold complexity. They can navigate tensions without resolving them. They can move forward while maintaining critique.

## The Governance Question

Here's what I keep coming back to:

If all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out—governance gets made by opportunists and true believers. That's worse.

Real example: Early AI ethics boards? Mostly AI optimists.

Why? Critics refused to participate. "I won't legitimize something I oppose."

Result: Weak ethics frameworks. No teeth.

The people who understood risks best weren't in the room.

My argument: If you see problems others don't, your responsibility is to stay in the room.

Not to validate bad systems. To push for better ones.

That's critical engagement. Different from complicity.

And that's what Force #3 (culture/community) offers that the other two forces don't: Values-driven participation without capture by either markets or bureaucracy.

## The Independent Media Center Model

This isn't the first time I've done this.

2010 Olympics. Vancouver. I played this really interesting role. I both worked for VANOC, the organizing committee, and was helping organize the protests against the Olympics.

Most insider outsider person of the whole situation.

As part of that, I built the True North Media House. 1,000-person self-accrediting independent media center for bloggers and citizen journalists. Took over a four-story building. It was incredible.

Also did this at COP15 in Copenhagen—got arrested in the protests. Did it at the 2008 Democratic National Convention—photographed Barack Obama and Bill Clinton.

Three times, all over the world. Same model:

When a hundred of you buy a membership and wear a badge that says you're a part of something, all of a sudden the badge becomes meaningful by the fact that there's a hundred other passionate impactful people wearing it.

We legitimized ourselves through collaboration and declaration and contribution.

Didn't ask permission. Just showed up. Put our good foot forward.

You got to give more than you take in order for these scenarios to work.

That's the same model we used for BC + AI.

## Provincial Replication (The Horizontal Distribution Strategy)

We didn't scale Vancouver AI to 10,000 members.

We built it to critical mass and helped others replicate.

Vancouver AI → Surrey AI → Victoria, Kelowna, Comox.

Each one strengthens the provincial network.

From my self-analysis of the launch:

"Surrey AI, Squamish AI, Mind AI & Consciousness, AI & Education, AI & Heavy Industry—these subgroups emerged organically because we created conditions for people to find each other and self-organize around shared interests."

The pattern: One big thing that collapses is less than twenty sustainable things that last.

Impact math:
- Model 1 (Scale Fast): 10,000 people × 5 years = 50,000 person-years, then collapse
- Model 2 (Build Deep, Replicate): 500 people × 20 regions × 20 years = 200,000 person-years, sustainable

Horizontal distribution over vertical scale.

That's not just organizational philosophy. It's political strategy.

You can't silence a distributed network by removing one node. You can't co-opt it by buying the center. Each region strengthens the whole.

## The Showing Up Checklist (How We Did It)

Based on 20 months of building BC + AI from zero to essential stakeholder, here's the framework:

**Step 1: Build alternative model.** Don't just critique existing systems. We didn't just complain about extractive VC models. We built community-owned association proving different model works.

**Step 2: Prove it works.** Evidence over theory. We went from 0 to 130+ paying members without VC. Self-sustaining in 4 months. Replication starting organically.

**Step 3: Document openly.** Surrey AI learned from Vancouver AI's playbook. Office Hours transcripts published. Framework shared. No proprietary model—it's ALL replicable.

**Step 4: Organize collective voice.** Association structure, not just informal community. 130+ members means we represent constituents, not just ourselves.

**Step 5: Show up to governance moments.** 30-day AI strategy sprint? We responded. Public consultations? We filled out the surveys. Advisory councils? We made ourselves available.

The result: When government needed to understand what's happening in BC's AI ecosystem, we were the obvious people to ask.

That's influence.

BC + AI followed it. Twenty months from zero to government consultation.
