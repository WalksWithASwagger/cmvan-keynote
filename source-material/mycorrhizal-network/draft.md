# The Mycorrhizal Network Model: Why We're Building Horizontal Distribution, Not Vertical Scale

Beneath every forest: Mycorrhizal networks.

Fungi connecting tree roots. Nutrient exchange between unlike species.

The pattern:
- Nurse logs (fallen trees) feed saplings through fungal network
- Birch supports fir during drought
- Network communicates: "Predator here" signals travel underground
- Diversity creates resilience (monoculture forests collapse)

What makes it work: Not same species clustering. But unlike species supporting each other.

The exchange:

Trees provide sugars (photosynthesis). Fungi provide minerals (soil access).

Neither could thrive alone. Together: Forest ecosystem.

When I started building BC + AI, everyone asked: "What's your growth target? 1,000 members? 10,000?"

My answer confused them:

"I don't want 10,000 members who never talk to each other. I want 130 committed people connecting unlike domains. Small but mighty."

The mycorrhizal model: Not vertical scale (biggest AI community). But horizontal distribution (most connected AI community).

Why: Network value equals connections between nodes, not number of nodes.

The math:

1,000 disconnected people equals zero valuable connections.

130 deeply connected people equals exponential unexpected collaborations.

James (creator-audience data) meets Zaro (FIFA community gaming): More valuable than 200 random new members.

Why: They're solving same problem from different angles. That connection creates innovation nobody predicted.

This article is about why we're building mycorrhizal networks, not unicorns.

## The Silicon Valley Model (What I'm Not Building)

The unicorn framework:

Assumptions:
- Bigger is better
- Winner takes all
- Network effects consolidate power
- Scale through homogeneity (same user type, same needs)
- Value through monopoly

The strategy:
1. Raise massive capital
2. Grow as fast as possible
3. Dominate market
4. Extract value through market control

Examples:

Facebook: Billions of users. Most don't know each other. Value from advertising.

LinkedIn: Professional network. Value from premium subscriptions.

Twitter/X: Mass broadcasting. Value from attention capture.

What they optimize for: User count. Engagement metrics. Ad revenue.

What they don't optimize for: Meaningful connections between unlike people.

The result: Massive. Powerful. Extractive. Fragile.

Why fragile:

Dependence on:
- Continued VC funding
- Advertising revenue
- Network effects holding
- No real alternative

When market shifts or funding dries up: Collapse potential is real.

The critique: I'm not against scale. I'm against scale for scale's sake. Growth through extraction. Homogeneous networks where everyone's the same. Power consolidation at top.

What I'm building instead: Small. Diverse. Distributed. Resilient.

The mycorrhizal model.

## The Mycorrhizal Principles Applied to Community

**Principle 1: Diversity Creates Resilience**

Forest: Monoculture vulnerable to disease. Diversity survives.

Community: Homogeneous network vulnerable to market shifts. Diversity survives.

BC + AI members:
- Biotech researchers (Simon)
- Film creators (Kevin Friel)
- Educators (Emily, Antonia)
- Indigenous data governance (Chris, Caroline)
- Real estate developers
- Sales professionals (Shane, Bruce)
- Journalists (Katherine)
- Lawyers (Lawrence)

When AI disrupts one domain, other domains aren't affected. Network survives.

The resilience: If we were only biotech, industry collapse equals community collapse.

Because we're 8+ domains: One domain's crisis equals other domains' stability.

I've watched this play out. When journalism started getting hit hard by AI, the journalists in our community had sales professionals to talk to about content multiplication. Educators to collaborate with on media literacy. Film creators to learn from about visual storytelling.

They didn't face the crisis alone. The network caught them.

**Principle 2: Unlike Species Support Each Other**

Forest: Birch supports fir. Fir supports cedar. Not competition—collaboration.

Community: James (creator tools) supports Zaro (gaming communities). Neither competes.

The unexpected connections:

Simon (biotech) plus Chris (Indigenous data sovereignty) equals Life Sciences AI subgroup with data governance principles nobody saw coming.

Kevin (film) plus Antonia (education) equals Film Club as educational tool.

Shane (sales) plus Katherine (journalism) equals content multiplication frameworks applied to newsrooms.

Each connection creates value neither saw coming.

Office Hours, October 24, 2025:

James joins for first time. Describes Appreciation Engine—creator-audience consent-based data platform.

Within 5 minutes, I'm saying: "You need to meet Zaro."

Zaro: FIFA gaming, community management, same consent-based model for gaming communities.

30 minutes later, they're collaborating on frameworks. Both level up. Potential product integration discussed.

That's the pattern. Unlike people solving similar problems from different angles. The collision creates insight.

**Principle 3: Nutrient Exchange (Not Extraction)**

Forest: Trees give sugars. Fungi give minerals. Reciprocal.

Community: Members give expertise. Receive connections, opportunities, learning.

What this looks like:

Kevin shares video generation experiments. Others learn. They share their discoveries. Kevin benefits from their insights.

Reciprocal flow. Not one-directional extraction.

When Kevin's startup wound down, community offered consulting gigs, project collaborations, introductions.

He'd been giving for a year. Network reciprocated.

That's how healthy networks work. Give before you ask. When you need help, the network remembers.

**Principle 4: Nurse Logs (Supporting New Growth)**

Forest: Fallen trees become nurse logs. Feed saplings through fungal network.

Community: Experienced members support newcomers.

Examples:
- Peter mentoring students every cohort
- Indigenous scholarships I fund (don't broadcast, just do)
- Experienced members running office hours
- Knowledge shared freely, not hoarded

The investment in new growth strengthens whole network.

When new members join, they're not starting from zero. They're plugging into existing relationships, accumulated knowledge, established patterns.

**Principle 5: Communication Through Network**

Forest: "Predator here" signals travel through mycelium. Trees respond collectively.

Community: Discord, office hours, events equal communication infrastructure.

When someone needs help, signal travels. Network responds.

Examples:

Armin needs customers. Network introduces.

Kevin needs pivot support. Network offers opportunities.

Lawrence needs research collaborators. Network connects.

The response is distributed, not centralized. I'm not the only one helping. The whole network is.

That's resilience. If I disappeared tomorrow, the network would keep functioning. Because the connections are peer-to-peer, not hub-and-spoke.

## Why Small + Connected Beats Big + Scattered

The network value formula:

Wrong: Value equals number of members.

Right: Value equals quality of connections between members.

The math:

Scenario A: 1,000 members, low connection density.
- Most people don't know each other
- Few meaningful collaborations
- Value equals individual benefits (access to events, content)
- Total value: 1,000 times individual value equals linear growth

Scenario B: 130 members, high connection density.
- Most people know each other
- Frequent unexpected collaborations
- Value equals individual benefits plus connection value plus collaboration value
- Total value: 130 times (individual plus connection) equals exponential growth

Which is actually bigger?

Real example: James and Zaro connection created more value than 200 random new members would have.

Why: They're both leveling up. Potential product collaboration. Shared framework development. Ongoing relationship.

That's exponential. Not linear.

Large AI communities (10,000+ members):
- Discord channels with hundreds of messages per day
- Nobody reading deeply
- No meaningful connections
- Just broadcasting into void

What's the value?

Small community advantage: 130 people means you can actually know them.

I've had 1-on-1 conversations with 80%+ of members.

I know:
- Who's working on what
- Who needs to meet whom
- Where unexpected synergies are

That pattern-matching only works at small scale.

The strategy: Don't optimize for size. Optimize for connection density.

When we hit 500 members: Will I still know 80%? Probably not.

Will I make sure they know each other? Yes.

Structure for connection, not just access.

## How To Actually Build This

The infrastructure required:

**1. Shared Values (The Filter)**

Not: "Everyone welcome!"

But: "People who share these values welcome."

BC + AI values:
- Indigenous leadership integration
- Education-first approach
- Ethics-centered practice
- Community over competition

Effect: Self-selection. Right people join. Wrong people don't.

This isn't exclusionary. It's values-aligned. We're building something specific. You either resonate with it or you don't.

**2. Connection Facilitation (The Active Work)**

Not: "Here's a Discord, talk among yourselves."

But: "James, meet Zaro. You're solving the same problem."

Active pattern-matching.

I spend hours every week thinking about who should meet whom. That's not scalable past a certain point. But it's essential at this stage.

**3. Cross-Domain Events**

Not: "Biotech meetup" and "Film meetup" separately.

But: Everyone at same events. Unlike domains mix.

Vancouver AI: Life sciences people meet film people meet educators.

Result: Unexpected combinations. Kevin (VFX) talking to Simon (biotech) about visualization of complex data. Neither would have found each other otherwise.

**4. Small Enough To Know Each Other**

Not: Scale beyond relationship capacity.

But: Grow at pace where connections can still form.

The limit: When I can't have 1-on-1 conversations with majority of members, we need different structure.

Maybe regional chapters. Each 100-200 people. Still small enough for connection density.

**5. Give Before Ask (Reciprocal Culture)**

Not: "What can I extract?"

But: "What can I contribute?"

Members who give: Get network support when needed (Kevin's pivot).

Members who only take: Don't get same reciprocity.

Self-regulating. The culture enforces itself.
