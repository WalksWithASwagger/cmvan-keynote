# HOW TO KEEP OUR SOULS INTACT WHEN THE MACHINES GET REALLY GOOD AT MAKING EVERYTHING

---

## PROLOGUE: CINEMA NOVO (0:00 – 2:00)

**[NO SLIDES. House lights still warm. Walk to center stage. No podium. Take a breath. Look at the room.]**

São Paulo. On the road to Cannes.

A festival built around the question every filmmaker in this room is already living: what happens when AI enters the frame?

**[Pause. Let the room feel itself.]**

You know who else asked dangerous questions about cinema? Cinema Novo. Glauber Rocha didn't have a playbook. He had a camera and something to say about Brazil. He didn't build a product. He built a movement.

**[Beat.]**

I'm not here to teach you. I'm here to share what I've been figuring out — and honestly, to learn from you.

Because here's the thing. I need to tell you something honest.

---

## ACT 1: LEFT HAND — WHAT'S ACTUALLY HAPPENING (2:00 – 14:00)

**[Walk stage left. Energy shifts. This is where we name what's real.]**

### The Non-Consensual Opening (2:00 – 4:00)

**[SLIDE 1: "I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual."]**

**[Pause. Direct eye contact. Serious tone.]**

I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

**[Let that land. Silence.]**

I said those words to 200 art students in Vancouver last month and the room went quiet.

It didn't ask me if it could steal my work. It didn't ask me if it could change everything around me. All I'm trying to do is keep my head above water and reinvent myself, you know? Just responding to this moment that I didn't ask for.

**[Beat.]**

I released thousands of photos on Flickr over two decades under Creative Commons. Shared them freely — that was the deal. When I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly 1,800 of my images in a major training corpus.

Nobody asked me. They just took it.

**[Turn to audience.]**

If you've ever released a film, posted a photograph, written a script, published anything online — it may already be in training data. You weren't asked. That's not hypothetical. That's what happened.

### Three Fears That Are Real (4:00 – 6:30)

**[SLIDE 2: Three Fears]**

Not hypothetical fears. Real shit happening right now.

**One: Stolen work without consent.**

Training data equals humanity's creative output scraped without permission. Your photos. Your scripts. Your scenes. Used without asking. No compensation. No attribution. Call it what it is.

**[Beat.]**

**Two: Race to the bottom.**

"Good enough" AI outputs devalue human craft. Clients are saying it out loud now: "Can't you just use AI for this?" Translation: "Why should I pay you?"

**[Beat.]**

**Three: Junior pipeline collapse.**

This is the one that keeps me up at night. Entry-level jobs are vanishing. The roles that trained the next generation are disappearing. How do people become directors if they can't be assistants first? How do cinematographers learn their craft if nobody hires camera assistants anymore?

I teach this stuff for a living. And I'm going to be honest with you: I don't have the junior pipeline problem solved. Nobody does. And anyone who stands up here and tells you they've got it figured out? Don't listen to them. They're lying.

### Name What You're Seeing (6:30 – 8:00)

**[SLIDE 3: "STOP SAYING 'BIAS.' NAME WHAT YOU'RE SEEING."]**

**[Direct. No softening.]**

And there's something else we need to talk about. What they built with our stolen work.

We've said the word "bias" so many times it's lost meaning. Algorithmic bias. Training data bias. Gender bias. Generic. Safe. Abstract.

Stop saying bias. Name what you're seeing.

A colleague of mine asked an image generator to create a "marketing professor." She got a man in a power pose — Harvard blazer, authority, gravitas. Then she asked it to make the professor female. She got a timid school teacher. Not the same authority. Not the same context. A diminished version.

**[Beat.]**

It took her multiple rounds of prompting to get a woman with the same authority the system gave a man by default.

That's not bias. That's misogyny embedded in image generation systems. Name it.

**[Direct.]**

When facial recognition fails on darker-skinned women at 40 times the rate it fails on lighter-skinned men — that's not a "performance gap." That's racism and sexism embedded in code. Dr. Joy Buolamwini proved it.

You're filmmakers. You understand this instinctively. What's missing from the frame matters as much as what's in it. Training data works exactly the same way.

I call it bias laundering — discrimination that looks like math.

### The 55% (8:00 – 9:30)

**[SLIDE 4: "55%"]**

Here's a number that changed how I think about all of this.

Gallup data: 55% of US workers have never used AI at all. Only 12% use it daily. Half the workforce is still on the sidelines.

**[Pause.]**

Think about what that means. If all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out of AI — if they decide it's too problematic to touch — governance gets made by opportunists and true believers. The people who understand the risks best aren't in the room when the decisions get made.

**[Direct.]**

That's worse. That's worse than engaging with critique intact.

### The First Protester (9:30 – 10:30)

**[SLIDE 5: "GOOD."]**

I had my first protester at a keynote recently. Back row. Video camera rolling. Took the mic during Q&A. Wouldn't give it back. Made YouTube soundbites.

**[Beat. Then warmth:]**

And here's the thing — she's right to be concerned.

If you're not 20 years old, pissed off, and challenging things, you're not really paying attention to what's going on right now.

Be angry. That anger is legitimate. Channel it.

Just don't let anger be the only thing you hold.

### We're Doing It Backwards (10:30 – 12:00)

**[SLIDE 6: "WE'RE DOING IT BACKWARDS"]**

Here's the part that really gets me. The part that makes me angry.

We're doing it backwards.

**[Let the line land.]**

We're automating art and keeping the drudgery. Think about that for a second. Studios want AI to replace creative work — writing, animation, VFX, scoring — but they still want humans doing spreadsheets and scheduling and data entry or whatever.

That's insane. That's backwards.

The punk rock move is to flip it. Use AI for the boring stuff. Stay stubbornly human for the creative stuff.

**[Pause.]**

Maybe the next evolution isn't about being better machines. It's about being worse machines and better humans.

### Holding the Left Hand (12:00 – 14:00)

**[SLIDE 7: Red hand print — LEFT hand]**

**[Hold up left hand. Physical gesture. Slow.]**

This is what I carry. Stolen work. Devalued craft. Collapsing pipelines. Discrimination laundered through math. A relationship I never consented to.

Don't drop these. They keep you honest.

**[Pause. Then the turn:]**

Now let me show you what's in my right hand.

**[Energy shift. Walk stage right.]**

## ACT 2: RIGHT HAND — WHAT'S ALSO TRUE (14:00 – 28:00)

**[SLIDE 8: "WHAT'S ALSO TRUE" — background inverts from black to white]**

What's also true. Not hype. Not LinkedIn bullshit. Not some tech bro telling you AI is going to solve everything. Just actual transformations I've watched happen with my own eyes.

### Luke Minaker: The 80/20 Flip (14:00 – 18:00)

**[SLIDE 9: Torn movie ticket, film strip, kid's dinosaur drawing]**

You're filmmakers. You know three-act structure better than I do. So let me tell you a story in three acts.

**Act One.**

A kid named Luke. Twelve years old. Sitting in a theater watching Jurassic Park for the first time. He sees those velociraptors come alive and something clicks. He turns to his mom and says: "This is what I want to do. I want to bring monsters to life."

**[Beat.]**

**Act Two.**

Thirty years later. Luke is an animator. But he's not bringing monsters to life. He's making cheap plastic gags to sell cheap plastic toys. Barbie commercials. Corporate animation. Cog in the machine.

The dream from that movie theater? Buried under 30 years of grind.

The ratio: 80% grind, 20% creative decisions. That's what the industry gave him.

**[Pause.]**

**Act Three.**

**[SLIDE 10: "80% GRIND / 20% CREATIVE" crossed out. Below: "80% CREATIVE / 20% GRIND" circled in red]**

AI tools arrive. And the ratio flips.

80% creative. 20% grind.

A year's worth of traditional production — completed in two to three weeks.

Luke's studio, Tiny Ghost, started two years ago with zero revenue. By January 2026, they were fielding calls from household names. Their original IP "Blood and Glitter" just got greenlit and funded.

**[Pause. Lean in.]**

But here's the line that got me. Luke said: "I don't care about the money. I am so excited that I get to do art again."

**[Beat.]**

The 12-year-old who wanted to bring monsters to life? He's back. After 30 years.

**[Let that land.]**

That's not replacement. That's liberation.

### Kevin Friel: The Conductor (18:00 – 21:00)

**[SLIDE 11: Conductor's hands/baton]**

I got this other buddy, Kevin. Twenty-five years in Hollywood visual effects. Dune. Detective Pikachu. Sonic. Like, the big studios, right?

Kevin told our Film Club something that reframed everything for me. He said: "My mum took me to the National Arts Center in Ottawa starting at age six to see the symphonic orchestra play."

**[Beat.]**

And then he made the connection.

"The conductor is still important hundreds of years into classical music. It's the role of knowing enough about all of the parts, but bringing out the best of the sum of the parts."

**[SLIDE 12: "85% AI. CRAFT FINISHES."]**

That's the orchestration era. One agent writes the script. Another generates scenes. A third scores the music. A fourth handles continuity. A conductor orchestrates them all.

Kevin's advice: "Get it 85% of the way there with AI, then use craft to finish."

**[Direct.]**

That last 15% — the human judgment, the aesthetic choices, the "you know what, this scene needs to breathe here" decisions — that's where the art lives.

Taste and judgment built over 25 years of doing the work — that's what makes output art, not noise. The tools don't replace the conductor. They give the conductor a bigger orchestra.

### Belgium: "AI Is My Hope to Continue to Exist" (21:00 – 24:00)

**[SLIDE 13: BLACK. White typewriter text. Clean. No collage. Single red dot — heartbeat.]**

**[Voice drops. Slower pace.]**

At our second Film Club meeting, an actress who goes by Belgium stood up. She'd been bedridden for 18 months. Couldn't work. Couldn't perform. Couldn't do the thing that made her who she is.

During those months, she started making AI avatars. Creating characters. Building stories from her bed.

**[Pause.]**

She said: "AI film is my hope to be able to continue to exist and to tell something."

**[Let the room sit with that.]**

For some people, AI tools are not about getting ahead. Not about efficiency. Not about flipping ratios.

They're about getting to participate at all.

**[Beat.]**

Don't forget that. When the debates get abstract and ideological, remember Belgium. Remember what access actually means to someone who almost lost their voice entirely.

### The Both/And Statement (24:00 – 26:00)

**[SLIDE 14: "AI is trained on stolen work without consent. AND I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been. BOTH STATEMENTS ARE TRUE."]**

**[Center stage. Both hands up. Direct and personal.]**

My personal testimony:

AI is trained on stolen work without consent. And I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been in my whole life.

**[Pause. Let both statements exist simultaneously.]**

Both statements are true.

I can critique the theft AND use the capability. I can resist extraction AND build with the tools. I can be pissed about non-consent AND leverage the moment.

**[Pause.]**

Not contradiction. Complexity.

### Holding the Right Hand (26:00 – 28:00)

**[SLIDE 15: Red hand print — RIGHT hand]**

**[Hold up right hand.]**

Don't drop this either. Don't drop it for ideological purity. Don't drop it because the critique is easier to hold.

The transformation is real. People's lives are changing. Luke got his art back. Kevin found a new way to conduct. Belgium found a way to exist.

**[Beat.]**

The capability explosion is happening. You can pretend it's not. Or you can engage with it critically.

## ACT 3: THE MIRROR (28:00 – 42:00)

**[Move center. Both hands visible. Then lower them.]**

Okay. So you've got both hands full. Now what? What do you actually do with this?

Here's the insight that changed everything for me. And honestly, it's the thing nobody talks about at AI conferences. They're too busy demoing features or whatever.

### AI Is A Mirror (28:00 – 30:00)

**[SLIDE 16: Mirror with distorted reflection]**

AI is a mirror.

It amplifies whatever you bring to it.

If you don't know who you are, what comes out? Confusion. Generic slop. The same flavorless content everyone else is generating. We've all seen it. You know it when you see it.

But clear identity in? Something powerful. Something that sounds like you. Something only you could have made.

**[Pause.]**

Self-knowledge is the prerequisite to AI mastery.

If you don't know who you are as a filmmaker, AI can't help you. It'll just give you noise. But if you know your aesthetic, your themes, your perspective — if you know what you'd choose and why — AI becomes the most powerful amplifier you've ever touched.

**[Direct to audience.]**

The work you're doing right now — studying cinema, developing taste, finding your voice, arguing about films at two in the morning with your friends — that IS the AI preparation. You just didn't know it yet. That's the stuff that matters.

### Become a Selector (30:00 – 33:00)

**[SLIDE 17: Vinyl record, gig flyers, setlists]**

Twenty years ago, a Canadian filmmaker named Brett Gaylor made a documentary called *RiP!: A Remix Manifesto*. The premise:

*"Culture always builds on the past. The past always tries to control the future."*

Sound familiar?

Here we are again. All our creative works slurped up into AI. Trained without consent. For profit.

But DJs navigated this exact tension. When sampling exploded, copyright battles raged. The artists who thrived weren't the ones who resisted sampling completely, and they weren't the ones who just pressed play on someone else's track. They were the ones who *selected*. Who curated. Who brought taste to abundance.

DJs were originally called **selectors**.

**[Pause.]**

That's your future role. Generation is cheap. Taste is not.

**[SLIDE 18: "GENERATION IS CHEAP. TASTE IS NOT."]**

AI can generate a thousand variations of your scene. You're the one who knows which one has soul. That judgment — developed through years of watching films, studying Cinema Novo, experiencing art, living life in São Paulo or Recife or Brasília — that's what can't be automated.

Brazil knows this already. Tropicalia was selection. Bossa nova was selection. Taking existing forms — American jazz, Portuguese guitar — and filtering them through specifically Brazilian sensibility until what came out couldn't have been made anywhere else.

That's what you do with AI. Filter it through your specifically Brazilian creative perspective. What comes out will be yours.

**[Stamp: YOUR TASTE IS YOUR MOAT]**

### Write for the Bot (33:00 – 37:00)

**[SLIDE 19: Typewriter with paper emerging]**

Okay, this next one is wild. An anonymous researcher at one of the big AI labs — like, this guy disguises his identity because he works at OpenAI — said something that really shook me. Let me read you the key part:

*"If there are values you have which are not expressed yet in text, and if there are things that you like or want, if they aren't reflected online, then to the AI, they basically don't exist. And that is dangerously close to won't exist."*

**[Let that sink in.]**

If favela stories, indigenous perspectives, São Paulo street culture, your grandmother's way of seeing the world — if those things aren't written, recorded, documented — they effectively don't exist to the AI. And what doesn't exist to the AI doesn't shape the future it's building.

Right now, the training data is mostly American, mostly corporate, mostly English, mostly bland.

Writing for the bot isn't just self-preservation. In this room, it's cultural activism.

Record your ideas. Transcribe your conversations. Build knowledge bases of your thinking. Make your values machine-readable.

**[SLIDE 20: "IF YOUR VALUES AREN'T IN TEXT, TO AI THEY DON'T EXIST. DANGEROUSLY CLOSE TO WON'T EXIST."]**

**Your homework tonight:** Write three sentences about what you believe as a filmmaker. What stories you think need telling and why. That's not a small thing. That's the beginning of a creative identity that AI can amplify instead of dilute.

### Ship Culture, Not Content (37:00 – 40:00)

**[SLIDE 21: Factory vs Garden — split panel]**

Okay, last framework before we go to the soul stuff. And this is the one for São Paulo. This is the one I've been thinking about most.

Products commoditize. Culture compounds. Community outlasts everything.

Stop thinking like a factory — predictable output, controlled production, extract value, move on.

Start thinking like a garden — a living system that requires tending, care, and produces things you didn't expect.

If you're grinding out "content," AI will eventually outgrind you. But if you're building a body of work, a community, a creative ecosystem — that's something a machine can't replicate. That's Cinema Novo. That's what this festival is building right now by connecting Brazil, France, and Canada.

**[Pause.]**

I heard something recently that I can't stop thinking about. Someone way smarter than me said: *"Data is relation, not resource."*

Your creative archive isn't a database. It's a biome. Your old student films aren't failures — they're soil for what grows next.

Don't let your life become corporate fertilizer. Compost your own work. Remix your own ideas. Grow something that only you could grow.

### The Brazilian Opportunity (40:00 – 42:00)

**[SLIDE 22: "YOUR PERSPECTIVE IS TRAINING DATA NO MODEL CONTAINS."]**

**[Warmer energy. Pride in voice.]**

Cinema Novo was ecosystem building before anyone called it that. Glauber Rocha didn't just make films. He built the conditions for a generation of cinema. Critics, musicians, writers, filmmakers — all creating a cultural shift together. Not a product. A movement.

You're part of that tradition.

**[Beat.]**

AI tools lower barriers. International platforms want Brazilian stories. The infrastructure is here. And now the tools of production are radically democratized.

**[Direct.]**

Parasite worked because Bong Joon-ho made a deeply Korean film. He didn't try to be universal by removing Korean specificity. The specificity WAS the universality.

Kleber Mendonça Filho builds from Recife, not Hollywood. Anna Muylaert tells São Paulo stories. Karim Aïnouz combines Ceará roots with international reach.

The more specific, the more universal. Don't copy Hollywood. Build from Brazilian culture.

**[Lean in.]**

Your perspective is training data no model contains.

## ACT 4: THE SOUL (42:00 – 48:00)

**[Quieter now. Slower. More intimate.]**

### Creative DNA (42:00 – 45:00)

**[SLIDE 23: BLACK. White typewriter. "CLOSE YOUR EYES."]**

So. The title. Let's talk about the title.

How to keep our souls intact. What does that even mean?

The list of what makes us uniquely human is getting shorter. Laughing. Crying. Kissing. Getting lost in the woods. Dancing until your atoms rearrange. Tasting food. Holding someone's hand. The smell of this city at night.

Here's something that guy at OpenAI said that stuck with me: the AI can't eat ice cream for you. Right? It can't decide which flavor you like. Only you can determine your preferences.

When the machines handle the calendaring, the accounting, the communications, the editing, the color grading, the storyboarding — what actually remains?

Embodied experience. Desires and hopes. Empathy and intuition. The things you refuse to automate.

Those are the stubbornly human things. That's where your soul lives.

**[Pause.]**

I want to try something with you. If you're comfortable. Don't worry, it's not weird.

Close your eyes for a moment.

**[Wait for the room to settle. 600 people closing their eyes takes a beat.]**

Think about one thing you do in your creative work — as a filmmaker, as a storyteller, as a student of cinema — that you would never want to give up to AI.

Not because you couldn't automate it. Maybe you could. But because it's the part that makes the work yours.

The last thing you'd hold onto.

**[Silence. Give them 20 seconds. Don't rush this.]**

Open your eyes.

**[Pause.]**

That thing you were thinking of? That's your creative DNA. That's what the machines can't generate. That's what this whole talk has been about, really.

Everything else is negotiable. You can let AI help with everything else. That part? That part isn't negotiable.

### Stay In The Room (45:00 – 47:00)

**[SLIDE 24: Empty chair at table. Red border. "STAY IN THE ROOM"]**

**[Energy back up. Direct.]**

One more thing before we close. And this is the uncomfortable part. This is where I might piss some people off.

The most skeptical people about AI — the ones who see manipulation in every generated image, who understand how narrative shapes perception, who know what it means to tell stories with integrity — I need you to engage the most.

Because if all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out of AI — if the filmmakers who understand these things best refuse to participate — then governance gets made by opportunists and true believers. By people who don't see the problems.

**[Direct.]**

That's worse.

If you see problems others don't, your responsibility is to stay in the room. Not to validate bad systems. To push for better ones.

In Brazil, in an election year, with deepfakes and disinformation on every feed — the people who understand how images lie are the people who need to be building the next thing. Not the ones sitting it out.

**[SLIDE 25: Two hands up. "BOTH HANDS FULL. YOU COMING?"]**

**[Center stage. Both hands up.]**

Three options.

**[Hold up left hand.]**

One: Embrace uncritically. Become a booster. Ignore the harms. Feel clean about your enthusiasm.

**[Hold up right hand.]**

Two: Resist completely. Become a doomer. Maintain purity. Feel clean about your principles.

**[Both hands up.]**

Three: Walk forward with both hands full.

**[Pause.]**

**[Left hand up.]**

This is non-consensual. This is unjust. This is extractive. I'm not okay with it.

**[Right hand up.]**

This is transformative. This creates capability. This is happening. I want agency in shaping it.

**[Both hands.]**

Both. Simultaneously. While moving forward.

**[Pause.]**

Brazilian cinema has always held complexity. Cinema Novo wasn't simple. Tropicalia wasn't simple. Your tradition is building art that holds paradox — political AND beautiful, critical AND generous, rooted AND universal.

This moment asks the same thing. Hold the paradox. Keep making.

Your generation gets to define what Brazilian cinema looks like when the machines can make everything. Don't let someone in California answer that question for you.

**[Final beat. Energy builds.]**

You are 600 people at the first edition of a festival bridging Brazil, France, and Canada. You are not attendees. You are founding members. What you build here shapes AI filmmaking for your generation.

Make things. Not one perfect film — many experiments.

Find five people. Start a group chat. Watch each other's work. Give real feedback.

Hold critique in one hand. Hold curiosity in the other. Keep walking.

Feed your soul, not the machine.

**[Direct eye contact across the room.]**

I'm here. Both hands full. Walking forward.

**[Beat.]**

You coming?

Obrigado.
