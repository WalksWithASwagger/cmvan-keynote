### Option 1A: Creative Rebellion in an Age of Synthetic Everything

**Subtitle:** Why the soul of your work isn't something AI can generate

**Abstract (140 words):**
AI can now create video, music, scripts, even entire film concepts. So what's left for you?

Not a strategy talk. Not a tools demo. This is an intervention.

The choice they're selling you — embrace AI and lose your soul, or reject AI and become irrelevant — is a lie. There's a third path. It's more punk rock than either option.

Drawing from two years building Canada's largest grassroots AI community (850+ members), and decades as a photographer who watched darkrooms become algorithms, Kris shares what he's learned: You don't compete with machines. You build creative ecosystems that require care, attention, and human vision over time. You rebel — not against technology, but against its soulless application.

The question isn't whether AI will change filmmaking. It already has. The question is: what kind of filmmaker will you become?

**1-line blurb:** An intervention on keeping your soul when machines can generate anything.

Everything that matters.

Twenty-seven years of pointing cameras at people taught me one thing: technology changes, but the human elements — taste, intention, lived experience, community — don't get automated. They get amplified. 

From Vancouver's AI Film Club to Cinema Novo's movement-building, the pattern holds: The filmmakers who thrive don't compete with machines. They create conditions where their vision, relationships, and cultural knowledge become irreplaceable.

Your generation gets to answer the question Hollywood won't: What does cinema look like when anyone can generate anything? 

This is how you start building the answer.

**1-line blurb:** What remains irreplaceable when machines can generate anything — and how to build from there.

### Option 1D: Don't Make Content. Build Culture.

**Subtitle:** Why ecosystems outlast algorithms

**Abstract (135 words):**
Streaming platforms cancel shows after one season based on metrics. Studios chase trends instead of developing voices. "Content creation" is replacing storytelling.

Meanwhile: Cinema Novo built a movement, not products. Agnès Varda made films for sixty years, each informing the next. Vancouver's AI Film Club grew from five panicked photographers to hundreds of creators sharing what works.

The difference? Ecosystems versus extraction.

This keynote is about shifting from product thinking (make it, launch it, move on) to ecosystem thinking (build conditions where your community and craft sustain each other for decades).

AI makes this both urgent and possible. Urgent because content is now commodity. Possible because the same tools let you build global community from São Paulo while staying rooted in Brazilian stories.

Don't make content. Build culture.

**1-line blurb:** Shifting from product thinking to ecosystem building — why community outlasts the algorithm.

### Option 1E: Cinema After the Flood

**Subtitle:** Making movies when the machines learned to dream

**Abstract (140 words):**
November 2022. A flood hit. Machines learned to dream — to generate images from text, then video, then entire worlds. Every creative industry woke up underwater.

What now?

Some filmmakers are building arks. Gatekeeping. Resisting. Others are drowning — outsourcing vision to prompts, generating slop, racing to the bottom.

There's a third response. Not ark-building or drowning. Growing gills. Adapting without abandoning what made cinema matter in the first place.

From the favelas of City of God to the streets of Roma, from the experimental edges of Vancouver's AI Film Club to Runway's creator community — filmmakers are discovering that human creativity isn't threatened by synthetic media. It's clarified by it.

This is a talk about what remains essential when everything else becomes generative. And how to build a practice — a community — around what can't be automated.

**1-line blurb:** Growing gills, not building arks — adapting without abandoning what makes cinema human.

### Option 2A: AI That Thinks & Creates Like You

**Subtitle:** Training assistants that amplify your voice, not replace it

**Abstract (120 words):**
What if your AI tools actually understood your creative vision?

Not generic ChatGPT. Not one-size-fits-all prompts. An assistant trained on your aesthetic preferences, your decision-making patterns, your specific way of seeing the world.

This is how you move from "user" to "collaborator."

In twenty minutes, we'll explore: How to build a corpus of your creative DNA. How to train assistants that reflect your voice. How to create what you actually need instead of accepting what generic AI offers.

This isn't about fancy prompts. It's about the question AI forces you to answer: What is your creative perspective, exactly? And once you know — how do you make the machines work for that?

**1-line blurb:** Training AI assistants that amplify your creative voice instead of replacing it.

This small-group session explores what taste actually means in the age of infinite generation. How to develop it. How to articulate it. How to encode it into tools that serve your vision.

We'll look at real examples from film, photography, and music — and discuss how you're already developing the curatorial instincts that separate signal from slop.

Your taste is the last moat. Here's how to build it deeper.

**1-line blurb:** Developing the curatorial instincts that separate signal from slop in an age of infinite generation.

### Option 2C: Building Your Second Brain for AI Collaboration

I'll share how 200 hours of coaching transcripts revealed patterns I didn't know I had — and how the same approach works for filmmakers building their voice.

Your second brain isn't just memory. It's the corpus that makes AI actually useful.

**1-line blurb:** Building knowledge infrastructure that makes AI collaboration actually work.

### Option 2D: The Mirror Problem

**Subtitle:** What AI reveals about your creative identity

**Abstract (115 words):**
AI is not just a tool. It's a mirror.

When you prompt an AI, you're revealing what you think you want. When you select from its outputs, you're revealing what you actually value. When you train it on your work, you're seeing your patterns reflected back.

This can be uncomfortable. It can also be the most useful creative development work you've ever done.

In this session, we'll discuss: What happens when you confront your creative DNA through AI tools. How to use that reflection to clarify your vision. How to build from honest self-knowledge rather than aspirational fantasy.

The machines show us who we are. The question is whether we're ready to look.

**1-line blurb:** Using AI as a mirror to clarify your creative identity and build from honest self-knowledge.

**Subtitle:** Building with AI when you're not a programmer

You don't need to know how to code. You need to know what you want to build.

In this hands-on workshop, we'll build something together. Could be a script development tool. A character dialogue generator. A storyboard visualizer. Whatever the room wants to make.

You'll see the complete workflow: How to describe what you want in plain language. How to push buttons and "fuck with it" until it works. How the thing you build reveals the thing you actually needed.

No programming required. Just curiosity and a laptop. By the end, you'll have built something that works.

**1-line blurb:** Turn ideas into working prototypes using AI coding tools — no programming required.
