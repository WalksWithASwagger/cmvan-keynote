# Both Hands Full — LaSalle College Keynote Recap

## Executive Summary

Kris Krüg delivered a keynote to LaSalle College Vancouver alumni and students on what it actually feels like to be a creative professional in the AI era. The core thesis: walk forward with "both hands full"—one hand holding critique and resistance to AI's harms, the other holding curiosity and willingness to engage. The talk covered real fears (stolen work, junior pipeline collapse, cognitive dependency), transformation stories from creative professionals, and practical approaches to building workflows that maintain human agency.

---

## Core Thesis: Both Hands Full

> "AI is trained on the stolen work of mankind without consent, and I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been in my whole life. Both of these things are true."

The framework:
- **Left hand**: The fears, critique, resistance
- **Right hand**: The reality, curiosity, transformation
- **The call**: Hold both. Keep walking.

---

## Key Themes

### 1. The Five Fears (Left Hand)

1. **Stolen work / consent**: Trained on mankind's creative output without permission
2. **Race to bottom**: "Good enough" AI output devalues human creative work
3. **Junior pipeline**: Entry-level roles that build seniors are vanishing
4. **Cognitive dependency**: What happens when we outsource early-stage thinking?
5. **Commodification**: If anyone can generate anything, why do they need us?

Audience additions during talk:
- Environmental impact (water, power)
- Reliability / quality concerns
- Students never learning to write
- Child safety / abuse potential
- Emotional content in art
- "Killer robots" / militarization

### 2. Transformation Stories (Right Hand)

**Maya Bruck** — UX designer, former Etsy. Reinvented workflow using Figma Make + ChatGPT. "Strategic skeptic" who now teaches.

**Kevin Friel** — 25-year Hollywood VFX veteran (Dune, Detective Pikachu). Built "Vibe Lounge" for real-time generative visuals. Uses AI as competitive differentiator.

### 3. Personal Testimony

- Trained model on 2,000 cross-processed street portraits
- Owns inputs and outputs (not using others' stolen work)
- Presented at Ars Electronica, won honorable mention
- Published peer-reviewed research
- "Not a consumer of ChatGPT from Silicon Valley—a producer of creativity and new models"

### 4. The Human Moat

Not "creativity" abstractly—it's **judgment, taste, discernment**.

> "If you can teach an AI why you choose one thing instead of another, you're teaching it something very useful."

Key documents to create:
- Writing style guide (how you sound)
- Worldview document (what you believe)
- Glossary and anti-glossary
- Decision logs with reasoning

### 5. Write for the Bot

Featured video: Anonymous OpenAI employee on Dwarkesh Patel podcast:

> "If there are values you have which are not expressed yet in text, and if there are things that you like or want, if they aren't reflected online, then to the AI, they basically don't exist. And that is dangerously close to won't exist."

The homework:
- Record and transcribe your ideas
- Build knowledge bases of your thinking
- Make your values machine-readable

### 6. The Selector Frame

RiP: A Remix Manifesto connection:
- "Culture always builds on the past. The past tries to control the future."
- DJs navigated this tension—they were called **selectors**
- Generation is cheap. Taste is not. Your taste is your moat.

## Quotable Moments

**On the non-consensual moment:**
> "I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual. It didn't ask me to steal my work, to take over this stuff, to change everything all around me. All I'm trying to do is keep my head above water and reinvent myself."

**On skeptics:**
> "The most skeptical people about AI—I need you to educate yourself the most because we need your voices at the table. If you choose to resist all the way and not engage, it means that the decisions of the future are going to be made by all the people that are on the hype side."

**On certainty:**
> "I don't have answers to any of these questions. I teach this shit for a living and I don't know. Anyone who will stand up and tell you that they've got all these things figured out—those are the people to not listen to."

**On the moment:**
> "This moment of changing everything where on one hand there's a whole bunch of stuff to be concerned about and afraid of, and on the other hand there's so much opportunity and curiosity and transformation taking place that it's an impossibility to look away."

## Q&A Highlights

**Key audience concerns raised:**

1. **Children and AI** — Safety protocols, developmental impact, learning dependency
2. **Water/environmental impact** — Data center resource consumption
3. **Education/assessment** — All students now performing at A-level; hard to know who's behind the AI
4. **Reliability** — Code quality, hallucinations, verification
5. **Data privacy** — Where does information go when using cloud tools?
6. **Keeping up** — Overwhelming pace of change, tool fatigue

**Notable exchange with skeptic:**
- Skeptic: "Are you not destroying yourself inside by saturating your life with AI?"
- Kris: "Not in my case. I feel more creative, productive, and powerful than I've ever been."
- (Critique note: This landed defensive rather than validating the rational fear)

**On keeping up:**
> "Just swim in the ocean. You're not going to get ahead of the ocean or try to control it. But if you just go for a swim in it, that's all that's really required."
