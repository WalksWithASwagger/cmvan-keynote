## LinkedIn Posts

### Option 1: The Thesis Post

I gave a talk to art and design students this week, and I led with this:

"AI is trained on stolen work without consent. And I'm more creative, powerful, and productive than I've ever been because of these tools."

Both things are true.

We're past the point where you can just pick a side. The boosters who tell you "get on board or get left behind"? Lazy. The doomers who say "refuse and resist"? Also lazy.

The real position is harder: hold both hands full.

Left hand: the fears are real. Consent was violated. Junior pipelines are collapsing. We're outsourcing cognition without knowing the cost.

Right hand: the transformation is real. I've watched designers compress weeks into hours. VFX veterans build tools they couldn't have dreamed of. My own work has never been more ambitious.

The people I need most in this conversation? The skeptics. The critics. The ones with genuine concerns.

Because if you opt out entirely, the decisions about this technology get made without you. Governance is set by whoever shows up.

So here's my ask: engage even if you're skeptical. Learn enough to know what you're criticizing. Walk forward with both hands full.

What are you holding in each hand right now?

### Option 2: The Skeptic Post

The people most critical of AI are the ones I need in the room the most.

Here's why:

If the skeptics opt out—refuse to engage, refuse to learn the tools, refuse to participate—then every decision about how this technology develops gets made by the true believers.

The governance. The norms. The policies. All set by people who think AI is the greatest thing ever.

That's not a balanced conversation. That's a runaway train.

I run an AI community with 1,800+ members. The most valuable voices aren't the hype people. They're the ones who push back. Who ask uncomfortable questions. Who won't let us handwave past the real problems.

Consent violations in training data? Real problem. Junior pipeline collapse? Happening now. Cognitive dependency? We're all guinea pigs in an experiment we didn't sign up for.

I don't have answers to these questions. I teach this for a living and I still don't know.

But I know this: the room is making decisions whether you're in it or not.

Skeptics: we need you at the table. Engage even if you refuse to adopt. Your critique is the counterweight this conversation desperately needs.

---

### Option 3: The Selector Post

DJs figured this out decades ago.

When sampling technology exploded, the music industry had the same fight we're having now about AI. Culture built on the past. The past trying to control the future.

The DJs who thrived weren't the ones who could sample the most. They were the ones with taste. The ones who could hear a 4-bar loop from a 1972 funk record and know it would work.

They were called *selectors*.

Generation is cheap. Selection is not.

That's the creative moat in the AI era. Not "creativity" in some abstract sense—everyone claims that. The moat is judgment. Taste. Discernment.

The ability to look at ten AI outputs and know which one is right. And be able to articulate why.

I've started building documents that capture this: a writing style guide, a worldview doc, a glossary of words I use and words I never use.

Not so AI can replace me. So AI can understand what makes my work mine.

Close your eyes for a second. Think of one thing you do in your creative work that you would never give up to AI—not because you couldn't automate it, but because it's the part that makes the work yours.

That's your creative DNA. That's the piece worth protecting.

What's yours?

### Thread 2: Five Fears (7 posts)

**[1/7]**
I teach AI for a living and I'll tell you straight: I don't have these figured out.

The 5 fears creative professionals actually have about AI:

🧵

**[2/7]**
1. STOLEN WORK

AI was trained on humanity's creative output. Without consent.

I found 1,800 of my photos in a training dataset. That doesn't mean every model used them—but the consent story is exactly as casual as critics claim.

**[3/7]**
2. RACE TO BOTTOM

When "good enough" costs $20/month, the floor drops out.

Client budgets compress. Expectations shift. The work that used to pay bills becomes commodity.

**[4/7]**
3. JUNIOR PIPELINE COLLAPSE

Entry-level tasks that train seniors are vanishing.

You'd spend 6 months converting assets, learning the systems, the clients, the back-and-forth. That's how juniors became seniors.

What replaces it?

**[5/7]**
4. COGNITIVE DEPENDENCY

If you don't use a muscle, you lose it.

What happens when we outsource idea generation, research synthesis, early drafts? We're all in an experiment we didn't sign up for.

Results aren't in.

**[6/7]**
5. COMMODIFICATION

If anyone can generate anything, why do they need you?

The counter: generation is cheap. Taste is not.

But the fear is real.

**[7/7]**
Asked the audience what I missed. They added:
- Environmental impact (water, power)
- Child safety
- Militarization
- Students never learning to write

Anyone who says they've got this figured out isn't paying attention.

Both hands full. Keep walking.
