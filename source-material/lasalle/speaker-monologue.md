# Both Hands Full — Verbatim Monologue

*For audio practice. Read aloud at natural speaking pace. ~45 minutes.*

---

Both Hands Full. How creatives are actually working with AI.

I want to talk about what it actually feels like to be a creative professional right now. Not what the LinkedIn gurus are saying. Not what the tech executives are promising. What it actually feels like to wake up every day and do creative work in the middle of this thing that none of us asked for but all of us have to figure out.

I'm Kris Krüg. I run AI Upgrade, which is a training program for creative professionals. I'm part of BC + AI Ecosystem Association, which is a nonprofit building community around creative and ethical AI. And I host the Vancouver AI Meetup, which is about 1,800 people now, meeting every month at the Space Centre. So I spend a lot of time in rooms like this one, talking to people about AI.

And here's what I've noticed. Every AI conversation you've ever been in probably sounded like one of two camps.

On one side, you've got the Boosters. "AI is the greatest thing ever." "Stop whining, get on board." "If you're not using AI, you're already behind." These are usually tech people, executives, investors. They've got a financial incentive to be excited. And some of them genuinely are excited. But there's a dismissiveness in that position, isn't there? A kind of "your concerns don't matter, just adapt" energy.

On the other side, you've got the Doomers. "AI is stealing our work." "It's destroying jobs." "We should resist." And honestly, I'm more sympathetic to this camp. Their concerns are real. I'll get to that. But there's something in this position too that lets you off the hook. If you're just resisting, you don't have to figure out what to actually do.

Here's the thing: both positions are lazy. Both let you avoid the harder work of holding complexity.

So let me tell you where I actually stand.

AI is trained on stolen work. All the work of mankind, scraped and slurped up without consent, without compensation, with no visibility into what they took or how they're using it. That's true. That's real. Your work might be in there. Mine definitely is.

And I'm more creative, powerful, and productive than I've ever been because of these tools. I have smashed through technical ceilings I couldn't break through before. I'm building things I never could have built. I'm exploring ideas faster than ever.

Both things are true.

And here's the framework I want to offer you tonight: we walk forward holding both.

This isn't about resolving the tension. It's about carrying it. In one hand, you're holding the fears, the concerns, the ethical problems. In the other hand, you're holding the possibilities, the transformations, the things you've discovered you can do. Neither hand is empty. We don't pretend the fears don't exist. We don't pretend the possibilities aren't real. We walk forward holding both.

So let me show you what's in each hand, starting with the fears.

Fear number one: trained on stolen work. This is the foundational ethical problem. All the visual art, writing, code, music that trained these models came from us, from creators, without consent. Whose values does AI encode? Whose worldview is baked into these systems? These aren't paranoid questions. They're essential ones.

Fear number two: race to the bottom. Clients can now get "good enough" for cheap. The floor just dropped. If you were competing on speed or volume, AI just undercut you. That's real. We have to acknowledge it.

Fear number three: the junior pipeline collapse. This one keeps me up at night. Entry-level tasks, the ones that used to build senior creatives, media monitoring, basic research, first-draft layouts, those are disappearing. AI is eating them. So how do we create the next generation of senior creatives if there's no entry-level work to train them on? I teach AI for a living, and I don't have this solved. Anyone who tells you they do is lying.

Fear number four: cognitive dependency. What happens when we outsource thinking? When we stop drafting and only edit? When we lose the muscle because we never use it? We don't know yet. But it's a real concern.

Fear number five: commodification. If everyone can generate, what's left? If a client can make their own logo in 30 seconds, why do they need you? This is the existential question.

Don't dismiss these fears. They're real. They're legitimate. We have to hold them.

But now let me show you what's in the other hand.

I have never felt as creative or productive or powerful as when I started messing with this stuff. It has helped me smash through technical ceilings I had not been able to break through before.

Let me show you what I mean.

I trained a model on 2,000 of my street portraits. Cross-process slide film. Work I've been doing for 20 years. And I used that to explore what my body of work actually means. Not stealing from others, learning from myself. Understanding my own aesthetic at a level I never had before.

I dropped Canada's 485-page federal budget into a tool and said "I want to click around and understand this thing." Charts. Diagrams. Filters. Interactive dashboard. Done in hours, not weeks.

I record everything. Transcribe it. Commit it to my knowledge base. Deploy AI on top of it. My past self becomes my future asset. Everything I've ever said, written, thought, organized and searchable and usable.

I vibe code something almost every day. It's only 9:40 today, and I already built a web app. In the bathtub. That's not a joke. That's a Wednesday.

This is the AutoLoom project. These are my portraits, the source material. 2,000 images of street photography. This is what I fed into the model.

This is the interface. AutoLoom, built by Philippe Pasquier's Metacreation Lab at SFU. You feed it your images, it learns your aesthetic. No coding required. It's a GAN-based visual synthesizer.

And this is the output. Left column: real portraits I took. Right column: AI-generated transformations in my style. This is me training AI on my own work. I own the model. I own the output. This is the ethical alternative to generic models trained on stolen work.

And we took it seriously. We packaged this into a project called Alpha Prism. Submitted it to Ars Electronica, one of the most prestigious digital art festivals in the world. We got Honorable Mention. The research paper was peer-reviewed, published, and is now being cited by other researchers.

This is what "train on your own work" looks like when you take it seriously. You can contribute to the field, not just consume it.

So what skills do you need to do this?

There's the tech skills: prompting, workflow, organization, compositing, model training. You need to know how to ask the right questions, organize your processes, combine outputs, and eventually train your own models.

But tech skills are table stakes. They get you started. The human skills make you irreplaceable: curiosity, adaptability, discernment, critical thinking. These can't be automated. These are what separate someone who uses AI from someone who's genuinely powerful with it.

Here's my actual stack. The tools I use every day.

Notion is home base, my second brain. If it's not captured, it doesn't compound.

Plaud is my capture layer. I'm wearing it right now, recording this talk. My past self becomes my future asset.

NotebookLM for grounded synthesis. Perplexity for research. ChatGPT for iteration and writing. Claude Code for prototype-first building. Midjourney for visual exploration. ElevenLabs for voice and video.

I try not to tool-hop. I run a small stack where each tool has a job, and the point is to compound creative effort over time.

But here's the punchline: the real unlock isn't the tools. It's clear creative identity. The clearer you are about your taste and worldview, the more useful AI becomes.

This is vibe coding. Prototype-first building.

Traditional development used to mean weeks of planning, framework setup, debug cycles, dependencies. Months to get something working.

Vibe coding means clear creative intent, Claude Code, iterate and ship. Single file, no framework. Hours, not months.

Remember when I said I built a web app in the bathtub by 9:40am? This is how. Clear intent plus Claude Code equals working prototype in hours.

195KB dashboard. Single HTML file. No framework. No dependencies.

Building teaches you what you actually need faster than planning ever could. What you think you want to build isn't what you actually need. Building is how you discover.

So here are four principles for walking forward with both hands full.

Principle one: use the tools while acknowledging the problems. You can be critical and engaged at the same time. Keep a kill switch. Human review is non-negotiable. Never automate beyond your trust level. AI is the assistant, you're the decider.

Principle two: train AI on your own work. You own the model. You own the output. The future is personalized, not generic slop. Your seven years of portfolio work, your writing style, your design decisions, these can become your competitive advantage. Not someone else's training data.

Principle three: become a selector.

Has anyone seen this movie? RiP!: A Remix Manifesto, 2008, by Brett Gaylor. This film came out at the beginning of remix culture, EDM, samples, mashups, copyright battles.

Twenty years ago, this film provided a lens for copyright and changing technology in music and art. The manifesto was: culture always builds on the past. The past tries to control the future. To build free societies, we must limit the control of the past.

Here we are again. All our creative works slurped up into these AIs. Same tension. Different technology.

DJs navigated this. They were originally called selectors. That's our future role. Generation is cheap now. Taste is not. AI generates, you select. Your taste, your judgment, your curation, that's the differentiator.

Selector skills: curiosity, adaptability, discernment, critical thinking. These are what make the tech skills valuable.

Principle four: write for the bot.

A lot of the smartest people I know right now are writing for the bot. Getting stuff out of their hearts and heads into the universe as a way to express to these new consciousnesses what humans are all about.

If your values aren't expressed in text, to AI they don't exist.

So here's your homework tonight. Writing style guide, how you sound. Worldview document, what you believe. Glossary and anti-glossary, words you use and words you never use. Decision logs with reasoning, why you chose what you chose.

This is context engineering. You're designing your knowledge so AI operates in a web of meaning, not isolated data. Your second brain becomes AI's context window.

You're not just documenting for AI. You're training the future on what matters.

Now let me ask you the real question.

Not "will AI replace me?" Not "should I use AI?"

The real question is: what does AI reveal about who you are?

AI is a mirror. It amplifies whatever you bring to it. If you bring confusion, you get confusion. Generic slop. If you bring clear creative identity, you get something powerful.

You're the original intelligence. AI is the amplifier.

Self-knowledge is the prerequisite to AI mastery. Not prompting skills. Not tool fluency. Self-knowledge.

So what's left? What's stubbornly human?

The AI can't eat ice cream for you. Only you can determine your preferences.

When AI can do calendaring, accounting, communications, video, what remains? Embodied experience: running, tasting, parenting. Preferences, desires, hopes. Empathy and intuition. Self-awareness. The things you refuse to automate.

These aren't limitations. They're the irreducible human core. This is what makes you you.

Before we open for questions, I want you to close your eyes for a moment.

What's the one thing you do that you would never want to give up to AI? The last thing you'd hold onto?

What makes your work special? What about it is unique?

Take twenty seconds. Think about it.

That thing you just thought of? That's your creative DNA. That's what's left. That's what AI can't eat.

Now let me tell you about some things we're building.

Ethos Lab is an AI experimentation club for Black and Indigenous youth. Free program. Six-month cohort starting Spring 2026. Hands-on AI tools for creative expression. Part of BC + AI Ecosystem Association. If you know young people in these communities, connect them. This is what building culture looks like. We're not just talking about AI equity. We're building it.

Vancouver AI Meetup. January 28th. Two weeks from tonight. MacMillan Space Centre. 1,800 members and growing. Every last Tuesday of the month. Free. Mark your calendars. This is what community looks like.

BC + AI Ecosystem Association. DIY, grassroots, creative and ethical. Real humans exploring AI's depths together. Scan to become a member. We're building something that can't be replicated: community.

And if tonight sparked something, AI Upgrade. Next cohort starts February 5th. Six-week certification course. Hands-on AI workflow mastery. One-on-one coaching calls. Community of creative professionals. I'm offering a special rate for LaSalle students. Scan the code or talk to me after.

So let's talk. Questions.

What's in your hands? What fears? What possibilities?

What was that thing you don't want to give up? That's where we start.

Start tonight. Write three sentences about your creative worldview. That's your first step.
