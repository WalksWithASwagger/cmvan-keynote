# Both Hands Full: How To Hold Critique And Curiosity Simultaneously

I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

That's what I told 200 art and design students at LaSalle College in January 2026. Not as provocation. As testimony. The room went silent in that specific way rooms go silent when someone names the thing everyone's been feeling but nobody's been saying.

"It didn't ask me," I continued. "Didn't ask to steal my work, to take over this stuff, to change everything around me. All I'm trying to do is keep my head above water and reinvent myself."

One day you're building a career through craft. Years of mastering your tools. Developing your eye. Earning your reputation the slow way, the hard way, the only way that used to exist. You've put in the hours. You've paid the dues. You've built something that feels solid.

Next day, someone's selling your style for $20 a month. Subscribe to "draw like you" as a service. Your voice, your aesthetic, your hard-won expertise, packaged and productized and available to anyone with a credit card.

You didn't choose this. Nobody asked. Nobody said "Hey, can we train AI on everything you've ever made?" Nobody said "This will fundamentally alter your industry—you cool with that?" It just happened. While you were paying your bills and raising your kids and doing your work, the world changed underneath you.

That's what I mean by non-consensual. This moment didn't ask permission. And here's the thing that makes most people's heads explode: You can be pissed about that—rightfully, legitimately pissed—AND still engage. Both are true. Both matter. Both are necessary.

Most conversations about AI want to force you to pick a side. You've been in these conversations. We all have. On one side, the Boosters: "AI is amazing! Embrace it fully! Stop being a luddite! The future is here and you're either on the train or under it!" They've got venture capital backing and tech conference keynotes and a dismissiveness that makes your teeth hurt.

On the other side, the Doomers: "AI is destroying everything! Resist! Don't be complicit! This is theft and we should burn it all down!" They've got legitimate grievances and moral clarity and a purity that leaves no room for nuance.

Both camps say the same thing underneath all the rhetoric: Pick a side. Choose your team. All in or all out.

I'm saying: What if you don't?

What if you hold the critique in your left hand—AI IS trained on stolen work, consent WAS violated, junior pipelines ARE collapsing, this IS extraction masquerading as innovation—and hold curiosity in your right hand—designers ARE compressing weeks into hours, new capabilities ARE being unlocked, transformation IS happening whether we like it or not—and you just keep walking forward with both hands full?

That's the practice I want to offer you. Not because it's comfortable. It's deeply uncomfortable. But because it's the only honest position I've found.

Let me show you what's in my left hand. The critique. The fears. The problems that are real and happening right now, not hypothetical concerns we can debate in theory.

First, the foundational theft. Everything AI learned came from us. Every model was trained on humanity's creative output, scraped without permission, used without compensation, deployed without consent. Your photos. Your writing. Your code. Your art. Used without asking. This isn't a gray area. This is theft. Call it what it is.

I released thousands of photos on Flickr under Creative Commons over two decades. When I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly 1,800 of my images in a major training corpus. That doesn't mean every AI model used them—training pipelines vary—but it means the consent story is exactly as casual as the critics claim. Nobody asked me. They just took it.

Second, the race to the bottom. "Good enough" AI outputs are devaluing human craft in real time. Why pay $5,000 for a designer when Midjourney costs $20 a month? Why hire a writer when ChatGPT writes "adequate" copy for free? Market pressure toward commodification. Real. Happening now. I watch students lose bids to clients who've decided AI is good enough. That pressure is messing with real people's livelihoods.

Third, the junior pipeline collapse. Entry-level jobs vanishing. The roles that used to train senior creatives are disappearing faster than anyone wants to admit. Peter brings this up Week 1 of every cohort: Ivy League grad can't find first job because entry work gets done by AI now. How do people become seniors if they can't be juniors first? Nobody knows. We're creating a skills gap we don't know how to fill, and we're all just hoping someone figures it out before we need the next generation of experts.

Fourth, cognitive dependency. If you outsource thinking, does the muscle atrophy? Use a calculator daily, mental math degrades. Use GPS constantly, spatial memory weakens. Use AI for everything—does critical thinking diminish? I don't know the answer yet. Neither does anyone else. We're all participants in an experiment we didn't sign up for, and the results won't be clear for years.

Fifth, commodification. If anyone can generate anything, why do they need you? Expertise devalued. Craft dismissed. "We'll just use AI" becomes the client's answer to everything. This is the fear underneath all the others: becoming obsolete. And it's not irrational. Stock photography collapsed. Junior copywriting is getting automated. Design work is being commodified. Not everywhere. Not all at once. But enough that the pattern is visible. Enough that the fear is rational.

At LaSalle, students added more. Environmental impact—training models burns obscene amounts of energy. Emotional content—AI can't feel but it fakes emotion convincingly enough to manipulate. Cultural homogenization—AI averages everything toward bland. All legitimate concerns. All worth holding.

I tell students: These risks are real. I'm not dismissing them. I'm not saying "it'll be fine" or "don't worry about it" or any of the toxic positivity that makes you want to flip tables. These fears are legitimate. You should hold them. AND—and this is the crucial pivot—you still have agency.

The binary trap goes like this: If you acknowledge these fears, you're expected to resist AI completely. If you engage with AI at all, you're supposed to pretend the problems don't exist. That's the trap. The forced choice. The false binary.

You can see the problems clearly AND engage anyway. Critique doesn't require withdrawal. The left hand holds these fears. Don't drop them. Don't pretend they're not there. Stay critical. They keep you honest.

Now let me show you what's in my right hand. The curiosity. The transformations. Not hype. Not theory. Actual things I've witnessed.

Maya Bruck. UX designer. Former Etsy. Self-described "strategic skeptic." When she started in our program, she was resistant. Suspicious. All the legitimate fears we just talked about. But she started experimenting with Figma plus ChatGPT. Careful experiments. Controlled tests. Seeing what worked and what didn't. Her trajectory went from resistant to experimental to fluent to teaching others in six weeks. She's now teaching AI integration to other designers. Her value went up, not down. Because she learned to use AI to amplify her judgment, not replace it.

Kevin Friel. 25 years in Hollywood VFX. Dune. Detective Pikachu. Bullet Train. The prestige projects that animation kids dream about. He built something called Vibe Lounge—real-time generative visuals. Uses AI not as a replacement for his craft but as a competitive differentiator. Kevin's not worried about AI taking his job. He's using it to do things that weren't possible before. Creating work that stands out precisely because of what he brings to the tools.

My own work. I trained a model on 2,000 cross-processed portraits. My own photos. Two decades of street photography. Not using others' stolen work—using my own consented data. The result: presented at Ars Electronica, got honorable mention, published peer-reviewed research. Own inputs, own outputs. Proving that alternative approaches exist. You can engage with AI without participating in the extraction model.

And across 80 cohorts, I've watched the pattern repeat. Week 1: "Will I be replaced?" Week 6: Building production systems, launching businesses, getting promoted. Fear to capability to strategy to empowerment. Not everyone. But enough that the pattern is undeniable. Designers compressing three-week projects into four hours. Journalists analyzing datasets they couldn't touch before. Sales leaders multiplying content output 53 times. Educators building tools their districts want to buy.

This isn't hype. This is documented. This is real people's lives changing.

And here's my personal testimony, the thing that sounds like contradiction but is actually just complexity: AI is trained on stolen work without consent. And I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been in my entire life. Both statements are true. I can critique the theft AND use the capability. I can resist extraction AND build with the tools. I can be pissed about non-consent AND leverage the moment.

Not contradiction. Complexity. The right hand holds this transformation. Don't drop it for ideological purity. This is real. People's lives are changing. The capabilities are unlocking. Pretending that's not happening doesn't make you more ethical. It just makes you less effective.

So how do you walk forward with both hands full? What does that actually look like day to day, when you're trying to navigate this mess?

Morning: build using AI. Compress four hours into 30 minutes. Use the tools. Engage the capability. Get the work done.

Afternoon: teach about consent violations and ethical concerns. Name the theft. Acknowledge the harm. Don't pretend it's fine.

Evening: advocate for alternative models. Metacreation Lab. Local training. Artist-owned data. Build the systems we want instead of just complaining about the ones we have.

All the same day. Not conflicting. All necessary.

Emily got this. Educator in our program. She uses AI to build Bee School, environmental education for kids. Right hand—engaging capability. But she also teaches those kids about consent and data sovereignty. Left hand—maintaining critique. Both in the same curriculum. Her colleagues said "we should ban AI in schools, don't participate." Emily said "I'm going to teach kids how to use it responsibly, because if I don't, who will?"

She's not uncritical. She's engaged critically. That's the difference.

Our whole community operates this way. BC + AI uses AI for practical applications. Right hand. But we also run working groups on Indigenous data sovereignty, ethics frameworks, consent models. Left hand. Both happening simultaneously. Not "let's use AI and ignore the problems." Not "let's resist AI and lose agency." But "let's engage with AI while actively building better models."

What I reject are the clean narratives. Pure Boosters who say "AI is all good, adopt uncritically" are ignoring real harms. Becoming complicit in extraction. Operating with no ethical grounding. These are the people who will look back in ten years and realize they participated in something harmful because they refused to look at the critique. They wanted the wins too badly to see the costs.

Pure Doomers who say "AI is all bad, resist completely" are losing agency. Ceding governance to true believers. Giving up any ability to shape outcomes. These are the people who will look back in ten years and realize they had no voice in how things unfolded because they opted out of the conversation. They wanted purity more than they wanted influence.

Both feel clean. Both are incomplete. Both are, frankly, lazy. Because they let you avoid the harder work of holding complexity.

The messy middle—both hands full—says "this is problematic AND I'm engaging anyway because opting out is worse." Uncomfortable? Absolutely. True? Also yes. I'd rather be uncomfortable and effective than comfortable and irrelevant.

Here's the governance question that keeps me up at night. If all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out, who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used? Who builds the guardrails? The people who don't see the problems. That's who.

Real example from early AI ethics boards: mostly AI optimists. Critics refused to participate. "I won't legitimize something I oppose." Noble stance. Clear conscience. Zero influence. Result: weak ethics frameworks with no teeth. Why? Because the people who understood the risks best weren't in the room.

My argument: if you see problems others don't, your responsibility is to stay in the room. Not to validate bad systems. To push for better ones. There's a difference between complicity and critical engagement. Complicity is uncritical participation. Critical engagement is participating while maintaining critique.

Emily's colleagues opted out. "We should ban AI from schools." Clean position. Clear stance. Zero impact on what kids actually learn. Emily stayed in. "I'm going to teach kids how to use it responsibly." Messy position. Uncomfortable stance. Massive impact. Kids in her program learn AI capability AND consent frameworks AND data sovereignty AND ethical considerations. Because she stayed in the room.

The alternative: if Emily opted out, kids learn AI from YouTube with zero ethical framework. Which is worse? I know my answer.

The people most uncomfortable with AI are often the people who should be shaping its use. If they opt out, we lose the ethical depth we need most. I'm not opting out. I'm staying in the room. Both hands full. Using the tools while pushing for better systems. Building with AI while advocating for artist compensation. Teaching capability while maintaining critique. All of it. Simultaneously.

This isn't easy. It requires holding tension that most people can't stomach. Most people want clean answers. "Is AI good or bad?" Both. Neither. Depends on how we use it and what systems we build around it. Can you hold that complexity? Or do you need a simple narrative?

The students who transform are the ones who can hold both hands full. The critique AND the curiosity. The fear AND the exploration. The students who stay stuck pick a side. Either uncritical adoption—lose ethical grounding—or complete resistance—lose agency. Both positions are cleaner. Both are incomplete. The messy middle is where the work happens.

The moment didn't ask permission. AI is here. Junior pipelines are collapsing. Your work is in training data. You didn't choose this. Nobody asked. It happened. Now what?

You can embrace uncritically. Become a booster. Ignore the harms. Participate in extraction. Feel clean about your enthusiasm. Feel complicit ten years from now.

You can resist completely. Become a doomer. Maintain purity. Lose influence. Feel clean about your principles. Feel irrelevant ten years from now.

Or you can walk forward with both hands full.

Left hand: this is non-consensual, this is unjust, this is extractive, I'm not okay with it.

Right hand: this is transformative, this creates capability, this is happening, I want agency in shaping it.

Both. Simultaneously. While moving forward. Can you hold complexity like a grown-up? Or do you need clean narratives?

I'm choosing complexity. Because if all the ethical, critical, thoughtful people opt out, governance gets made by people who don't see the problems. That's worse than engaging with critique intact.

So I'm here. Both hands full. Walking forward. You coming?
