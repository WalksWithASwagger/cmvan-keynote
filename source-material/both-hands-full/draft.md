# Both Hands Full: How To Hold Critique And Curiosity Simultaneously

I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

LaSalle College, January 2026. 200 art and design students.

Silence.

> "It didn't ask me to steal my work, to take over this stuff, to change everything around me. All I'm trying to do is keep my head above water and reinvent myself."

The feeling: One day you're building a career through craft. Years of mastering your tools, developing your eye, earning your reputation.

Next day: Someone's selling your style for $20/month. Subscribe to "draw like you" as a service.

You didn't choose this.

Nobody asked: "Hey, can we train AI on all your work?" Nobody said: "This will fundamentally alter your industry—cool with that?"

It just... happened.

That's what this is. A moment that didn't ask permission.

And here's the thing: You can be pissed about that AND still engage.

Both are true.

Most conversations about AI force you to pick a side:

The Boosters: "AI is amazing! Embrace it fully! Stop being a luddite!"

The Doomers: "AI is destroying everything! Resist! Don't be complicit!"

Both camps say: Pick a side.

I'm saying: What if you don't?

What if you hold the critique in your left hand—AI IS trained on stolen work, consent WAS violated, junior pipelines ARE collapsing—

AND hold curiosity in your right hand—designers ARE compressing weeks into hours, new capabilities ARE unlocked, transformation IS happening—

And keep walking forward with both hands full?

This article is about that practice.

---

## Left Hand: The Critique

What's actually true about the problems with AI. Not hypothetical fears. Real shit happening right now.

**Fear 1: Stolen Work Without Consent**

Training data equals humanity's creative output scraped without permission.

Your photos. Your writing. Your code. Your art.

Used without asking. No compensation. No attribution.

This is theft. Call it what it is.

I released thousands of photos on Flickr under Creative Commons over two decades. When I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly 1,800 of my images in a major training corpus. That doesn't mean every AI model used them—training pipelines vary—but it means the consent story is exactly as casual as critics claim.

Nobody asked me. They just took it.

**Fear 2: Race To The Bottom**

"Good enough" AI outputs devalue human craft.

Why pay $5,000 for a designer when Midjourney costs $20/month? Why hire a writer when ChatGPT writes "adequate" copy?

Market pressure toward commodification. Real. Happening now.

Clients are saying it out loud: "Can't you just use AI for this?" Translation: "Why should I pay you when a tool can approximate it?"

That pressure is fucking with people's livelihoods.

**Fear 3: Junior Pipeline Collapse**

Entry-level jobs vanishing. The roles that trained seniors are disappearing.

Peter's example from Week 1 of every cohort: Ivy League grad can't find first job. Entry work done by AI now.

How do people become seniors if they can't be juniors first?

Nobody knows.

This isn't theoretical. I'm watching it happen. Junior designers getting cut. Junior writers replaced by AI drafts that seniors edit. The pathway from novice to expert is eroding.

We're creating a skills gap we don't know how to fill.

**Fear 4: Cognitive Dependency**

If you outsource thinking, do you atrophy?

Use calculator daily → Mental math degrades. Use GPS constantly → Spatial memory weakens. Use AI for everything → Does critical thinking diminish?

The muscle atrophy problem. Real concern.

I don't know the answer yet. Neither does anyone else. We're all participants in an experiment we didn't sign up for.

**Fear 5: Commodification**

If anyone can generate anything, why do they need you?

Expertise devalued. Craft dismissed. "We'll just use AI" becomes the answer.

This is the fear underneath all others: Becoming obsolete.

And it's not irrational. It's happening in pockets. Stock photography collapsed. Junior copywriting getting automated. Design work commodified.

Not everywhere. Not all at once. But enough that the pattern is visible.

At LaSalle, students added more fears:
- Environmental impact (training models burns energy)
- Emotional content (AI can't feel, but it fakes emotion)
- Cultural homogenization (AI averages everything toward bland)

All legitimate.

What I tell students: These risks are real. I'm not dismissing them. I'm not saying "it'll be fine."

I'm saying: These fears are legitimate. AND you still have agency.

The binary trap: If you acknowledge these fears, you're expected to resist AI completely.

That's the trap.

You can see the problems clearly AND engage anyway.

Critique doesn't require withdrawal.

The left hand: Hold these fears. Don't drop them. Stay critical. They keep you honest.

## Right Hand: The Curiosity

What's also true. Not hype. Actual transformations I've witnessed.

**Transformation 1: Maya Bruck (UX Designer)**

Former Etsy. Self-described "strategic skeptic." Reinvented her workflow using Figma + ChatGPT.

Her trajectory: Resistant → Experimental → Fluent → Teaching others.

Not replaced. Transformed.

She's now teaching AI integration to other designers. Her value went up, not down. Because she learned to use AI to amplify her judgment, not replace it.

**Transformation 2: Kevin Friel (VFX Veteran)**

25 years Hollywood. Dune. Detective Pikachu. Built "Vibe Lounge"—real-time generative visuals.

Uses AI as competitive differentiator, not replacement.

Kevin's not worried about AI taking his job. He's using it to do things that weren't possible before. Creating work that stands out because of what he brings to the tools.

**Transformation 3: My Own Work**

Trained a model on 2,000 cross-processed portraits. My own photos. Not using others' stolen work.

Result:
- Presented at Ars Electronica
- Honorable mention
- Published peer-reviewed research
- Own inputs, own outputs

Not a consumer of ChatGPT. A producer of models.

Proving that alternative approaches exist. You can engage with AI without participating in the extraction model.

**Transformation 4: Students Across 80+ Cohorts**

Week 1: "Will I be replaced?" Week 6: Building production systems, launching businesses, getting promoted.

Pattern repeats: Fear → Capability → Strategy → Empowerment.

Not everyone. But enough that the pattern is undeniable.

Real things happening:
- Designers compressing 3-week projects into 4 hours
- Journalists analyzing datasets they couldn't touch before
- Sales leaders multiplying content output 53x
- Educators building tools their districts want to buy

This isn't hype. This is documented across cohorts.

**The Capability Explosion**

My personal testimony:

> "AI is trained on stolen work without consent. And I'm more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I've ever been in my whole life."

Both statements are true.

I can critique the theft AND use the capability. I can resist extraction AND build with the tools. I can be pissed about non-consent AND leverage the moment.

Not contradiction. Complexity.

The right hand: Hold the transformation. Don't drop it for ideological purity.

This is real. People's lives are changing.

## Walking Forward (Both Hands Full)

The practice isn't: Pick a side and commit.

It's: Hold both sides and keep moving.

What this looks like day-to-day:

Morning: Build using AI. Compress 4 hours into 30 minutes.

Afternoon: Teach about consent violations and ethical concerns.

Evening: Advocate for alternative models. Metacreation Lab. Local training. Artist-owned data.

All the same day. Not conflicting. All necessary.

**Student Who Got This: Emily (Educator)**

Her practice:
- Uses AI to build Bee School (right hand—engaging capability)
- Teaches kids about consent and data sovereignty (left hand—maintaining critique)
- Both in same curriculum

Result: Kids learn AI literacy AND ethical thinking. Not one or the other.

Her colleagues said: "We should ban AI in schools. Don't participate."

Emily said: "I'm going to teach kids how to use it responsibly. Because if I don't, who will?"

She's not uncritical. She's engaged critically.

That's the difference.

**The Community Who Got This: BC + AI**

Our structure:
- We USE AI (right hand—practical application)
- We CRITIQUE AI (left hand—Indigenous data sovereignty work, ethics subgroup, consent frameworks)

Both happening simultaneously.

Not: "Let's use AI and ignore the problems."

Not: "Let's resist AI and lose agency."

But: "Let's engage with AI while actively building better models."

**What I Reject: The Clean Narratives**

Pure Boosters: "AI is all good! Adopt uncritically!"

Ignores real harms. Becomes complicit in extraction. No ethical grounding.

These are the people who will look back in 10 years and realize they participated in something harmful. Because they refused to look at the critique.

Pure Doomers: "AI is all bad! Resist completely!"

Loses agency. Cedes governance to true believers. No ability to shape outcomes.

These are the people who will look back in 10 years and realize they had no voice in how things unfolded. Because they opted out of the conversation.

Both feel clean. Both are incomplete.

The messy middle (both hands full): "This is problematic AND I'm engaging anyway because opting out is worse."

Uncomfortable? Yes. True? Also yes.

I'd rather be uncomfortable and effective than comfortable and irrelevant.

## The Governance Question

If all the thoughtful, ethical, critical people opt out—governance gets made by opportunists and true believers.

That's worse.

Real example from early AI ethics boards: Mostly AI optimists. Critics refused to participate. "I won't legitimize something I oppose."

Result: Weak ethics frameworks. No teeth. Why? The people who understood risks best weren't in the room.

My argument: If you see problems others don't, your responsibility is to stay in the room.

Not to validate bad systems. To push for better ones.

Difference:
- Complicity = Uncritical participation
- Critical engagement = Participating while maintaining critique

Emily's colleagues opted out. "We should ban AI from schools."

Emily stayed in. "I'm going to teach kids how to use it responsibly."

Result: Kids in her program learn AI capability AND consent frameworks AND data sovereignty AND ethical considerations.

Because she stayed in the room.

The alternative: If Emily opted out, kids learn AI from YouTube with zero ethical framework.

Which is worse?

The people most uncomfortable with AI are often the people who should be shaping its use.

If they opt out, we lose the ethical depth we need.

I'm not opting out. I'm staying in the room. Both hands full.

## The Practice

This isn't easy. It requires holding tension.

Most people want clean answers. "Is AI good or bad?"

Both. Neither. Depends on how we use it and what systems we build around it.

Can you hold that complexity?

Or do you need a simple narrative?

The students who transform are the ones who can hold both hands full. The critique AND the curiosity. The fear AND the exploration.

The students who stay stuck are the ones who pick a side. Either uncritical adoption (lose ethical grounding) or complete resistance (lose agency).

Both positions are cleaner. Both are incomplete.

The messy middle is where the work happens.

Both hands full. Keep walking.

I'm choosing complexity.

Because if all the ethical, critical, thoughtful people opt out—governance gets made by people who don't see the problems.

That's worse than engaging with critique intact.

So I'm here. Both hands full. Walking forward.
