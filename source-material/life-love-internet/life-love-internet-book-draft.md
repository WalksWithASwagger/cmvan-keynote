- Chapter 1: Childhood in Harrisburg - A World of Possibilities
    
    The first thing you need to know about Harrisburg, South Dakota? It’s flat. Fucking flat. The kind of flat that makes you wonder if the world really is round after all. But in that flatness, in that endless horizon, I found my first taste of possibility.
    
    Born on December 14, 1976, I came into a world still reeling from Watergate, still high on the promise of space exploration.
    
    I was born in my mom’s bed, in the house that my dad built with his own two hands. It was a humble start, but it was also emblematic of the life they were building—a life where self-reliance and faith walked hand in hand. Dad was the one who cut my umbilical cord, wrapping it with a shoelace and tying it off with the kind of pragmatism that defined his approach to everything. They believed that God was the great doctor, and if He wanted you healed, He’d take care of it Himself.
    
    They had me without any medical intervention, just faith and a lot of courage. Somehow, the two of them did it. And from the moment I was born, my mom was there for me, my first and closest friend.
    
    My mom was young, full of life, and full of time. She was my primary caregiver, especially during those first years in South Dakota. We spent our days together—taking long walks, digging in the garden, swinging from trees. She made me a beautiful baby book by hand, full of illustrations, the kind you just don’t see anymore.
    
    My old man—he was a study in contrasts. On the surface, a quiet, conservative agriculture economist and statistician. But beneath that buttoned-up exterior beat the heart of a renaissance man.
    
    Dad was the kind of guy who could crunch numbers by day and ride his motorcycle into the sunset. He built our first family home with his own two hands, a testament to his belief in self-reliance and hard work. While he was deeply rooted in his career, his entrepreneurial spirit led him to tinker with various side ventures, always exploring new possibilities. A man of faith and intellect, Dad was as comfortable diving into biblical scholarship as he was poring over economic reports. He was an athlete too, keeping himself fit and instilling in us the value of physical discipline. Music was another passion—the sound of his guitar or trumpet often filled our home, a melodic counterpoint to the rhythm of rural life.
    
    As the years went on, Dad's thirst for knowledge never waned. He went back to school, earned his master’s degree, all while remaining deeply involved in our family life. He was living proof that you could have depth and breadth, that you could be serious and playful, traditional and forward-thinking all at once. Looking back, I realize how much of my own restless curiosity, my drive to explore and create, was shaped by Dad’s example. He showed me that life wasn’t about fitting into one box, but about constantly pushing your boundaries, always learning, always growing.
    
    Our house was a laboratory of ideas. Dinner conversations weren’t about the weather or the neighbors; they were about market gaps, untapped potential, the next big thing. Dad would come home, eyes blazing with his latest venture. “Kris,” he’d say, “there’s gold in them thar hills. You just gotta know where to dig.”
    
    And dig we did. Not for gold, but for golf balls.
    
    Picture this: 8-year-old me, knee-deep in pond scum, fishing out Titleists and Callaways like they were precious pearls. It wasn’t glamorous, but it was my first taste of the hustle. Buy low, sell high. Find value where others see trash. These weren’t just golf balls; they were my education in economics, in spotting opportunity.
    
    But the real gold rush happened a few years earlier when I decided to turn pest control into profit. At five years old, I was already thinking like an entrepreneur, always on the lookout for the next big thing. First, it was pumpkins and Indian corn, sold from a wagon that creaked down the neighborhood streets. But then, I had a revelation—wasp nests.
    
    Armed with a slingshot, I’d go from house to house, shooting down wasp nests from the eaves of my neighbors’ homes. Once the nests were down, I’d load them up in my wagon and head to the front door, ready to do business. With a neatly drawn price chart in hand, I’d ring the bell, show off their freshly downed wasp nest, and let them know how much they owed me.
    
    I was convinced this was going to make me a rich young kid. The looks on my neighbors’ faces as I stood there, proudly holding up their wasp nests, were priceless. Some were amused, others baffled, but most of them paid up. In that moment, I wasn’t just selling a service—I was selling peace of mind, and I charged accordingly.
    
    That hustle taught me more than any classroom ever could. It wasn’t just about making money; it was about seeing a need, taking initiative, and understanding the value of your work. It was my first taste of creating value from nothing, and the rush of turning an idea into cash was intoxicating.
    
    But Harrisburg, for all its charms, felt like a cage. The internet was still a sci-fi dream, but I could feel the tremors of a coming revolution. My first computer, a hand-me-down Commodore 64, became my portal to other worlds. I’d stay up late, bathed in the blue glow of the screen, typing out BASIC commands like incantations.
    
    **CREATE. RUN. DEBUG.** The poetry of potential.
    
    Music was my other escape. For ten years, I poured my soul into a trumpet. Jazz, classical, didn’t matter. It was all algorithms to me—patterns to be understood, then broken. Little did I know, those late nights in the band room were training my brain for a future where code would be my instrument, and the internet my stage.
    
    Looking back, it’s easy to romanticize those days. To paint Harrisburg as this crucible of entrepreneurial spirit. But let’s get real—it was also suffocating. The same flatness that spoke of possibility also whispered of limitation. For every dream sparked, another was snuffed out by small-town reality.
    
    I loved my folks, still do. But even then, I knew their dreams were too big for Harrisburg. They were planting seeds in concrete, hoping for a forest. Me? I was planning my escape.
    
    **By 1980, when I was four years old, my parents decided it was time for us to leave the cult behind and forge a new path.** Harrisburg wasn’t enough. We packed up our lives and headed west to Sacramento, California—a place that loomed on the horizon like a promised land, where the world felt bigger and the possibilities seemed endless. **We were the first California Krugs, pioneers of our own making.**
    
    When we made the move to California, Mom was still my rock, my constant. Dad was busy with work and his MBA, so it was Mom who took care of us kids. She even worked at the same school I attended, so from 7:00 AM to 6:00 PM, all of us Krug kids—me and my two younger sisters, Karla and Kelsey—were together at Capitol Christian School. Karla was born at home in Harrisburg, just like me, but by the time Kelsey came along, we were in California, and she was born in a real life hospital. Mom later returned to teaching and eventually became a school administrator, all while earning her Master’s in Education. She was hilarious, friendly, curious, and always ready to explore the world with us.
    
    Harrisburg was my launchpad. But the stars? They were calling my name.
    
    Little did I know, the real adventure was just beginning. Canada was waiting, and with it, a digital tsunami that would reshape everything I thought I knew about business, art, and what it means to be human in an increasingly artificial world.
    
    But that’s a story for another chapter. For now, let’s raise a glass to Harrisburg. To flat horizons and big dreams. To the roots that ground us and the visions that propel us forward.
    
    Here’s to the world of possibilities hidden in the most unlikely places.
    
    Here’s to the digital romantic, born in the heart of analog America.

The air crackles with tension as my parents pack our lives into cardboard boxes, whispering about freedom and new beginnings. We're leaving the cult, they say. Paper routes become my first taste of normalcy, the slap of newsprint against driveways a comforting rhythm in a world that still feels off its axis. Sports offer another lifeline. It seemed like harmless fun at the time, a reckless prank.

My mom pulled some strings, and I landed at Victory Christian High School, where my old football coach took me under his wing. For a while, it seemed like things were getting back on track. It was a bold move, but it felt like the right one—except for one thing. I was no longer eligible to play sports due to transfer rules.

In my world, Christian parents kept their kids busy to keep them out of trouble, and I'd been no exception. The play was edgy, queer, and everything that would've sent my church community into a tailspin. But I loved it. Summers weren't just for hanging out; I took advanced college prep courses, including a forensic science lab at Sacramento State University where I knocked out six college credits. Weed wasn't just about getting high; it was about opening my mind and seeing the world from a different perspective. Magic: The Gathering sharpened my strategic thinking—those deeply technical, geeky late-night card games were as intense as any sport I'd played. And snowboarding? That was pure freedom, a way to connect with nature and push myself to new limits on the slopes. These weren't just hobbies—they were ways of life that connected me to new communities and expanded my horizons.
