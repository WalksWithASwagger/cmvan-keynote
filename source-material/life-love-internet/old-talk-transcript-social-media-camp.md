## KRIS'S TALK

Thanks for coming. I get nervous when I speak in public, so these notes are a crutch and I'm going to try not to use them too much.

You may have noticed the talk had a bit of a confusing title — it was called something like "Action Is the Antidote to Despair: Empowerment Through Art and Activism and Technology." But I kind of scratched that last week. So this talk's now called "Life, Love, and the Internet."

When I talk at things like this I usually talk about technical stuff — how to use social media, a campaign I've done, photography. I very rarely talk really much about myself or who I am or where I've come from and why I have this perspective that in some ways stands in opposition to other people. So I hope to share a little bit about my personal story today — hopefully that meets you where you're at and doesn't just sound like me talking about myself too much.

---

## WHERE I CAME FROM

I'm American. I grew up in California. I grew up in a Christian home — my mom was a schoolteacher and my dad was a bureaucrat. Intellectualism, creativity, asking questions — they weren't highly valued in my upbringing. But my dad was a super geek, so we always had technology around. My dad brought home an Apple 2e in the 80s with a 600 baud modem. So from like 1986 I was online — bulletin boards, hacking into other systems. Technology has definitely been a part of my DNA.

I realized this week as I was preparing this talk that I've been online for like 30 years.

I moved to Canada at 17 — in search of powder and mountains, went to university at Trinity Western in 1995. The internet is just taking off. First graphical web browser had just been released. I had a strong technical background. I was able to slide right into the demand: every professor on campus needed someone to build them a website. So I arrived in Canada and from the moment I got here, I hit the ground running with technology. I was building websites for professors. I built the first website for my university. It opened up a lot of doors.

---

## SPARK ONLINE — 1998

In 1998, after I'd been doing this for a couple years — this is ancient times on the internet, 14-15 years ago — me and five buddies started an online magazine called **Spark Online**. The tagline was: **"Exploring electronic consciousness."**

This magazine was formative for me, and maybe even for parts of the internet, in some ways. We were running an online community way before this kind of stuff existed. There were no content management systems available for free like WordPress. There was no idea of blog posts and multiple authors and comments and a discussion thing going on. We duct-taped together hand-crafted HTML pages with a bulletin board system tagged onto the bottom of each post. We had a net art gallery showcasing people experimenting with new digital mediums. We were talking philosophy — what does it mean to have a digital self? How is the internet changing perceptions of friendship and geography?

For me and the guys and girls who ran that project — it got us off on the right foot. We got to know internet people. San Francisco people. New York people. It blew up and grew and really gave us a great foothold.

It ran for three years. It grew pretty big. And it crushed under the weight of its own success — we had to hand-code everything, we were taking old paradigms from the old world like a monthly magazine with sections and articles and applying that to the internet. This was the day when every ecommerce shop on the internet looked like a shopping mall. So we let it go.

But I was fully off to the races at this point.

---

## SILICON VALLEY AND THE DARK TIME

In 1999 I moved to California. Took a job with a Silicon Valley startup. Started ascending the ranks of corporate marketing. I had a young family — I have a daughter who came with me to the awards last night. I was doing what I was told. Graduated. Had a baby. Had a wife. Had a fat corporate job. Ascending the ranks of marketing at a Silicon Valley startup.

I was pretty unhappy. Commuting a couple hours every day. They'd feed you at work so I wouldn't leave my desk. I got over 200 pounds. I was very unhappy. I wasn't in a very good place. I was barely keeping my head above water — just trying to do all the things the world told me I was supposed to do.

And at the moment when I was probably at my weakest, tragedy hit my family.

We had a son. He was helicoptered to Stanford University's Neonatal Intensive Care Unit as soon as he was born. He spent the next three weeks in there. Many times close to dying. Seizure disorders. Surgeries. A host of problems.

So here I am — young dad, young marketing executive, working my ass off, being somewhat successful, barely keeping my head above water, being unsatisfied with the results of my work. And then this.

## THE CAMERA SAVED MY LIFE

The reason I lay all this down for you is to bring you to this: there's a point at which my camera and becoming a photographer literally saved my life.

I was fat, unhappy, depressed, barely holding the family together. Miserable. I didn't really know what to look for in life — my Christian upbringing had said: marriage, family, home, job, house. And it wasn't working for me. I was depressed. Just sitting around waiting to die. I didn't know what I was engaged with. I wasn't engaged.

Then: the company I was working for went through a big corporate merger. We all had to stay at the company but we didn't have real work to do. A lot of free time. And I decided at some point that **it would be cheaper for me to buy a camera and shoot these guys who were going to be around for a few weeks than to hire a photographer**. A very pragmatic decision.

But having that camera in my life started to trigger something.

On the weekends I would take my camera home with me **just as a way to get out of the house**. I was in a kind of contentious situation, as I've been alluding to, and I just needed a break. So I'd take my camera and the sun would be up and I'd grab it and hit the streets of San Francisco. I just wandered around. It gave me something to do that was active but out of the house. And in the process of doing this, I had an awakening. I started connecting with people again. I started having these flashbacks of who I used to be when I was a younger guy, and what I was all about. That spark for life — it lit back up again through getting out on the streets with my camera.

I'd bring those photos back to my creative services department — I was VP of Marketing at the software company. We had a big fat 11×17 Xerox printer. I'd print out these elaborate photo books of all the places I'd been, the people I'd seen. Just for me. Killing time.

People started picking them up. Reacting to them. Pulling me aside. My friends, my employees, my co-workers could see I wasn't very happy. They started saying: "I don't know what this thing is you're doing but this is what you need to do. This is really really awesome. There's something going on here."

I created a Flickr account — this is like 2003. I started sharing my images online. Every day for the last 10 years I have photographed, edited, uploaded and shared photos online. Every day.

And part of it was the response. Here I was, taking photos of my young disabled barely-holding-on-to-life son, and sticking them on the internet. And they were beautiful. And people were being moved by them. In 2003, Flickr bought one of the photos of my son and made it the homepage for Flickr's website — through the whole height of their success, through Stewart and Katarina being on the cover of Time magazine, Yahoo acquiring Flickr. My son.

**This dark, tragic thing that had happened to me was being celebrated.** My camera was waking me back up to life. I was able to start to process some of this stuff — what I was going through with my son — via art and photography and the internet. I started to feel reempowered. Reawakened. Inspired. That love for life lit back up.

---

## REINVENTING THE LIFE

I began a process of re-architecting my life to reconnect with my passions. I promised myself I'd never find myself in a situation again where I was totally disconnected from the fruits of my labor — where I was just a cog in a marketing machine selling widgets to someone I never met.

I moved back to Canada. I started a little open source community web startup with some other guys in Vancouver. We co-founded **Bryght** — building open source websites, getting people online. I was sharing my photos, traveling around.

Every time I started to gravitate toward the area of my passions, I was being met with success. Intrinsic success — feeling good, being proud of my work, feeling happy. And external success — people being touched by it and changed by it.

I realized I was the biggest person holding myself back from being who I wanted to be.

So I quit my job and I decided to become a photographer full-time. This was around 2009. It's a scary thing. But I've never been busier since that day.

## TIPS AND PRINCIPLES

Okay. That's the most "talk about me" part. Now I want to share some tips and a bit about my approach.

Right now on the internet, people all over the world are sharing ideas, feelings, photos, pictures of their love. I believe that what's evolving is a bit of a collective consciousness — a public space of ideas and emotions and discourse. You can take a barometer: there's a website called We Feel Fine that goes into Twitter and looks for the phrase "I feel" or "I felt" and pulls up a barometer of how the whole internet is feeling about any particular topic.

**LISTENING POSTS.** The basics of any internet campaign or project needs to be setting up listening posts. A combination of Google alerts, RSS, and Twitter search — using these intelligent robots to go out there, find the things that matter most to you, and bring them back to you in real time. The first step is to tap into it, listen to it, figure out what's going on.

**AUDIENCE AS PARTICIPANTS.** Many of your customers or fans or patrons of your art want to do more than just buy your product. Through the internet you can encourage your audience to do a whole lot more — they can share your work, promote you. Think about the internet not just as a way to schlep your wares, but as a way to encourage participation, to foster community, and to grow.

**BUILD A POSSE.** I don't think I'm telling a big secret here, but the trick to the internet is to have a posse. There's a posse of people who think like you. When you talk about yourself on the internet all day long you sound really lame. But if you talk about your friends and all of their awesome work, and you push all the love and the traffic to them, you start to build a network. It's not completely reciprocal — you're not "hey if you do this I'll do that" — but you find the people you believe in, whose work you trust, and you start to work with them and promote them and shine the light on them. You'll find by doing so you actually elevate your own game. Rising tide floats all boats.

**PIRACY VS. OBSCURITY.** A lot of times when I talk to art students or young creatives they're afraid of sticking their work online because people might steal it or copy their ideas. I tell those people: **piracy isn't your biggest fear on the internet. It's obscurity. It's relevance.** It's having your work not mean anything. It's not putting it out there. I personally have 60,000 high-res original photographs of major world events — last three Olympics, 10 trips to China, all these TED events — online for free under a Creative Commons license. Photographers say: aren't you afraid that's going to cannibalize your thing? I found the opposite is true. **The more that I give away the more that I get back. It's like coins in a karmic piggy bank.** As long as my deposits are more than my withdrawals, everything's going to be cool.

Also: giving something away on one hand isn't to limit your commercial opportunities on another. My Creative Commons license is an Attribution ShareAlike license — you can use my work for whatever you want without asking me as long as you attribute me and link back to the original source and your work is non-commercial. I retain the right to sell commercially. If your usage falls outside the license I've laid down, then negotiations begin.

I don't worry about people who use my work outside the license. There's only so many hours in the day. I spend my time chasing positive things.

**DOCUMENT EVERYTHING.** If you don't stick it on the internet, it didn't even happen. If it's worth doing it's worth documenting. I've invested a lot of time in tags and titling on Flickr. The result after 10 years is I'm kind of using Flickr and Google as a free online stock photography agency for myself. Every thing you publish is an on-ramp into your world through Google.

**BEFORE, DURING, AND AFTER.** Tell them what you're going to tell them, then tell them, then tell them what you told them. When I come into an event like this: I do things online leading up to it (post that I'm going to be there). While I'm here: I'm tweeting, taking photos, putting up videos in real time. After it's all said and done: I'll write a wrap-up post. By doing so I'm building links, making associations, telling Google. Links are the currency of the internet.

**DON'T BUILD NEW SILOS.** The temptation of the early web days was to build a new silo every time you had a new project. Find where your people hang out online and layer your campaigns on top of what already exists.

**DISTRIBUTED WORK LIFE.** The promise of technology is that we can do what we do from anywhere. I refuse to use it as a digital shackle. Everything I own is available online from any computer. If my computer falls in the ocean I get a new one and I'm back in business right away. This leaves me free to turn on a dime, travel quickly, go to interesting places, be nimble.

**GALIANO ISLAND.** I live part of the year off the grid on a small island in the Salish Sea. My big fear about moving to Galiano Island was that I'd step outside the flow of Vancouver and lose my edge. I found the opposite was true. All the stuff that doesn't matter — the life baggage you accumulate living a fast-paced city business life — faded away. What was left was the stuff I cared about the most. The stuff I had energy for. I had clarity. I knew what I had to do. Splitting my time between rural and connected has been hugely valuable for my business life, my focus, my career.

**DIGITAL FAST.** For the last two years I've been doing a digital fast. Taking time to put down everything with a power button. No camera, no computer, no cell phone. I do it on Cortez Island at Hollyhock. The first year I did 10 days. Last year I did 21 days. I have an email autoresponder: "I'm taking a digital fast. Your email has not been received. It will be deleted when I get home. Please contact me after September 1st."

People respect it. The stuff that doesn't matter fades away. The stuff that does matter comes right back.

If you could take one thing away from my talk: **try to find a little space to do a digital fast this year.** Especially if you're hyper-connected. Our brains aren't wired for this stuff. We're really good at it and we're very adaptable, and it's amazing technology — don't get me wrong, I'm a huge advocate — but set aside a little time for yourself and see what happens.

**REPUTATION.** The internet's based on reputation. The things I say about myself are fine and they're important. But the things that you say about me — that's really what matters. Life is long and the world is small. Remember to be kind. Keep a lid on it.

## CLOSING

We have at our fingertips the most powerful tools for storytelling and communication we've ever had in our whole lives. We can go online right now and in two minutes start a blog that you can publish across the whole world. People can read your ideas. Go forth in creativity and kindness. Find these tools that can make you powerful, make you nimble, allow you to chase after your dreams. You'll find that you'll be met with success beyond your wildest expectations.

Thank you very much for having me here. It's really an honor to be able to share some of my work and my life with you. I'll see you on the internet.
