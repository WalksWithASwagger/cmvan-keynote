# Punk Rock AI — ElevenLabs Audio Script v4
## Personal essay version — biography as argument

*For audio generation. Paragraph breaks = natural pauses. No stage directions, no slide cues, no third-party case studies. This is Kris's story as the creative argument.*

## The Permission Gap

I grew up not knowing that a person could be a creative professional.

I mean that literally. Where I came from — religious conservative, small-town South Dakota, then Sacramento — the people who made art, who took photographs, who designed things, who worked in film — those people were called worldly. That was not a compliment. It meant: not your people. Not your path. Not a real life.

So I tried things and nothing worked. Music — ten years of trumpet. Drawing. Writing. I wasn't bad at any of it exactly. I just had no map. No model. No one who showed me that making things could actually be a life you build.

At twenty, I got to know a photographer for the first time. Not a famous one. Just a person who did that for work, who lived that way. Something cracked open. I remember thinking: these are real jobs. These are real people. You can actually do this.

But I still hadn't found my medium. That took eleven more years. And it didn't happen the way I expected.

## The Camera Origin

It happened in a hospital.

In 2001 I was living in Silicon Valley, working at a startup backed by Accel Partners. September 11th happened. Then Judah was born — my son — with serious health complications. He was rushed to the NICU at Stanford. I was there every day, watching my newborn son hooked up to more wires than a server rack.

I picked up a camera. Not for any artsy reason. The company needed photos and we were too broke to hire a pro.

But something else happened. The camera got me out of the house. It gave me somewhere to put my attention when my attention was otherwise drowning. I'd take the camera home on weekends just to have a reason to leave — to get out of the house, to get a break from a life that was coming apart. I'd walk the streets of San Francisco and I'd look through the viewfinder and for those hours I could hold my grief up to the light instead of being swallowed by it. The camera was a survival device before it was a creative tool. It interrupted the despair.

I posted the photos to Flickr. Raw, unfiltered. One of Judah's NICU photos ended up on Flickr's homepage.

I remember thinking: it's not about the technique. People are responding to the truth in the image.

That was the first time I understood what any of this was actually for. Not for IPOs. Not for product launches. For moments like that one. For connection. For making something real when the world goes fragile.

That camera didn't leave my hands for the next twenty years.

## 145,000 Frames

That became my operating principle. I released a hundred and forty-five thousand photographs on Flickr under Creative Commons.

The critics said I was crazy. Piracy, they said. I told them they were thinking about it wrong: piracy isn't your biggest fear. Obscurity is. Having your work not mean anything. Not putting it out there. Those photos spread across the internet like a benevolent virus, each one carrying my name. The calls came from National Geographic, Rolling Stone, the New York Times. They didn't want the free stuff. They wanted the premium experience. Giving away made me invaluable.

For twenty years that logic was sound. AI broke it. I'll come back to that.

And then I built a daily practice around it. Shoot every day. Archive everything. Review what worked. Understand why.

## Open Source as Values

Around the same time, I moved back to Vancouver and co-founded Bryght with Boris Mann — the world's first Drupal services company — out of an East Van office with second-hand desks and whiteboards covered in code.

We weren't just building websites. We were building a movement. We landed Dead.net for the Grateful Dead. Warner Brothers Records. We built digital homes for musicians who believed in what open content could do. A client once asked me: "So let me get this straight — you want me to pay you for software that's free?" I told him: you know how to drive a car, but if the engine explodes, you don't rebuild it yourself. You pay a mechanic. We were the mechanics. The software was free. The expertise wasn't.

Open source taught me something I've never unlearned: value doesn't live in the locked file. Value lives in the relationship, the expertise, the community, the trust. The more you share, the stronger the network becomes.

I couldn't see it yet, but that insight — value lives in relationship — would become the very principle AI would later turn against me.

## Dada to Punks to DJs to AI

I'd been making this move before I knew it had a name.

In 1998, I launched a community called Spark Online — tagline: exploring electronic consciousness. This was before blogs, before social media, before YouTube. A small circle of people building public lives on the web, running an online community before there was any framework for what that even meant. We were duct-taping together hand-coded HTML and bulletin board systems and talking about what it meant to have a digital self. I didn't know what I was building. I just knew it felt essential.

And then Judah was born. And the practice deepened. I started documenting everything — not as marketing, but as memory. Publish before, during, and after. Not to build an audience. To build infrastructure against forgetting. Every moment you document is a moment you can give back to yourself later.

I didn't know yet that I was doing what every generation of weirdos had always done.

Because this story is not new.

The Dadaists in the 1910s picked up the newspaper — a corporate mass-production technology — and tore it into collage. They made art from the infrastructure of industrial capitalism. The Xerox punks in the 1970s picked up the copy machine — a corporate office tool — and made zines. They used it to self-publish manifestos, artwork, music journalism that no gatekept magazine would touch. The DJs in Jamaica picked up the sound system — corporate audio infrastructure — and invented a whole new art form.

That idea took a boat to the Bronx and became Hip Hop.

Every generation: the corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it's actually for.

AI is the latest chapter. The corporations built it. Now the question is what the weirdos do with it.

## The Deeper Lineage

The lineage goes deeper than Dada.

William S. Burroughs was doing cut-ups in the 1950s. Taking a page of text — newspaper, novel, anything — cutting it into strips and reassembling it. The point wasn't the randomness. The point was what Burroughs said about it: arrangement and selection is the medium. Not the words — the choosing of which words go next to which words.

The Situationists in France were doing something similar — détournement, they called it. Taking the tools and images of corporate spectacle and turning them against the spectacle. Not rejecting the infrastructure — redirecting it. Using the enemy's weapons to say something the enemy would never sanction.

This is older and deeper than any of us tends to remember. Every time we talk about AI as unprecedented — we're forgetting centuries of artists who did exactly what we're doing now. They picked up the corporate tool. They used it wrong. They made it mean something.

We are in that tradition. This is not a departure from creative history. It is creative history, doing what it has always done.

## The Selector

In those Kingston dancehalls, the hero wasn't the musician. It was the Selector. The person who chose the records. Because making the sound — by the 1960s — wasn't the hard part anymore. Knowing which sound the room needed at that exact moment. That was the skill. That was taste.

And here's what I want you to notice about the Selector: the Selector was listening first. Before the first record dropped, the Selector was reading the room. Feeling the energy. Taste isn't just aesthetic preference. It's social intelligence. It emerges from being present in the room before you open your mouth.

Generation is completely free now. AI can write a screenplay, design a brand identity, produce a track in ten seconds. What's scarce — genuinely scarce — is taste. Your specific weird eye. Your refusal to use the same five corporate adjectives. Your instinct for when something is technically fine but somehow wrong.

The Selector is the job. It always was. AI just turned the volume up on it.

## The Feedback Loop

Think about what the feedback loop used to look like for a photographer before any of this.

You'd shoot a roll of film. Develop it — days of waiting. If you were good enough, maybe a magazine published it. Maybe a reader wrote a letter to the editor. That letter went in the mail. Weeks later, if you were lucky, it got published. You'd read it. The loop — from making something to getting real feedback to making the next thing — took months. Sometimes a year. That's why mastery traditionally took fifteen years. Not because people were slower. Because the loop was slow.

Then Flickr happened.

I started posting every single day. Not because I was already good — because I wanted to become good. Every day: comments, favorites, people telling me what landed and what didn't. I'd incorporate it, go back out the next day, run the whole loop again. I think that compression shortcut the traditional mastery path from fifteen years to maybe five. Not for everyone — only for people who were genuinely using the loop, actually listening, willing to throw work away.

And the archiving mattered as much as the posting. Every day I published, I also filed. Before the shot, during the shoot, after. Documenting the process was how I built a memory of my own development that I could actually learn from. Memory infrastructure — the practice of building a record of your making so that future you has something to work with.

What AI does now is compress that further. Dramatically further. You can make a hundred variations of something in an afternoon, critique them, filter them, run taste loops that used to take a season. But here's what nobody tells you: speed is not the point. The point is what you do at each transition. The AI assists within each phase. It must not determine what comes next. That's the human's job. Every time.

---

## The Cutting Room Floor

The loop is only as good as the taste you bring to it. Speed without judgment is just noise at scale. The Flickr practice taught me that before AI ever arrived.

My theory of taste: it's not what you choose to keep. It's what you choose to throw away. The cutting room floor is where your taste actually lives. The internet saw what I kept. But my taste was built by what I refused to upload. The rejects. The almosts. The technically fine but somehow wrong.

Here's a question nobody asks: what did you throw away this week? What's on your cutting room floor? Because that's your taste. Not what you chose to keep — what you refused to keep. The machine didn't teach me what good looks like. Two decades of deciding did.

## The Pattern You Couldn't Name

I've watched something happen to creative people when they first start using AI for their own work — their actual work, not experiments.

A visual artist I know uploaded twenty of her own photographs to an image model and asked it to abstract the underlying patterns across all of them. What came back stopped her cold.

The AI had identified a structural pattern in how she composed images — a specific relationship between negative space and subject position — that she had never consciously articulated. She'd been doing it for fifteen years. It was hers. But she'd never seen it named.

She described it as: seeing a map of a city you've lived in for decades but never seen from above.

What you thought was instinct turns out to be twenty years of choosing, encoded. The AI didn't create that pattern. She created it, one image at a time, over fifteen years of practice. What the AI did was make it legible. Visible. Nameable.

That's not replacement. That's revelation. And it only works if you have twenty years of choosing to reveal.

## The AI Chapter

And then AI arrived. And here's where the story gets complicated in a way I haven't fully resolved.

I need to tell you about the deal I made.

For twenty years, I operated on a covenant with the internet. The deal was this: I share freely. In return, I get attribution. I get community. I get the relationship — the people who find my work, follow the thread, reach back, become collaborators and clients and friends. Open source taught me that the value doesn't live in the locked file. The value lives in the relationship, the expertise, the trust. So I shared. Extensively. Radically. A hundred and forty-five thousand photographs under Creative Commons.

That deal held for twenty years.

Then AI came and broke it.

I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

When I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly eighteen hundred of my images scraped into a major AI training corpus. Nobody asked me. Nobody paid me. Nobody followed the thread back and said: we built this with your work, here's what we gave you in return. They took the file. They did not keep the relationship.

The thing that made me who I am — the openness, the belief that sharing builds more than hoarding — that's the thing that was exploited. I haven't resolved that. I'm living inside it.

## The Three Fears

I want to name the fears directly. Not "AI is scary" — that's not useful. The specific things.

First: consent and theft. Not just my photos — the entire infrastructure of human creative work, scraped without permission, without compensation, without attribution, to build commercial products. I want to say this precisely: I am not anti-remix. Dada was remix. Hip Hop was remix. The open internet I helped build was remix. Remix has culture. Extraction has appetite. What AI did was not remix. It was extraction — taking without the relationship, without the credit, without the community that makes the sharing mean something.

Second: the junior pipeline. How does a young photographer learn if there's no market for the work that teaches you? How does a junior designer build a practice if the entry-level work gets automated away? The pipeline of creative skill-building depends on there being rungs on the ladder. What happens if the bottom rungs disappear?

Third: the race to the bottom. AI-generated content competing with human creative work at the low end, driving down rates, normalizing mediocrity-at-scale. Good enough for the client who doesn't care. Which is a lot of clients.

These are real. I don't want to paper over them.

---

## The Junior Pipeline

On the second fear — the pipeline — I want to be honest in a way that's uncomfortable.

I don't have a clean answer.

I know what the argument for concern is: the entry-level work has historically been where young creatives build the skills that eventually become the senior work. You learn composition by shooting thousands of product photos you don't care about. You learn to write by cranking copy that pays the rent. If AI displaces the entry-level work, where does the next generation get the practice hours?

I also know what the counterargument is: every wave of automation created new kinds of entry-level work. Desktop publishing killed typesetting and created graphic design. The internet killed the travel agency and created the UX industry. Maybe something new grows in the space that AI opens.

I genuinely don't know which of those is right. I suspect it's both, depending on the field and the decade.

What I do know: if we don't acknowledge the problem clearly, we won't build the systems to address it. The junior pipeline isn't a talking point. It's a generation of creative people trying to figure out if there's a rung on the ladder for them. We owe them honesty, not reassurance.

## Name What You See

We've said the word bias so many times it's lost all meaning. Stop saying bias. Name what you're seeing.

But first — before you can name it — you have to slow down enough to notice. The first job is not prompting. The first job is noticing. Looking at the output and actually seeing what's there. Who's present. Who's absent. What posture, what skin tone, what assumed language, what world the model is taking for granted.

When you type CEO into an image generator and get twenty white men in power poses — that's not bias. That's racism and misogyny encoded into mathematics. When a model mimics an artist's exact style without credit or compensation — name the theft. Don't euphemize it into a technical vocabulary that makes it easier to ignore.

AI doesn't know what's fair. It only knows what's frequent on the internet. The mirror reflects. It doesn't correct. Correcting the mirror is your job.

## Frequent Is Not Fair

Three questions to carry into every AI interaction: Who is missing from this output? What assumptions did the model make? What would change if the training data weren't dominated by English-language internet?

The model doesn't know what's missing. It only knows what was there when they scraped it. You are the only one in this conversation who knows what's absent. You are the only one who knows what your community needs that doesn't yet exist in the training data.

That gap is not a flaw to fix. It's a creative assignment.

## The Punk Condition

And here's what I need you to sit with at the same time: I am more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I have ever been in my entire life.

Both of those statements are true. At the exact same time.

That's not a contradiction. That's complexity. That is what I'm calling the punk condition — the same situation the Dadaists were in with the newspaper, the same situation the Xerox punks were in with the copy machine, the same situation the Jamaican selectors were in with the sound system. The corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it's actually for.

I hold the critique in one hand. I hold the capability in the other. Both hands full. That's the only honest place to stand.

And if you're paralyzed — if the weight of the critique is making it hard to move — I want to offer something I've carried since the dark years with Judah in the NICU: action is the antidote to despair. Not solutions. Not answers. Not the certainty that it'll work out. Action. Make something small. Make it today. The making itself is the act of resistance.

## The Analog Oasis

This is also why I need Galiano Island.

I live part of the year off the grid on a small island in the Salish Sea — solar panels, rainwater collection, growing food. I co-founded IMERSS — the Institute of Multidisciplinary Ecological Research in the Salish Sea — because I needed to be in relationship with something that doesn't update, doesn't version, doesn't get deprecated.

I've done ten-day digital fasts. I've done twenty-one-day digital fasts. No camera, no computer, no cell phone. I send an autoresponder: your email has not been received, it will be deleted when I get home. And every time, the same thing happens — the stuff that doesn't matter fades away, and what comes back is the signal. I don't want my nervous system colonized by technology. The analog oasis isn't a retreat from the work. It's where I remember what the work is for.

I'm not romanticizing rural life. I'm not anti-technology. I'm saying: if your whole existence is inside the feedback loop, you lose your ability to hear the signal inside the noise. Standing in the Salish Sea at low tide reminds me what real feedback looks like — the feedback that took thousands of years of trial and consequence to encode.

The punk who tears down walls also knows when to be quiet. The Selector also knows when not to play a record.

## Who Writes the Rules?

Here's the question that keeps me in this, even when it's uncomfortable.

If every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person opts out of using AI because it is messy — who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used? Who builds the guardrails?

The tech bros. The people who don't see the problems. The people who think the consent issues aren't real, that the pipeline doesn't matter, that cultural homogenization is fine as long as it's efficient.

Vancouver is the home of Adbusters and Greenpeace. We have been turning corporate infrastructure into counterculture since before most tech cities had a scene. That's the tradition you're in. Not opting out — using it wrong. Using it to tell stories that matter.

And you don't have to do this alone. Don't build community — that word has lost its teeth. Build a posse. A small circle of people who share your taste, your ethics, your trust. People who will tell you when the work is bad and lift it when it's good. The Selector didn't work alone. The dancehall was full. The weirdos always traveled in packs.

## True North

I've lived this principle more than once.

At the 2010 Vancouver Olympics, we set up what we called True North Media House — a guerrilla newsroom out of a converted warehouse, no official press credentials, just bloggers and veteran photojournalists and smartphones and the instinct to tell the stories that weren't being told. We didn't have permission. We had cameras, feeds, tags, links, and each other. While the mainstream media was bogged down in bureaucracy, we were nimble. We were making our own press passes. Bloggers rubbing shoulders with veteran photojournalists, coders with activists, figuring out what citizen journalism could actually be.

It wasn't perfect. It was scrappy. It was exactly the same move I'd made with every other tool: pick it up, use it wrong, share everything, build community around it.

Nimble is not the same as naive. We knew the problems with the Olympics. We used the platform anyway, on our terms.

## The Same Creative Impulse

Every time I run an AI meetup or workshop in Vancouver, we start with an indigenous welcome. The person who's given that welcome most often is Anthony Joseph — Isiléyu — from the Coast Salish territory.

He told a story once that I think about more than almost anything else in this conversation.

He said: my ancestors didn't have chainsaws. But they would have used them if they'd been there.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

The creative impulse is continuous. The tools change. The people who pick them up and make something with them — they're in the same lineage, doing the same fundamental thing. Every generation figures out what the new tool is actually for. Not what the manufacturer says it's for. What the maker, the carver, the selector, the photographer, the weirdos figure out it's for.

The tool is never neutral. But neither are we.

## The Best Tool

There's a book I wrote in 2010 called Killer Photos with Your iPhone.

The premise was simple: the best camera is the one you have with you. Not the most expensive camera. Not the camera with the best specs. The one that's in your pocket when the moment happens. That camera, used with skill and intent and a good eye, will make better images than a camera that stays home because it's too heavy.

That same logic applies now.

Everyone thinks they need to wait for the perfect AI tool — the one that's more powerful, more reliable, more ethically sourced, more aligned with their values. And yes, push for better tools. Yes, engage with the ethics. But don't wait. The best AI tool is the one you'll actually use. The one you understand well enough to run the loop with. The one you can bring to the moment.

A sophisticated tool in the hands of someone who hasn't developed taste is still just noise at scale. A modest tool in the hands of someone who has — that's a different story.

## The Three Documents

Which is why the most practical punk move I know is this: write three documents.

One — a personal AI policy. What will you use these tools for? What will you refuse? It takes three minutes. Write it down.

Two — a style guide. What are the ten words you absolutely refuse to say? What does your work sound like when it's actually you? What's your sentence structure? Your rhythm? Your refusals? Ban the word delve. Four minutes.

Three — a worldview document. What do you believe about people, about craft, about justice? Are you a feminist? An anarchist? A punk? Five sentences. Three minutes.

Feed those three documents to whatever AI you use. Do that, and the machine stops giving you the average of the internet. It starts amplifying what makes you you. If your values aren't in text, to AI they basically don't exist.

And a fourth thing, that I think matters as much as the documents: before you broadcast, listen. Set up listening posts. Read the communities you want to serve. What is this room asking for? What grief is under the surface? What possibility is trying to be born? The Selector doesn't just know the records. The Selector knows the room.

## Write for the Bot

For a designer in Vancouver, for an Indigenous artist, for anyone whose perspective isn't already over-represented in the training data — writing those three documents is not narcissism. It's not personal branding. It's anti-erasure infrastructure.

The model learns from what exists. If your voice doesn't exist in text, it doesn't exist in the model. The default isn't neutral — it's the aggregate of whoever showed up and wrote things down. For generations, that wasn't everyone. It still isn't.

Writing for the bot is how you make the bot work for you instead of against you. It's the Selector move. It's the punk move. It's how you get your weird specific frequency into the system instead of letting the system smooth you into the average.

And every time you document your process — the making, the choosing, the rejecting, the reasoning behind the decisions — you leave a trace that proves a human was there. In the AI age, process is provenance.

## The Stubborn Human Soul

I want to tell you something I believe, and I want to be careful about how I say it.

There is something in creative work that doesn't compress. That doesn't tokenize. That twenty years of Flickr practice built in me that I cannot fully articulate, cannot fully hand to a machine, cannot fully explain even to myself. It lives in the decisions I make before I know I'm making them. It lives in the things I refuse without being able to say exactly why. It lives in the cutting room floor.

The machine can do extraordinary things. It is getting better faster than most people realize. But this stubborn specific thing — the residue of embodied experience, the taste built from consequence, the judgment that comes from having actually cared about something for a long time — that persists.

Not because it's magic. Because it was earned. And earning doesn't compress.

The AI era doesn't make that less valuable. It makes it more valuable. Because now everything else is cheap.

## Release Day

Every chapter of my life has been the same move: pick up the tool, use it wrong, share everything, build a posse.

That's what I'm asking you to do.

The global Release Day is May twenty-ninth. Four weeks. I want you to take the month of May — externalize your creative taste in those three documents, feed it to the machine, and build something that only you could build. A zine. A short film. A photo series. A manifesto. A webapp. The album you've been sitting on for years. I don't care what it is.

It doesn't need to be perfect.

Punk never was.

## Close

I've carried a line since I was fifteen. My youth pastor in Sacramento used to say it: any dead fish can float downstream. But it takes a live fish to swim against the current.

That's what this is. The current is fast right now. The corporations are moving. The hype is deafening. The consent violations are real and ongoing. The junior pipeline questions are real and unresolved. And in the middle of all of that — if you're thoughtful, if you're critical, if you actually give a damn — you have more creative power available to you right now than any generation before you.

Pick it up. Use it wrong. Share what you learn. Build a posse.

My friend Kevin says it best: let's use these tools to f*** it up, y'all.

You coming?
