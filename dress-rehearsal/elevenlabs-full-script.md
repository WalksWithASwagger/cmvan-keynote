# Punk Rock AI — ElevenLabs Audio Script v2
## Personal essay version — biography as argument

## The Permission Gap

I grew up not knowing that a person could be a creative professional.

I mean that literally. Where I came from — religious conservative, small-town South Dakota, then Sacramento — the people who made art, who took photographs, who designed things, who worked in film — those people were called worldly. That was not a compliment. It meant: not your people. Not your path. Not a real life.

So I tried things and nothing worked. Music — ten years of trumpet. Drawing. Writing. I wasn't bad at any of it exactly. I just had no map. No model. No one who showed me that making things could actually be a life you build.

At twenty, I got to know a photographer for the first time. Not a famous one. Just a person who did that for work, who lived that way. Something cracked open. I remember thinking: these are real jobs. These are real people. You can actually do this.

But I still hadn't found my medium. That took eleven more years. And it didn't happen the way I expected.

## The Camera Origin

It happened in a hospital.

In 2001 I was living in Silicon Valley, working at a startup backed by Accel Partners. September 11th happened. Then Judah was born — my son — with serious health complications. He was rushed to the NICU at Stanford. I was there every day, watching my newborn son hooked up to more wires than a server rack.

I picked up a camera. Not for any artsy reason. The company needed photos and we were too broke to hire a pro.

I started taking it everywhere — to the hospital, to the streets of San Francisco, trying to make sense of a world that felt like it was coming apart. I posted the photos to Flickr. Raw, unfiltered. One of Judah's NICU photos ended up on Flickr's homepage.

I remember thinking: it's not about the technique. People are responding to the truth in the image.

That was the first time I understood what any of this was actually for. Not for IPOs. Not for product launches. For moments like that one. For connection. For making something real when the world goes fragile.

That camera didn't leave my hands for the next twenty years.

## 145,000 Frames

I moved back to Vancouver and co-founded Bryght with Boris Mann — the world's first Drupal services company — out of an East Van office with second-hand desks and whiteboards covered in code. We built Dead.net for the Grateful Dead. Open-source wasn't just about free software to us. It was about refusing to build walls. The more you give away, the more valuable you become.

That became my operating principle with the camera too. I released a hundred and forty-five thousand photographs on Flickr under Creative Commons. People told me I was devaluing my work. I told them they were thinking about it wrong. Those photos spread across the internet like a benevolent virus, each one carrying my name. The calls came from National Geographic, Rolling Stone, the New York Times. They didn't want the free stuff. They wanted the premium experience. Giving away made me invaluable.

And then I built a daily practice around it. Shoot every day. Archive everything. Review what worked. Understand why.

## Dada to Punks to DJs to AI

Before I get to what happened next — I need to give you a framework. Because this story is not new.

The Dadaists in the 1910s picked up the newspaper — a corporate mass-production technology — and tore it into collage. They made art from the infrastructure of industrial capitalism. The Xerox punks in the 1970s picked up the copy machine — a corporate office tool — and made zines. They used it to self-publish manifestos, artwork, music journalism that no gatekept magazine would touch. The DJs in Jamaica picked up the sound system — corporate audio infrastructure — and invented a whole new art form. The Selector. The Riddim. The practice of choosing what the room needed, which record, which moment, which frequency.

That idea took a boat to the Bronx and became Hip Hop.

Every generation: the corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it's actually for.

AI is the latest chapter. The corporations built it. Now the question is what the weirdos do with it.

## The Selector

In those Kingston dancehalls, the hero wasn't the musician. It was the Selector. The person who chose the records. Because making the sound — by the 1960s — wasn't the hard part anymore. Knowing which sound the room needed at that exact moment. That was the skill. That was taste.

Generation is completely free now. AI can write a screenplay, design a brand identity, produce a track in ten seconds. What's scarce — genuinely scarce — is taste. Your specific weird eye. Your refusal to use the same five corporate adjectives. Your instinct for when something is technically fine but somehow wrong.

The Selector is the job. It always was. AI just turned the volume up on it.

## The Feedback Loop

Think about what the feedback loop used to look like for a photographer before any of this.

You'd shoot a roll of film. You'd develop it — days of waiting. If you were good enough, maybe a magazine published it. Maybe a reader wrote a letter to the editor. That letter went in the mail. Weeks later, if you were lucky, it got published. You'd read it. The loop — from making something to getting real feedback to making the next thing — took months. Sometimes a year. That's why mastery traditionally took fifteen years. Not because people were slower. Because the loop was slow.

Then Flickr happened.

I started posting every single day. Not because I was already good — because I wanted to become good. Every day: comments, favorites, people telling me what landed and what didn't. I'd incorporate it, go back out the next day, run the whole loop again. I think that compression shortcut the traditional mastery path from fifteen years to maybe five. Not for everyone — only for people who were genuinely using the loop, actually listening, willing to throw work away when it wasn't right.

That's what people miss about that era. It wasn't the audience. It was the loop speed.

What AI does now is compress that further. Dramatically further. You can make a hundred variations of something in an afternoon, critique them, filter them, run taste loops that used to take a season. The question is never whether the tools are fast. The question is whether you've developed the judgment to run the loop well. Whether you know which ninety-seven to throw away.

## The Cutting Room Floor

Because the loop is only as good as the taste you bring to it. Speed without judgment is just noise at scale. The Flickr practice taught me that before AI ever arrived. The machine didn't teach me what good looks like. Twenty years of choosing did.

And that became my theory of taste: it's not what you choose to keep. It's what you choose to throw away. The cutting room floor is where your taste actually lives.

Here's a question nobody asks: what did you throw away this week? What's on your cutting room floor? Because that's your taste. Not what you chose to keep — what you refused to keep. The machine didn't teach me what good looks like. Two decades of deciding did.

## The AI Chapter

And then AI arrived. And here's where the story gets complicated in a way I haven't fully resolved.

I find my relationship with AI completely non-consensual.

Those hundred and forty-five thousand Flickr photos I released freely — that was the deal I made. Community mattered more to me than locking every file in a vault. And when I checked a dataset search tool, I found roughly eighteen hundred of my images scraped into a major AI training corpus. Nobody asked me. Nobody paid me. They just took it.

The thing that made me who I am — the openness, the belief that sharing builds more than hoarding — that's the thing that was exploited. I haven't resolved that. I'm living inside it.

---

## The Three Fears

And I want to name the fears directly. Not "AI is scary" — that's not useful. The specific things.

First: consent and theft. Not just my photos — the entire infrastructure of human creative work, scraped without permission, without compensation, without attribution, to build commercial products. That's not abstract. That's theft.

Second: the junior pipeline. How does a young photographer learn if there's no market for the work that teaches you? How does a junior designer build a practice if the entry-level work gets automated away? The pipeline of creative skill-building — generations of it — depends on there being rungs on the ladder. What happens if the bottom rungs disappear?

Third: the race to the bottom. AI-generated content competing with human creative work at the low end, driving down rates, normalizing mediocrity-at-scale. Good enough for the client who doesn't care. Which is a lot of clients.

These are real. I don't want to paper over them.

---

## Name What You See

We've said the word bias so many times it's lost all meaning. Stop saying bias. Name what you're seeing.

When you type CEO into an image generator and get twenty white men in power poses — that's not bias. That's racism and misogyny encoded into mathematics. When a model mimics an artist's exact style without credit or compensation — name the theft. Don't euphemize it into a technical vocabulary that makes it easier to ignore.

AI doesn't know what's fair. It only knows what's frequent on the internet. The mirror reflects. It doesn't correct. Correcting the mirror is your job.

Three questions to carry into every AI interaction: Who is missing from this output? What assumptions did the model make? What would change if the training data weren't dominated by English-language internet?

## The Punk Condition

And here's what I need you to sit with at the same time: I am more creative, more productive, and more powerful than I have ever been in my entire life.

Both of those statements are true. At the exact same time.

That's not a contradiction. That's complexity. That is what I'm calling the punk condition — the same situation the Dadaists were in with the newspaper, the same situation the Xerox punks were in with the copy machine, the same situation the Jamaican selectors were in with the sound system. The corporations build the infrastructure. The weirdos figure out what it's actually for.

I hold the critique in one hand. I hold the capability in the other. Both hands full. That's the only honest place to stand.

## Who Writes the Rules?

Here's the question that keeps me in this, even when it's uncomfortable.

If every thoughtful, ethical, critical creative person opts out of using AI because it is messy — who writes the rules? Who shapes how this technology gets used? Who builds the guardrails? Who trains the next generation of models?

The tech bros. The people who don't see the problems. The people who think the consent issues aren't real, that the pipeline doesn't matter, that cultural homogenization is fine as long as it's efficient.

Vancouver is the home of Adbusters and Greenpeace. We have been turning corporate infrastructure into counterculture since before most tech cities had a scene. That's the tradition you're in. Not opting out — using it wrong. Using it to tell stories that matter. Using it to get your weird specific voice into the training data instead of leaving that field to the defaults.

## The Three Documents

Which is why the most practical punk move I know is this: write three documents.

One — a personal AI policy. What will you use these tools for? What will you refuse? It takes three minutes. Write it down.

Two — a style guide. What are the ten words you absolutely refuse to say? What does your work sound like when it's actually you? What's your sentence structure? Your rhythm? Your refusals? Ban the word delve. Four minutes.

Three — a worldview document. What do you believe about people, about craft, about justice? Are you a feminist? An anarchist? A punk? A Buddhist? Five sentences. Three minutes.

Feed those three documents to whatever AI you use. Do that, and the machine stops giving you the average of the internet. It starts amplifying what makes you you.

As someone far smarter than me put it: if your values aren't in text, to AI they basically don't exist. And that is dangerously close to won't exist.

---

## Write for the Bot

For a designer in Vancouver, for an Indigenous artist, for anyone whose perspective isn't already over-represented in the training data — writing those three documents is not narcissism. It's cultural activism.

The model learns from what exists. If your voice doesn't exist in text, it doesn't exist in the model. The default isn't neutral — it's the aggregate of whoever showed up and wrote things down. For generations, that wasn't everyone. It still isn't.

Writing for the bot is how you make the bot work for you instead of against you. It's the Selector move. It's the punk move. It's how you get your weird specific frequency into the system instead of letting the system smooth you into the average.

## The Stubborn Human Soul

I want to tell you something I believe, and I want to be careful about how I say it.

There is something in creative work that doesn't compress. That doesn't tokenize. That twenty years of Flickr practice built in me that I cannot fully articulate, cannot fully hand to a machine, cannot fully explain even to myself. It lives in the decisions I make before I know I'm making them. It lives in the things I refuse without being able to say exactly why. It lives in the cutting room floor.

The machine can do extraordinary things. It is getting better faster than most people realize. But this stubborn specific thing — the residue of embodied experience, the taste built from consequence, the judgment that comes from having actually cared about something for a long time — that persists.

Not because it's magic. Because it was earned. And earning doesn't compress.

The AI era doesn't make that less valuable. It makes it more valuable. Because now everything else is cheap.

## Release Day

I covered the Olympics with a guerrilla newsroom — no press passes, just bloggers and smartphones and the instinct to tell the stories that weren't being told. I taught photography at Emily Carr and VFS. I moved to Galiano Island, off the grid, solar power and rainwater, because I needed the analog oasis as a counterweight to the digital noise. I co-founded a research institute in partnership with the Coast Salish people to study the Salish Sea. Every chapter was the same move: pick up the tool, use it wrong, share everything, build community.

That's what I'm asking you to do.

The global Release Day is May twenty-ninth. Four weeks. I want you to take the month of May — externalize your creative taste in those three documents, feed it to the machine, and build something that only you could build. A zine. A short film. A photo series. A manifesto. A webapp. The album you've been sitting on for years. I don't care what it is.

It doesn't need to be perfect.

Punk never was.

## Close

I've carried a line since I was fifteen. My youth pastor in Sacramento used to say it: any dead fish can float downstream. But it takes a live fish to swim against the current.

That's what this is. The current is fast right now. The corporations are moving. The hype is deafening. The consent violations are real and ongoing. The junior pipeline questions are real and unresolved. And in the middle of all of that — if you're thoughtful, if you're critical, if you actually give a damn — you have more creative power available to you right now than any generation before you.

Pick it up. Use it wrong. Share what you learn. Build community around it.

My friend Kevin Friel says it best: "Let's use these tools to f*** it up, y'all."

You coming?
